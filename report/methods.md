# 3 Methods

This section describes the forward and inverse PINN problem setups
(Sec. 3.1–3.3), the suite of QNM extraction algorithms applied to the
resulting waveforms (Sec. 3.4), the new plateau-based fit-window selection
that is the principal methodological contribution of this work (Sec. 3.5),
and the convention used to report a single (ω, τ) pair per experiment
(Sec. 3.6).

## 3.1 Forward problem: Schwarzschild Zerilli ℓ = 2

We work with the even-parity Zerilli master equation for axial perturbations
of a Schwarzschild background of mass $M$. In tortoise coordinates
$x = r + 2M\ln(r/2M - 1)$ the equation reads
$$
\partial_t^2 \Phi - \partial_x^2 \Phi + V_\ell(r(x))\,\Phi = 0,
$$
with the Zerilli potential $V_\ell$ for multipole $\ell$. We fix
$M = 1$ throughout and study the lowest non-trivial multipole $\ell = 2$.

The numerical domain is
$x \in [-50,\,150]\,M$, $t \in [0,\,50]\,M$. The initial data are an
outgoing Gaussian pulse,
$$
\Phi(x,0) = A\,\exp\!\left[-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right],
\qquad
\partial_t \Phi(x,0) = -\partial_x \Phi(x,0),
$$
with $A = 1$, $x_0 = 4\,M$, $\sigma = 5\,M$. Boundary conditions are
absorbing at both $x_{\min}$ and $x_{\max}$, implemented as Dirichlet
conditions $\Phi = 0$ on the spatial boundary.

The reference solution is obtained by a fourth-order Runge–Kutta
method-of-lines finite-difference (FD) integration of the same problem
with $\Delta x = 0.2\,M$, $\Delta t = 0.1\,M$. This FD waveform is used
throughout as the ground truth for benchmarking the PINN field accuracy
and for cross-checking the QNM extraction methods on a non-PINN signal.

The reference theoretical Schwarzschild ℓ = 2 QNM values, used for
percentage-error reporting, are taken from Berti–Cardoso–Will (BCW)
continued-fraction calculations:
$M\omega_0 = 0.3737$, $\tau_0/M = 11.241$ (fundamental, $n=0$);
$M\omega_1 = 0.3467$, $\tau_1/M = 3.674$ (first overtone, $n=1$).

## 3.2 Forward PINN

We solve the forward problem with a fully-connected PINN implemented in
DeepXDE (PyTorch backend). The network has hidden layers $[80, 40, 20, 10]$
with $\tanh$ activations and a single scalar output $\Phi(x, t)$.
Inputs are normalised to the unit interval; the output is wrapped in a
$\tanh$ envelope (`tanh_bound`) to keep activations within the dynamic
range of the IC.

The training loss is the standard PINN composite,
$$
\mathcal{L}_\text{fwd} =
\lambda_r \mathcal{L}_\text{PDE} +
\lambda_i \mathcal{L}_\text{IC} +
\lambda_b \mathcal{L}_\text{BC},
$$
with $\mathcal{L}_\text{PDE}$ the mean-squared residual of the Zerilli
equation evaluated at $N_r = 32{,}000$ collocation points,
$\mathcal{L}_\text{IC}$ the mean-squared mismatch with the prescribed
initial $\Phi$ and $\partial_t \Phi$ at $N_i = 800$ points, and
$\mathcal{L}_\text{BC}$ the boundary-condition residual at $N_b = 400$
points. Loss weights are $\lambda_r = \lambda_i = \lambda_b = 100$.

Optimisation proceeds in two stages: 10{,}000 iterations of Adam with
learning rate $10^{-3}$, followed by 30{,}000 iterations of L-BFGS. We
use a greedy adaptive sampling scheme [Wu 2022] that, every 1000 Adam
iterations, draws $10^5$ candidate residual points, evaluates the PDE
residual on a batch of 5000, and replaces the lowest-residual fraction
of the existing collocation set with the highest-residual 30 % of the
candidates. This concentrates samples near the wavefront and the
late-time fast oscillations where the residual is largest.

Throughout, all computations use double precision (`float64`), which we
found necessary to avoid floating-point-induced stalls in L-BFGS for
this problem.

## 3.3 Inverse problem

The inverse problem treats the black hole mass $M$ and the fundamental
QNM template parameters $(\omega, \tau, A, \phi)$ as learnable scalar
variables, jointly with the field $\Phi$. The training data are noisy
samples of the true Zerilli waveform, generated by adding Gaussian
noise to the FD reference,
$$
\Phi_\text{obs}(x_i, t_i) = \Phi_\text{FD}(x_i, t_i) +
\eta_i,\quad
\eta_i \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\,\sigma_\text{obs}^2\right),\quad
\sigma_\text{obs} = 0.01 \times \max_{x,t} |\Phi_\text{FD}(x,t)|,
$$
at $N_\text{obs} = 500$ points $(x_i, t_i)$ drawn uniformly from the
spatial domain and the post-burst time interval. The
fixed observer position $x_q = 10\,M$ is used both for the ringdown
template constraint described below and for all post-hoc QNM extraction.

The composite inverse loss has three terms:
$$
\mathcal{L}_\text{inv} =
\lambda_r \mathcal{L}_\text{PDE}(M) +
\lambda_d \mathcal{L}_\text{data} +
\lambda_\text{ring} \mathcal{L}_\text{ring}(\omega, \tau, A, \phi).
$$
The PDE residual now contains the learnable $M$ through the Zerilli
potential. The data term is the mean-squared mismatch between the
network output and the observations,
$\mathcal{L}_\text{data} = N_\text{obs}^{-1}
\sum_i \big(\Phi_\theta(x_i, t_i) - \Phi_\text{obs}(x_i, t_i)\big)^2$.
The ringdown term enforces a damped-sinusoid template at the observer,
$$
\mathcal{L}_\text{ring} = \frac{1}{N_\text{ring}} \sum_{j=1}^{N_\text{ring}}
\Big(\Phi_\theta(x_q, t_j) - A e^{-t_j/\tau} \cos(\omega t_j + \phi)\Big)^2,
$$
with the times $t_j$ taken from a uniform grid on
$[t_\text{ring}^\text{min},\,t_\text{max}]$. The network architecture and
the (Adam, L-BFGS) optimiser schedule are the same as for the forward
problem; here we use 10{,}000 Adam iterations followed by 15{,}000 L-BFGS
iterations.

We initialise $M_\text{init} = 0.8$ (20 % below truth),
$\omega_\text{init} = 0.30$ (≈ 20 % below the BCW value 0.3737), and
$\tau_\text{init} = 8.0$ (≈ 29 % below the BCW value 11.241). Honest
parameter offsets are essential: an initialisation already close to
truth would not test that the inverse pipeline can actually move the
parameters in the right direction.

### 3.3.1 Variants studied

The hyperparameters $t_\text{ring}^\text{min}$, $\lambda_\text{ring}$
and $N_\text{ring}$ control the strength and temporal placement of the
ringdown constraint. We study four configurations
(Table 3.1) chosen to isolate the dominant error sources in
the inverse pipeline:

| Variant | $t_\text{ring}^\text{min}$ | $\lambda_\text{ring}$ | $N_\text{ring}$ | Template |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| baseline (`inverse_qnm`) | 10 | 1   | 200  | single mode |
| **A** (`tring18`)        | 18 | 1   | 200  | single mode |
| **D** (`combo`)          | 18 | 100 | 1000 | single mode |
| E (`2mode`)              | 18 | 100 | 1000 | two modes (fundamental + first overtone) |

The baseline reflects the pre-analysis default (template active across
the full post-burst window with weak weighting). Variant A shifts
$t_\text{ring}^\text{min}$ to a value motivated by the plateau analysis
of Sec. 3.5, on the hypothesis that template
contamination from the rapidly-decaying overtone biases the recovered
fundamental when the window starts too early. Variant D combines that
choice with a hundred-fold increase in the template weight and a
five-fold increase in the number of template evaluation points,
testing whether a more strongly enforced and densely sampled template
improves the joint fit. Variant E adds the first overtone to the
template as a second damped sinusoid with its own learnable
$(\omega_1, \tau_1, A_1, \phi_1)$, initialised to
$\omega_1 = 0.28$, $\tau_1 = 4.7$ (intentional offsets from the BCW
overtone values 0.3467 and 3.674). The motivation, outcomes and
analysis of variants A, D and E are given in Sec. 4.

## 3.4 QNM extraction methods

Once a forward or inverse PINN has been trained, the QNM parameters
$(\omega, \tau)$ are extracted post-hoc from the time series
$\Phi(x_q, t)$ at the fixed observer $x_q = 10\,M$. We apply five
extraction methods, the first three of which mirror standard
numerical-relativity practice and the last two of which are the
plateau-based methods that constitute our methodological contribution.

**Method 1 (M1) — FFT + log-line through local maxima.**
The frequency $\omega$ is estimated from the location of the largest
peak of a zero-padded, Hann-windowed FFT of $\Phi(x_q, t)$ over the
fit window, with parabolic interpolation around the peak bin. The
damping time $\tau$ is estimated from a least-squares straight-line
fit through the $\log|\Phi|$ values at the local maxima of $\Phi$ in
the same window. This is the procedure of Patel et al. 2024.

**Method 2 (M2) — direct nonlinear least squares.**
A single damped sinusoid $A e^{-t/\tau}\cos(\omega t + \phi)$ is fitted
to $\Phi(x_q, t)$ over the chosen window using `scipy.optimize.curve_fit`
with bound constraints. M2 is a four-parameter fit and is the most
sensitive to overtone contamination at the early edge of the window.

**Method 3 (M3) — ESPRIT subspace decomposition.**
We apply the ESPRIT algorithm with model order $K = 4$ to recover up
to four (complex) damped sinusoids simultaneously. The mode with the
largest amplitude is reported as the fundamental. M3 results are given
in Appendix B; they are consistent with M1/M2/M4 to leading order but
are noisier on the inverse-PINN waveforms because ESPRIT is sensitive
to the conditioning of the Hankel matrix at low SNR.

**Method 4 (M4) — two-mode NLS with start-time plateau scan.**
A two-mode template
$$
\Phi_\text{model}(t) =
A_0 e^{-t/\tau_0}\cos(\omega_0 t + \phi_0) +
A_1 e^{-t/\tau_1}\cos(\omega_1 t + \phi_1)
$$
is fitted by `curve_fit` over a window
$[t_0,\,t_\text{max}]$, with the mode of longer $\tau$ identified as the
fundamental. The fit is repeated for $t_0$ on a uniform grid of 16
values in $[10,\,25]\,M$. We then identify the contiguous block of
$\lfloor 0.5 \cdot 16 \rfloor = 8$ consecutive start times of lowest
combined relative scatter in $(\omega_0, \tau_0)$ and report the
plateau mean and plateau standard deviation as the M4 estimate of
$(\omega, \tau)$ and its window-systematic uncertainty
$(\sigma_\omega, \sigma_\tau)$.

**Method 5 (M5) — two-mode NLS with two-dimensional plateau scan.**
M5 generalises M4 by sweeping both edges of the fit window
$[t_0,\,t_\text{end}]$. We use a $10 \times 6$ rectangular grid with
$t_0 \in [10, 25]\,M$ and $t_\text{end} \in [t_\text{end}^\text{min},\,
50]\,M$, where $t_\text{end}^\text{min}$ is set so that the smallest
window has at least $8\,M$ of data. The plateau is the contiguous
$5 \times 3$ rectangle in the grid with smallest combined relative
scatter; the plateau-mean and plateau-standard-deviation are reported
as for M4. M5 is the first (to our knowledge) systematic 2-D
plateau analysis applied to PINN-derived QNM extraction.

## 3.5 Plateau-based fit-window selection

The damped-sinusoid template $A e^{-t/\tau} \cos(\omega t + \phi)$ is
the correct model for $\Phi(x_q, t)$ only inside the QNM regime, after
the prompt response of the initial pulse has passed and before the
late-time power-law tail becomes comparable to the exponential. Fitting
the template to data outside this regime is *model misspecification*,
not a noise problem, and the resulting parameter biases do not vanish
with more data. The fit window is therefore a hyperparameter of the
extraction, and a defensible $(\omega, \tau)$ measurement requires a
defensible choice of window.

We adopt the following operational definition: a fit window
$[t_0,\,t_\text{end}]$ lies inside the QNM regime if and only if
shifting either edge by a small amount produces no significant change
in the recovered $(\omega, \tau)$. The set of such windows forms a
*plateau* in the space of window choices. Inside the plateau the
template is by definition consistent with the data, and the variation
of $(\omega, \tau)$ across the plateau provides an estimate of the
window-systematic uncertainty.

For the Schwarzschild ℓ = 2 fundamental at our parameters, the relevant
timescales are: the prompt-burst duration ($\sim 5\,M$ in our IC), the
overtone decay time $\tau_1 = 3.674\,M$ (so the overtone is suppressed
by a factor $e^{-(t-t_*)/\tau_1}$ relative to its peak), the
fundamental decay time $\tau_0 = 11.241\,M$, and the Price-tail
crossover, which we estimate by setting $e^{-t/\tau_0} \sim t^{-(2\ell+3)}$
for $\ell = 2$, giving a crossover near $t \sim 80\,M$. Since our
simulation domain extends only to $t = 50\,M$, the right edge is
expected to be deep inside the QNM regime; this is borne out
empirically by the M5 plateau rectangles, which extend in
$t_\text{end}$ all the way to the domain end (Sec. 4).

The plateau procedure differs from the standard practice of quoting a
single number from a single fit window, including that of
Patel et al. 2024, who do not specify a numerical
$t_0$ or $t_\text{end}$ and report no fit-window sensitivity. The
present approach is closer in spirit to the start-time scans used in
gravitational-wave ringdown analyses [Berti et al. 2007;
Buonanno–Cook–Pretorius 2007; LIGO/Virgo ringdown analyses], with the
extension that we sweep both edges and report the spread across the
plateau as a systematic error contribution.

## 3.6 Reporting convention

A single $(\omega, \tau)$ pair per experiment is reported using two
different estimators:

- $\omega$ from the M4 plateau mean,
- $\tau$ from M2 over the window
  $[t_\text{post-burst},\,t_\text{max}]$ with
  $t_\text{post-burst} = 10\,M$ (the first local maximum of
  $|\Phi(x_q, t)|$ after the prompt rise, identified once on the
  forward waveform and held fixed across all variants).

This convention follows established practice in numerical-relativity
QNM extraction. Patel et al. 2024 themselves use different estimators
for $\omega$ (FFT) and $\tau$ (log-line through local maxima); Berti
et al. 2007 use FFT for $\omega$ and amplitude-envelope fit for $\tau$.
The motivation in all cases is that $\omega$ is best estimated from
phase information aggregated across many cycles (the M4 plateau is
the lowest-variance such estimator in our setup, with
$\sigma_\omega \sim 10^{-5}$ for the converged variants), while $\tau$
is best estimated from amplitude information over the longest available
decay range (a wider M2 window).

A subtlety arises for the inverse PINN. The ringdown loss term used at
training time contains an A–τ degeneracy: a smaller $\tau$ paired with
a larger $A$ produces a near-equivalent fit at the early edge of the
template window while leaving negligible residuals at late times where
$e^{-t/\tau}$ is small. The optimiser drifts down this degenerate
direction during L-BFGS, so the *learned* $\tau$ recorded in the
parameter history can differ substantially from the decay rate
actually present in the converged $\Phi(x_q, t)$. A direct empirical
diagnostic of this effect is that M2 over the M4-plateau window
$[t_\text{ring}^\text{min}, t_\text{max}]$ recovers the (biased)
learned $\tau$, whereas M2 over the wider physics-validated window
$[t_\text{post-burst}, t_\text{max}]$ recovers a value much closer
to truth. We therefore quote M2 over the wider window as the
headline $\tau$ across all variants, and report the narrower-window
value separately as a diagnostic in Sec. 4 to characterise
the degeneracy.

For each method and each variant, the full (per-window, per-method)
results are tabulated in Appendix C and the corresponding plateau
plots and 2-D heatmaps are shown in Appendix D. The headline
single-number table in Sec. 4 collects only the values
selected by the convention above.
