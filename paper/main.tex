\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We apply physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) to the
Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$ Zerilli equation in the time domain in
three settings: forward, inverse, and a curriculum-learning
extension of the forward problem. The forward PINN reproduces a
finite-difference reference field with relative $L^2$ error
$0.58\%$, compared with $28.06\%$ in the closest prior PINN study
of the same equation \citep{Patel2024pinn}, and recovers the
fundamental quasi-normal mode (QNM) frequency $\omega$ to
$0.029\%$ of the Leaver continued-fraction value. The inverse
PINN jointly trains the network field, the black-hole mass $M$,
and a damped-sinusoid ringdown template against $500$ noisy
observations of the field at $1\%$ Gaussian noise, recovering
$M$ to $0.34\%$, $\omega$ to $0.30\%$, and the damping time
$\tau$ to $1.6\%$. We show analytically and empirically that the
embedded template loss admits an amplitude--rate degeneracy along
which $\tau$ drifts by ten to twenty percent from the Leaver
value with the loss almost stationary; quoting $\tau$ from a
post-hoc fit to the converged network field, rather than from
the learned template scalar, removes this drift. The curriculum
PINN partitions the temporal domain into two or three windows
trained sequentially with numerical-IC transfer between them, in
both hard-stitched and soft-overlap blended forms; we find that
it lowers the per-window error on the earliest sub-domain by
$\sim\!37\%$ but does not improve the full-domain RMSD once the
windows are stitched, on account of an interpolation error that
compounds at every splice. We also
introduce two plateau-based fit-window estimators that scan,
respectively, the start time alone and both edges of the QNM fit
window, and report the across-plateau standard deviation
$\sigma_\omega$ as a window-systematic uncertainty on $\omega$.
The plateau width separates inverse-PINN configurations on which
the two-mode nonlinear least-squares fit converges from those on
which it does not, and so doubles as a deterministic quality
flag computed from the same scan.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

The ringdown phase of a perturbed black hole carries a discrete
spectrum of complex frequencies, the quasi-normal modes (QNMs),
whose values are fixed by the background geometry alone and so
provide a direct spectroscopic probe of the underlying spacetime
\citep{Berti2009review,KonoplyaZhidenko2011}. The ringdown
analysis of GW150914 \citep{LIGO2016ringdown} turned QNM-based
black-hole spectroscopy into an observational discipline.
Tests of the no-hair conjecture, of general relativity in the
strong-field regime, and of beyond-GR or environmental
modifications all turn on how accurately the QNM frequencies are
extracted from a measured ringdown, and how reliably the
associated systematics are quantified
\citep{Berti2007ringdown,Isi2021priors}.

QNMs are computed in two ways, in the frequency domain and in
the time domain. In the frequency domain, Leaver's continued-fraction method
\citep{Leaver1985} and its later refinements tabulate QNMs to
arbitrary numerical precision for Schwarzschild and Kerr
backgrounds and serve as the reference values against which any
other method is calibrated. In the time domain, the master
equation (the Zerilli equation for even-parity perturbations of a
Schwarzschild background) is integrated numerically and the QNMs
are read off the late-time waveform by fitting one or more damped
sinusoids \citep{Berti2009review}. The time-domain approach more
closely mirrors how QNMs are extracted from gravitational-wave
data \citep{Buonanno2007ringdown,LIGO2016ringdown,Isi2021priors},
and it is the approach we adopt here.

The principal methodological issue in the time-domain pipeline is
the choice of fit window. The damped-sinusoid template is
misspecified outside the QNM regime, and the recovered
$(\omega,\tau)$ then carry a window-dependent bias that does not
vanish with more data. Existing analyses generally quote a single
fixed window without a sensitivity scan; in some cases this
systematic is comparable to the quoted statistical errors
\citep{Isi2021priors}.

Physics-informed neural networks \citep{Raissi2017} are a
mesh-free PDE solver in which a neural network is trained to
satisfy the equations of motion together with initial and
boundary conditions through a composite loss. PINNs have been
applied to the QNM problem on Schwarzschild and Kerr backgrounds
in both the frequency and time domains; we survey this literature
in Sec.~\ref{sec:bg-prior}.

Two limitations of that literature motivate the present work.
First, \citet{Patel2024pinn}, the only prior PINN study of the
time-domain Zerilli problem, report a relative $L^{2}$ field
error of $28.06\%$ against their finite-difference reference,
which dominates the resulting QNM error budget; their fit window
is also fixed by hand without a sensitivity scan. Second, no
prior PINN-for-QNM study performs the inverse task of jointly
recovering the black-hole mass and a damped-sinusoid ringdown
template from noisy observations of the field. The embedded
template loss admits an amplitude--rate degeneracy that, if
uncontrolled, biases the learned $\tau$.

We address both points with a forward and inverse PINN for the
Schwarzschild $\ell=2$ Zerilli equation, supplemented by a
curriculum-learning extension of the forward problem.
For the forward problem we extend the architecture-matched
\citet{Patel2024pinn} baseline by doubling the number of L-BFGS
iterations (from $15{,}000$ to $30{,}000$) and adding
residual-greedy adaptive sampling, reaching relative $L^{2}$
field error $0.58\%$ against the finite-difference reference, a
factor of approximately $50$ smaller than the prior work.
We apply five post-hoc QNM extraction methods to the converged
network field. Two of them replace the conventional single-window
fit by a plateau scan over the fit-window edges and report the
across-plateau dispersion of $(\omega,\tau)$ as the
window-systematic uncertainty; the construction is given in
Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}. Under this convention the forward PINN
recovers the fundamental QNM frequency to $0.029\%$. For the inverse problem we train under
$1\%$ Gaussian observation noise on $500$ field samples, isolate
the amplitude-rate degeneracy of the embedded ringdown template
analytically and empirically, and adopt a reporting convention
that extracts $\tau$ post-hoc from the converged network field
rather than from the learned template scalar. A six-variant
ablation of the template-loss hyperparameters identifies a
configuration that recovers the black-hole mass to $0.34\%$,
$\omega$ to $0.30\%$ with plateau standard deviation
$\sigma_\omega = 4\times 10^{-5}$, and $\tau$ to $1.6\%$. The
curriculum-learning extension partitions the temporal domain
into $K = 2$ or $K = 3$ windows trained sequentially, with the
trained $k$-th network supplying the initial condition for the
$(k{+}1)$-th, in both hard-stitched and soft-overlap blended
forms. The strategy is motivated by the observation that the
full-domain forward PINN concentrates $\sim\!41\%$ of its RMSD
on the late-time band $t \in [37.5,50]\,M$, despite that band
occupying only one quarter of the temporal domain.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:background} reviews the Schwarzschild perturbation
problem, the standard QNM extraction methods, the PINN framework,
and the prior PINN-for-QNM literature. Section~\ref{sec:methods}
states the forward, inverse and curriculum PINNs and defines the
five extraction methods. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports the
forward PINN field accuracy, the six inverse PINN variants, and
the three curriculum PINN variants.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the relation to prior work,
the role of the plateau as a quality diagnostic, the residual
gap between forward and inverse precision, and why the
curriculum extension fails to improve on the single-window
baseline.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes. Full hyperparameter tables
and per-window extraction tables are collected in the appendices.

\section{Background and related work}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Schwarzschild perturbations and the QNM spectrum}
\label{sec:bg-zerilli}

Throughout this paper we work with a non-rotating, uncharged,
asymptotically flat black hole of mass $M$, described by the
Schwarzschild line element
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{d}s^{2} \;=\;
  -f(r)\,\mathrm{d}t^{2}
  + f(r)^{-1}\,\mathrm{d}r^{2}
  + r^{2}\bigl(\mathrm{d}\theta^{2}
              + \sin^{2}\!\theta\,\mathrm{d}\varphi^{2}\bigr),
  \qquad f(r) \;=\; 1 - \frac{2M}{r},
  \label{eq:schwarzschild}
\end{equation}
in geometric units $G = c = 1$. The horizon is at $r = 2M$ and
spatial infinity at $r \to \infty$.

The ringdown of the black hole is described by small perturbations
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:schwarzschild}. We write the perturbed metric as
$g_{\mu\nu} = g_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{Schw}} + h_{\mu\nu}$ with
$|h_{\mu\nu}| \ll |g_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{Schw}}|$, keep only terms
linear in $h_{\mu\nu}$ in the vacuum Einstein equations
$R_{\mu\nu}[g] = 0$, and look for the dynamics of $h_{\mu\nu}$ on
the fixed Schwarzschild background
\citep{ReggeWheeler1957,Berti2009review}. Because the background
is spherically symmetric, every component of $h_{\mu\nu}$ admits
a tensor-spherical-harmonic expansion of the schematic form
\begin{equation}
  h_{\mu\nu}(t,r,\theta,\varphi)
  \;=\; \sum_{\ell, m}
  h_{\mu\nu}^{(\ell m)}(t,r)\;Y^{(\ell m)}_{\mu\nu}(\theta,\varphi),
  \label{eq:harmonic-expansion}
\end{equation}
where $Y^{(\ell m)}_{\mu\nu}$ denotes the appropriate set of
tensor harmonics on the unit two-sphere
\citep{ReggeWheeler1957,Berti2009review}. Substituting
Eq.~\eqref{eq:harmonic-expansion} into the linearised vacuum
equations decouples them between distinct $(\ell, m)$. Within
each $(\ell, m)$ the system further decouples by parity. Under
the reflection $(\theta,\varphi)\to(\pi-\theta,\varphi+\pi)$
the tensor harmonics split into two classes,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{P}\,h_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{axial}} \;=\; (-1)^{\ell+1}\,h_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{axial}},
  \qquad
  \mathcal{P}\,h_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{polar}} \;=\; (-1)^{\ell}\,h_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{polar}},
  \label{eq:parity}
\end{equation}
the \emph{axial} (odd-parity) and \emph{polar} (even-parity)
sectors. Since the background and the linearised operator
commute with $\mathcal{P}$, the two sectors are independent and
are analysed separately.

General relativity has gauge freedom, so the components of
$h_{\mu\nu}$ can be changed by an infinitesimal coordinate
transformation $x^{\mu} \to x^{\mu} + \xi^{\mu}$ that induces
$h_{\mu\nu} \to h_{\mu\nu} - \nabla_{\mu}\xi_{\nu} - \nabla_{\nu}\xi_{\mu}$ without changing any physical quantity. The
physical content of each sector is captured by a single
gauge-invariant scalar function of $(t,r)$, constructed from the
metric perturbation components. For the polar sector, in the
Regge--Wheeler gauge the perturbation is described by four metric
functions $H_0, H_1, H_2, K$ of $(t,r)$ at each $(\ell, m)$; the
linearised vacuum Einstein equations relate them algebraically
and reduce the dynamics to a single gauge-invariant scalar
$\Phi(t,r)$ via the Moncrief construction
\citep{Moncrief1974,Berti2009review,KonoplyaZhidenko2011}. The
full metric perturbation is reconstructed from $\Phi$ via
\begin{equation}
  h_{\mu\nu}(t,r,\theta,\varphi)
  \;=\; \frac{1}{r}\sum_{\ell, m}
  \Phi^{(\ell m)}(t,r)\;
  T^{(\ell m)}_{\mu\nu}(\theta,\varphi),
  \label{eq:moncrief-ansatz}
\end{equation}
where $T^{(\ell m)}_{\mu\nu}$ is a known tensor-harmonic
template \citep{Moncrief1974,Berti2009review}. For the axial
sector the analogous reduction yields the
Regge--Wheeler equation; for the polar sector it yields the
Zerilli equation. Both equations take the form
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{t}^{2}\Phi
  - \partial_{x}^{2}\Phi
  + V_{\ell}\bigl(r(x)\bigr)\,\Phi \;=\; 0,
  \label{eq:zerilli}
\end{equation}
in the tortoise coordinate $x$, defined by
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}r} \;=\; \frac{1}{f(r)}
  \;=\; \left(1 - \frac{2M}{r}\right)^{-1}
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  x \;=\; r + 2M\,\ln\!\left(\frac{r}{2M} - 1\right).
  \label{eq:tortoise}
\end{equation}
The reason for the change of variable in Eq.~\eqref{eq:tortoise}
is that the curved-background wave operator acting on $\Phi(t,r)$
contains a position-dependent factor $f(r)$,
$g^{\mu\nu}\partial_{\mu}\partial_{\nu}\Phi
= -f^{-1}\partial_{t}^{2}\Phi + f\,\partial_{r}^{2}\Phi + \ldots$,
and the substitution $\partial_{r} = f^{-1}\partial_{x}$ converts
$f\,\partial_{r}^{2}$ into $f^{-1}\partial_{x}^{2}$ up to lower
order terms that are absorbed into the effective potential. Multiplying through by
$-f$ then leaves the d'Alembertian in the flat 1+1 form
$-\partial_{t}^{2} + \partial_{x}^{2}$ that appears in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}. Eq.~\eqref{eq:tortoise} sends the horizon
at $r = 2M$ to $x \to -\infty$ and leaves spatial infinity at
$x \to +\infty$, so the physical region $r > 2M$ maps onto the
full real line in $x$. The Zerilli potential for the polar sector
at multipole $\ell$ is
\begin{equation}
  V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r) \;=\;
  \frac{2 f(r)}{r^{3}}\,
  \frac{n^{2}(n+1)\,r^{3} + 3 n^{2} M r^{2}
        + 9 n M^{2} r + 9 M^{3}}{(n r + 3 M)^{2}},
  \qquad n \;\equiv\; \tfrac{1}{2}(\ell - 1)(\ell + 2),
  \label{eq:V-zerilli}
\end{equation}
and vanishes both at the horizon and at infinity, since
$f(2M) = 0$ at the horizon and $f \to 1$ with $V \sim r^{-2}$ at
infinity \citep{Berti2009review}. Quantitatively, the
asymptotic behaviour is
\begin{equation}
  V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r(x)) \;\sim\;
  \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r^{2}}
      \;\xrightarrow[x\to+\infty]{}\; \dfrac{\ell(\ell+1)}{x^{2}} \;\to\; 0,
      & x \to +\infty,\\[2pt]
    \;\propto\; e^{x/(2M)}
      \;\xrightarrow[x\to-\infty]{}\; 0,
      & x \to -\infty.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:V-asymptotic}
\end{equation}
In both asymptotic regions Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} therefore
reduces to the free wave equation
$(\partial_{t}^{2} - \partial_{x}^{2})\Phi = 0$, whose general
solution is a sum of left- and right-movers,
$\Phi(t,x) = F(t-x) + G(t+x)$. Equation~\eqref{eq:zerilli} is
therefore a wave equation on a finite-height potential barrier
centred near $r \simeq 3M$, the photon-sphere radius for
Schwarzschild, with free propagation on either side; this
barrier is what gives the QNM spectrum its
characteristic ringdown structure. Figure~\ref{fig:bg-potential}
shows $V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r(x))$ as a function of the tortoise
coordinate for $\ell = 2, 3, 4$, and Figure~\ref{fig:bg-tortoise}
shows the tortoise map $r(x)$ used in
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}--\eqref{eq:tortoise}. The present paper
studies the \emph{polar} ($\ell = 2$) sector and uses
Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli} throughout. We adopt $M = 1$ as our unit
of mass and time.

A quasi-normal mode is a solution of Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} of
separated form $\Phi(t,x) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega t}\,
\psi(x)$. Substituting this ansatz turns Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}
into the time-independent Schr\"odinger-like equation
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}^{2}\psi}{\mathrm{d}x^{2}}
  + \bigl(\omega^{2} - V_{\ell}(r(x))\bigr)\psi(x) \;=\; 0.
  \label{eq:zerilli-freq}
\end{equation}
In the asymptotic regions where $V_{\ell} \to 0$,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli-freq} reduces to
$\psi'' + \omega^{2}\psi = 0$ with general solution
$\psi(x) = a_{\pm}\,\mathrm{e}^{+\mathrm{i}\omega x}
+ b_{\pm}\,\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega x}$. Combined with the
time factor $\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega t}$, the basis
$\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega(t \mp x)}$ describes waves moving
to the right (sign $-$) or to the left (sign $+$). The QNM
boundary conditions select the modes that are purely ingoing at
the horizon (left-moving as $x \to -\infty$) and purely outgoing
at infinity (right-moving as $x \to +\infty$),
\begin{equation}
  \psi(x) \;\to\; \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega x}\;\;(x \to -\infty),
  \qquad
  \psi(x) \;\to\; \mathrm{e}^{+\mathrm{i}\omega x}\;\;(x \to +\infty),
  \label{eq:qnm-bc}
\end{equation}
with no incoming radiation from either asymptotic region
\citep{Berti2009review,KonoplyaZhidenko2011}. The boundary
conditions Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-bc} state that radiation is allowed
to leave through both ends but never to enter; energy is
therefore continuously lost from the system, and the associated
linear operator is non-Hermitian. Non-Hermitian eigenvalue
problems do not in general have real eigenvalues, and the
spectrum $\{\omega_{n}\}$ of admissible frequencies is instead
discrete and complex,
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{n} \;=\; \omega_{n}^{R} - \mathrm{i}/\tau_{n},
  \qquad \omega_{n}^{R}, \tau_{n} > 0,
  \label{eq:qnm-omega}
\end{equation}
ordered by overtone index $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ in increasing
imaginary part. The real part $\omega_{n}^{R}$ is the oscillation
frequency of the mode and the imaginary part sets the damping
time $\tau_{n}$, the e-folding time over which the mode
amplitude decays. Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-omega} into
$\Phi = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_{n} t}\psi_{n}(x)$ gives
$\Phi \propto \mathrm{e}^{-t/\tau_{n}}\,\mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega_{n}^{R} t}\psi_{n}(x)$,
and taking the real part with an arbitrary complex amplitude
$A_{n}\,\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}\phi_{n}}\psi_{n}(\xq)$ yields the
real damped sinusoid
$A_{n}\,\mathrm{e}^{-t/\tau_{n}}\cos(\omega_{n}^{R} t + \phi_{n})$
at the observer position. The time-domain field at a fixed
observer position is, in the QNM regime (after the prompt
response of the initial data and before the late-time power-law
tail), well approximated by a sum of these damped sinusoids,
$\Phi(\xq, t) \approx \sum_n A_n e^{-(t-t_0)/\tau_n}
\cos(\omega_n^R(t-t_0) + \phi_n)$. This representation is the
basis for every extraction method discussed below.

\label{sec:bg-spectrum}
For Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$ the fundamental QNM ($n = 0$) and the
first overtone ($n = 1$) have the values
\begin{equation}
  M\omega_{0} = 0.3737, \quad \tau_{0}/M = 11.241,
  \qquad
  M\omega_{1} = 0.3467, \quad \tau_{1}/M = 3.651,
  \label{eq:qnm-ref}
\end{equation}
computed to arbitrary numerical precision by Leaver's
continued-fraction method \citep{Leaver1985} and tabulated in the
review of \citet{Berti2009review}. These are the reference values
against which every extraction reported in this paper is
benchmarked. Physically, the fundamental $\ell = 2$ mode is the
dominant component of the ringdown signal observed in
binary-black-hole mergers and is the primary target of
black-hole spectroscopy
\citep{Buonanno2007ringdown,LIGO2016ringdown,Isi2021priors}; the
first overtone enters the analysis at sufficient signal-to-noise
ratio and is the next target.

\label{sec:bg-methods}
Frequency-domain methods compute the QNM spectrum of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-bc} from the master equation directly. The
semi-analytic WKB approximation, originally due to
\citet{SchutzWill1985} and extended to higher orders by
\citet{IyerWill1987} and \citet{Konoplya2003wkb}, expands
$\psi(x)$ around the peak of $V_{\ell}$ and produces closed-form
expressions for $\omega_{n}$ that are accurate at low overtone
index but degrade for $n \gtrsim \ell$. The asymptotic-iteration
method (AIM) of \citet{Cho2010aim} recasts the time-independent
form of Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} as a two-term recursion whose
fixed point yields $\omega_{n}$, with accuracy comparable to CFM
on Schwarzschild and Kerr. Leaver's continued-fraction method
(CFM) \citep{Leaver1985}, refined by \citet{Nollert1993}, expresses
$\psi(x)$ as a power series at the horizon whose coefficients
satisfy a three-term recursion; the QNM condition becomes a
continued-fraction equation that can be solved to arbitrary
numerical precision and produces the reference values quoted in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-ref}.

In the time domain the master equation Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} is
integrated forward from prescribed initial data and the QNMs are
read off the late-time waveform $\Phi(\xq, t)$ at a fixed observer
position $\xq$ by fitting one or more damped sinusoids using a
spectral, nonlinear-least-squares, or signal-subspace estimator;
the Prony \citep{Berti2007prony}, matrix-pencil
\citep{HuaSarkar1990} and ESPRIT \citep{Roy1989esprit} methods are
the principal subspace variants. The estimators M1--M5 introduced
in Sec.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods} all belong to this time-domain
family. This is the approach the present paper follows, and the
one that mirrors how QNMs are recovered from gravitational-wave
data \citep{Buonanno2007ringdown,LIGO2016ringdown,Isi2021priors}.

\subsection{Time-domain QNM extraction and the fit-window problem}
\label{sec:bg-extraction}

A ringdown waveform $\Phi(\xq, t)$ measured at fixed observer
position is, in the QNM regime, a sum of damped sinusoids
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(\xq, t) \;\approx\;
  \sum_{n} A_n\,\mathrm{e}^{-(t-t_0)/\tau_n}
  \cos\bigl(\omega_n^{R}(t-t_0) + \phi_n\bigr),
  \label{eq:qnm-template}
\end{equation}
plus a prompt response at early times and a power-law tail at
late times \citep{Berti2009review}. Three families of methods
recover $(\omega_n,\tau_n)$ from samples of $\Phi$. Spectral
methods locate $\omega_n^{R}$ as peaks in the discrete Fourier
transform of $\Phi$ inside the QNM regime and recover $\tau_n$
from the linear slope of $\log|\Phi|$ versus $t$
\citep{Berti2009review}. The latter follows by isolating the
fundamental mode at late times in Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-template},
$|\Phi(\xq,t)| \approx A_{0}\,e^{-(t-t_{0})/\tau_{0}}$ at the
local extrema of the cosine, so that
\begin{equation}
  \log|\Phi(\xq, t_k)| \;\approx\;
  \log A_{0} - \frac{t_k - t_0}{\tau_{0}}
  \label{eq:logline}
\end{equation}
is linear in $t_k$ at the peak times $\{t_k\}$, and a least-squares
straight-line fit returns $\tau_{0}$ as the negative reciprocal
slope. Nonlinear least squares (NLS) methods fit
Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-template} directly, jointly optimising the
amplitudes, phases, frequencies and decay times for a
user-specified number of modes \citep{Buonanno2007ringdown}.
Subspace methods such as ESPRIT \citep{Roy1989esprit} treat the
sampled waveform as the output of a linear time-invariant system
whose poles encode $(\omega_n,\tau_n)$. Concretely, the analytic
signal sampled at uniform spacing $\Delta t$ is modelled as a sum
of complex exponentials,
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(\xq, k\Delta t)
  \;\approx\; \sum_{n=0}^{K-1} c_{n}\, z_{n}^{\,k},
  \qquad
  z_{n} \;=\; e^{(-1/\tau_{n} + \mathrm{i}\omega_{n}^{R})\Delta t},
  \label{eq:esprit-model}
\end{equation}
with complex amplitudes $c_n$. The poles $\{z_{n}\}$ are recovered
by a generalised eigendecomposition of a Hankel data matrix built
from the samples \citep{Roy1989esprit,HuaSarkar1990}, after which
$(\omega_{n}^{R},\tau_{n})$ are read off via
$\omega_{n}^{R} = \Im(\log z_{n})/\Delta t$ and
$\tau_{n} = -\Delta t/\Re(\log z_{n})$, without a nonlinear
optimisation. All three families share a single
hyperparameter that dominates the systematic error budget, namely
the choice of fit window $[t_0, t_{\mathrm{end}}]$. Outside the
QNM regime the template of Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-template} is
misspecified, and the recovered $(\omega_n,\tau_n)$ carry a
window-dependent bias that does not vanish with more data.
Existing analyses generally quote a single fixed window and do
not scan its edges
\citep{Buonanno2007ringdown,LIGO2016ringdown}; in the
gravitational-wave data analysis literature this issue is known
and has been addressed by Bayesian priors over the start time
\citep{Isi2021priors}, but a deterministic plateau-based
diagnostic reported alongside the primary estimate is not in
common use. Sections~\ref{sec:qnm-methods} and \ref{sec:plateau}
introduce five estimators M1--M5, of which M4 and M5 implement
plateau scans over the start time alone and over both window
edges respectively, and report the in-plateau dispersion as a
window-systematic uncertainty. Figure~\ref{fig:bg-plateau-cartoon}
illustrates the qualitative shape of $\omega(t_0)$ that motivates
the plateau construction.

\subsection{PINNs and prior PINN-for-QNM work}
\label{sec:bg-pinn}

A physics-informed neural network \citep{Raissi2017} is a
mesh-free PDE solver. The candidate solution is a neural network
$\Phi_\theta(x,t)$ with trainable parameters $\theta$, and
$\theta$ is tuned so that $\Phi_\theta$ satisfies the PDE
together with its initial and boundary conditions. Concretely,
for a PDE written in residual form
$\mathcal{N}[\Phi](x,t) = 0$, where $\mathcal{N}$ is a (possibly
nonlinear) differential operator (for the Zerilli problem of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}, $\mathcal{N}[\Phi] \equiv \partial_{t}^{2}\Phi - \partial_{x}^{2}\Phi + V_{\ell}\,\Phi$),
the pointwise residual
\begin{equation}
  r_{\theta}(x,t) \;\equiv\; \mathcal{N}[\Phi_{\theta}](x,t)
  \label{eq:pinn-residual-bg}
\end{equation}
is evaluated at a finite set of \emph{collocation points}
$\{(x_i, t_i)\}_{i=1}^{N_r}$ sampled from the spacetime domain.
The fit is made quantitative by a composite loss
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(\theta) \;=\;
  \lambda_{\mathrm{pde}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{ic}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{bc}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}},
  \label{eq:pinn-loss-bg}
\end{equation}
in which the PDE term is the mean squared residual on the
interior collocation set,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}(\theta)
  \;=\; \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{i=1}^{N_r} r_{\theta}(x_i, t_i)^{2},
  \label{eq:pinn-pde-loss-bg}
\end{equation}
and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ic}}, \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bc}}$ are
the analogous mean squared residuals of the IC and BC operators
on collocation points sampled at $t = 0$ and at the spatial
boundaries respectively. The weights $\lambda_*$ balance the
three contributions and are themselves a hyperparameter; their
choice and the gradient pathologies that follow when they are
badly balanced are an active area of PINN research
\citep{Wang2021gradientpath,Wang2022ntk,McClenny2023sapinn}.

The spatial and temporal derivatives that appear in
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pde}}$ are obtained by
\emph{automatic differentiation}, which propagates exact
analytic derivatives through the chain of operations that defines
$\Phi_\theta$. Concretely, if $\Phi_\theta = f_{L} \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_{1}(x,t)$ is the composition of
layers $f_{\ell}(z) = \sigma(W_{\ell} z + b_{\ell})$, then any
derivative such as $\partial_{t}^{2}\Phi_{\theta}$ is obtained by
repeated application of the chain rule through the layers, with
the Jacobians of each $f_{\ell}$ stored during the forward pass
\citep{Baydin2018ad}. No spatial mesh is required and no truncation
error is introduced at the level of the derivatives, in contrast
to finite-difference solvers; the only discretisation error comes
from the finite collocation set. The same property makes the
framework apply unchanged to nonlinear and high-dimensional
problems.

The loss Eq.~\eqref{eq:pinn-loss-bg} is non-convex in $\theta$
and is typically minimised in two phases. A first-order optimiser
(Adam, \citealt{Kingma2015adam}) takes stochastic gradient steps
from the random initialisation and brings the network into a
basin of low loss. A quasi-Newton optimiser (L-BFGS,
\citealt{Liu1989lbfgs}) then uses an approximation to the loss
Hessian, built from a few past gradients, to take much larger
steps inside that basin and reduce the residual by several
further orders of magnitude on smooth PDE problems; the trade-off
is that L-BFGS requires a near-quadratic local landscape and
therefore needs the Adam phase first. Two further refinements
are now standard. Gradient-enhanced PINNs (gPINN,
\citealt{Yu2022gpinn}) add a residual on the gradient of the
PDE residual, $\nabla \mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{pde}}$, which forces
the residual not only to be small in absolute value but also to
vary smoothly across the domain, suppressing the
fit-the-residual-by-oscillation pathology of plain PINNs on
stiff problems. Residual-adaptive (RAR/RAD) sampling
\citep{Wu2023adaptive} concentrates collocation points where
the current PDE residual is largest. On wave-like equations the
residual is concentrated along the wavefront, so uniform sampling
wastes collocation budget on the quiescent region behind the
pulse; RAR moves points to where they are needed and empirically
outperforms uniform random sampling on this class of problems.
Mature implementations such as DeepXDE \citep{Lu2021deepxde}
expose all of these features as standard options.

Fig.~\ref{fig:bg-pinn-arch}; the specific architecture used in
this work is given in Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-pinn-arch} of
Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}.

\label{sec:bg-prior}
Five prior works apply PINNs to the QNM problem; all five solve
the forward problem (computing QNMs from a known equation), not
the inverse problem. \citet{Ovgun2021qnmnn} use a
feedforward network to compute QNMs of dS and AdS black holes from
the shooting form of the eigenvalue equation. \citet{Cornell2022pinnqnm}
introduce a frequency-domain PINN for Schwarzschild QNMs in which
the complex eigenvalue $\omega$ is a trainable parameter alongside
the network weights, and report sub-permille accuracy on the
fundamental $\ell = 2$ mode against the Leaver reference.
\citet{Luna2022teukolskypinn} extend this to time-domain Kerr
perturbations by training a PINN on the Teukolsky equation with
the normalisation boundary condition hard-enforced via an output
transform, with sequential warm-starts in the spin parameter.
\citet{Patel2024pinn} are the closest comparator. They solve the
time-domain Zerilli and Regge--Wheeler
equations on a Schwarzschild background with a fully-connected
PINN, report forward-problem field accuracy of
$\mathrm{RL2} = 28.06\%$ against an FD reference, and quote the
M2 NLS fit on a single fixed window as the primary QNM
estimate. \citet{Luna2024qnm} address the frequency-domain QNM
eigenvalue problem in modified gravity, with a network
parametrising the radial wave function and the eigenvalue
$\omega$ trained simultaneously. Our forward PINN matches the
setup of \citet{Patel2024pinn} closely enough for a direct
field-accuracy comparison (Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}). The
eigenvalue result of \citet{Cornell2022pinnqnm} is in the
frequency domain and is not directly comparable to our
time-domain post-hoc fit.
The two limitations stated in the introduction (forward field
accuracy and absence of an inverse formulation) translate into
four concrete gaps that we address in
Secs.~\ref{sec:methods}--\ref{sec:res-inv}: no inverse PINN that
recovers the mass jointly with a damped-sinusoid template; no
fit-window systematic on the recovered $(\omega,\tau)$; no
analysis of the amplitude--rate degeneracy of an embedded
template; and no ablation of the template-loss hyperparameters.
Figure~\ref{fig:bg-pipeline} summarises the resulting forward and
inverse pipelines.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We applied a forward, inverse and curriculum PINN to the
Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$ Zerilli equation. On the forward
problem, doubling the
number of L-BFGS iterations (from $15{,}000$ to $30{,}000$) and
adding residual-greedy adaptive sampling reduces the relative
$L^2$ field error against the FD reference from $28.06\%$ in
\citet{Patel2024pinn} to $0.58\%$, a factor of approximately $50$
smaller on an architecture-matched baseline. The fundamental QNM
is recovered from the converged field to $0.029\%$ in $\omega$
(M4) and $1.99\%$ in $\tau$ (M2).

On the inverse problem, we identify an approximate
$A_{\mathrm{ring}}$--$\tau$ degeneracy of the embedded ringdown
template loss that allows the learned $\tau$ to drift by ten to
twenty percent from the Leaver value while the loss remains
stationary. Quoting $\tau$ from a post-hoc fit to the converged
network field rather than from the learned template scalar
bypasses the degeneracy and recovers $\tau$ to a few percent on
the same runs. A six-variant ablation of the template-loss hyperparameters
identifies a configuration that recovers the black-hole mass to
$0.34\%$, $\omega$ to $0.30\%$ with plateau standard deviation
$\sigma_\omega = 4\times 10^{-5}$, and $\tau$ to $1.6\%$, all
under $1\%$ Gaussian observation noise on $\Nobs = 500$ field
samples.

The main methodological contribution is the plateau-based
extraction of Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}. Across the six inverse
variants the plateau standard deviation $\sigma_\omega$ separates
configurations on which the two-mode NLS converges from those on
which it does not, supplying a deterministic quality flag at no
extra cost. The same diagnostic applies unchanged to the forward
PINN and to the FD reference solver, so that no quoted number
depends on a fixed-by-hand fit window.

The curriculum PINN of Sec.~\ref{sec:cur-pinn} delivers its
intended local benefit on the earliest sub-domain (a $37\%$
reduction in RMSD on $t\in[0,25]\,M$ relative to the same
sub-domain of the single-window baseline) but does not translate
that benefit into a lower full-domain RMSD or a tighter QNM
extraction. The cost is the per-stitch interpolation error of
the numerical IC transfer between consecutive windows, which
grows with the number of stitching boundaries; the soft-overlap
blending and continuity anchors do not recover the lost
precision and in fact perform worse than the hard-partition
stitch on this problem. The single-window forward PINN with
greedy adaptive sampling therefore remains the recommended
configuration for the time-domain Zerilli QNM problem.

Three follow-up directions are immediate. A Cram\'er--Rao bound
on $(M,\omega,\tau)$ for the noisy ringdown template would
quantify how much of the residual factor-of-ten gap between
forward and inverse $\omega$ accuracy is intrinsic to the
available information and how much is training systematics. The
deterministic point estimate plus plateau $\sigma_\omega$ can be
embedded in a Bayesian posterior over $(M,\omega,\tau)$ in the
spirit of \citet{Isi2021priors}, with the trained PINN as the
forward operator. The same pipeline lifts to Kerr by substituting
the Teukolsky operator for the Zerilli operator and to
multi-channel ringdowns by training one head per harmonic.

\section{Finite-difference reference solver}\label{app:fd}

This appendix gives the full mathematical specification of the
finite-difference (FD) reference solver used to generate
$\Phi_{\mathrm{FD}}(x,t)$ throughout the paper.

\subsection{Continuous problem}

We integrate the standard stable form of the master equation
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli},
\begin{equation}
  \partial_t^2 \Phi(x,t) - \partial_x^2 \Phi(x,t)
  + V_\ell(x)\,\Phi(x,t) = 0,
  \qquad
  (x,t) \in [x_L,x_R]\times[0,t_{\max}],
  \label{eq:fd-pde}
\end{equation}
with $V_\ell$ the Zerilli potential of Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli} for
$\ell=2$ and $M=1$, on the rectangle
$[x_L,x_R]=[-50,150]\,M$, $t_{\max} = 50\,M$. The initial data are
the outgoing Gaussian of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ic}, namely
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(x,0) = A\,\exp\!\Bigl(-\tfrac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma^2}\Bigr),
  \qquad
  \partial_t\Phi(x,0) = -\partial_x\Phi(x,0),
  \label{eq:fd-ic}
\end{equation}
with $A=1$, $x_0 = 4\,M$, $\sigma = 5\,M$. The far-field
boundaries are the radiative Sommerfeld conditions of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:bc-sommerfeld},
\begin{equation}
  (\partial_t - \partial_x)\Phi\bigr|_{x=x_L} = 0,
  \qquad
  (\partial_t + \partial_x)\Phi\bigr|_{x=x_R} = 0,
  \label{eq:fd-bc}
\end{equation}
which transmit outgoing waves with no reflection in the limit
$V_\ell\to 0$ that holds asymptotically far from the photon-sphere
peak at $r \simeq 3M$.

\subsection{Method of lines}

We reduce Eq.~\eqref{eq:fd-pde} to a first-order system in time by
introducing $\Pi \equiv \partial_t \Phi$, giving the pair
\begin{equation}
  \partial_t \Phi = \Pi,
  \qquad
  \partial_t \Pi  = \partial_x^2 \Phi - V_\ell\,\Phi.
  \label{eq:fd-system}
\end{equation}
The spatial domain is discretised on the uniform grid
$x_i = x_L + i\,\Delta x$ for $i=0,\dots,N_x-1$, with
$\Delta x = 0.2\,M$ and $N_x = 1001$ points. Let
$\Phi_i^n \approx \Phi(x_i, t_n)$ and
$\Pi_i^n \approx \Pi(x_i, t_n)$. The interior second derivative is
the standard three-point centred stencil,
\begin{equation}
  (\partial_x^2 \Phi)_i
  = \frac{\Phi_{i+1} - 2\Phi_i + \Phi_{i-1}}{(\Delta x)^2}
  + \mathcal{O}\bigl((\Delta x)^2\bigr),
  \qquad i = 1,\dots,N_x-2.
  \label{eq:fd-d2-interior}
\end{equation}
At the two endpoints we use the four-point second-order one-sided
formulas
\begin{align}
  (\partial_x^2 \Phi)_0 &= \frac{2\Phi_0 - 5\Phi_1 + 4\Phi_2 - \Phi_3}{(\Delta x)^2}
    + \mathcal{O}\bigl((\Delta x)^2\bigr),
  \label{eq:fd-d2-left}\\
  (\partial_x^2 \Phi)_{N_x-1} &= \frac{2\Phi_{N_x-1} - 5\Phi_{N_x-2} + 4\Phi_{N_x-3} - \Phi_{N_x-4}}{(\Delta x)^2}
    + \mathcal{O}\bigl((\Delta x)^2\bigr),
  \label{eq:fd-d2-right}
\end{align}
so that the global spatial truncation error of the discrete
operator $L_h \Phi \equiv \partial_x^2 \Phi - V_\ell\,\Phi$ is
$\mathcal{O}((\Delta x)^2)$ uniformly in $i$.

The semi-discrete system is then the $2N_x$-dimensional ODE
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d} t}\!
  \begin{pmatrix} \Phi \\ \Pi \end{pmatrix}
  =
  \begin{pmatrix} \Pi \\ L_h \Phi \end{pmatrix}
  \equiv
  F(\Phi,\Pi),
  \label{eq:fd-mol}
\end{equation}
on which we apply classical fourth-order Runge--Kutta time
integration with step $\Delta t = 0.1\,M$ and
$N_t = 500$ steps,
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    k_1 &= F(y^n), \\
    k_2 &= F(y^n + \tfrac12\Delta t\,k_1), \\
    k_3 &= F(y^n + \tfrac12\Delta t\,k_2), \\
    k_4 &= F(y^n + \Delta t\,k_3), \\
    y^{n+1} &= y^n + \tfrac{\Delta t}{6}\bigl(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4\bigr),
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:fd-rk4}
\end{equation}
with $y^n \equiv (\Phi^n,\Pi^n)^\top$. The temporal truncation
error is $\mathcal{O}((\Delta t)^4)$ per step and
$\mathcal{O}((\Delta t)^4)$ globally over the fixed integration
window $[0,t_{\max}]$. Combined with
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:fd-d2-interior}--\eqref{eq:fd-d2-right} the scheme
is second-order accurate in space and fourth-order accurate in
time.

\subsection{CFL stability}

For the constant-coefficient wave equation obtained by setting
$V_\ell \equiv 0$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:fd-pde}, the maximum
characteristic speed is $c = 1$. Reducing Eq.~\eqref{eq:fd-pde} to
the first-order system Eq.~\eqref{eq:fd-system} and applying the
centred stencil Eq.~\eqref{eq:fd-d2-interior} produces purely
imaginary spatial eigenvalues
$\mu_k = \pm i\,2\sin(k\Delta x/2)/\Delta x$ with
$|\mu_k|\le 2/\Delta x$, so the RK4 stability boundary on the
imaginary axis $|\mu \Delta t|\le 2\sqrt{2}$ gives the linear
stability condition
\begin{equation}
  \nu \equiv \frac{c\,\Delta t}{\Delta x} \le \sqrt{2}
  \approx 1.414.
  \label{eq:fd-cfl}
\end{equation}
We use $\nu = 0.5$, well inside the stability region; the
potential $V_\ell(x) \ge 0$ acts as a non-negative mass term that
does not increase the maximum characteristic speed and therefore
does not tighten the bound. With this Courant factor the scheme
is dissipation-free at leading order and introduces only
numerical dispersion at $\mathcal{O}((\Delta x)^2)$.

\subsection{Boundary update}

The Sommerfeld conditions of Eq.~\eqref{eq:fd-bc} are imposed
algebraically on $\Pi$ at every RK4 substage. With
$(\partial_t - \partial_x)\Phi = 0$ at $x = x_L$ and
$\Pi = \partial_t \Phi$, the left endpoint update is
$\Pi_0 = (\partial_x \Phi)_0$. Using the second-order one-sided
first derivative,
\begin{equation}
  (\partial_x \Phi)_0 = \frac{-3\Phi_0 + 4\Phi_1 - \Phi_2}{2\Delta x}
  + \mathcal{O}\bigl((\Delta x)^2\bigr),
  \label{eq:fd-dx-left}
\end{equation}
and analogously $\Pi_{N_x-1} = -(\partial_x\Phi)_{N_x-1}$ at the
right endpoint with the mirror stencil. The resulting boundary
update preserves the second-order spatial accuracy of the
interior scheme and is applied after each of the four RK4
substages \eqref{eq:fd-rk4}, so that the intermediate stages
$y^n + \alpha\,\Delta t\,k_j$ also satisfy the radiative
condition before being passed to $F$.

\subsection{Causal isolation of the QNM window}

The Sommerfeld update is approximate, since it treats the
asymptotic problem $V_\ell \to 0$ as exact at finite $|x|$. The
arrival times of any residual reflections at the observer column
$\xq = 10\,M$ are bounded below by Eq.~\eqref{eq:bc-arrival},
which evaluates to at least $108\,M$ from the left boundary and
$280\,M$ from the right boundary, both well outside
$t_{\max} = 50\,M$. The QNM extraction window
$t \in [10,50]\,M$ is therefore causally disconnected from the
boundaries on the simulated domain, and the FD reference is
insensitive at machine precision to the quality of the boundary
update inside the window of interest.

\subsection{Cost and outputs}

The full simulation has $N_x N_t \approx 5\times 10^5$
space-time grid points and runs in under one minute on a single
CPU core. The solver writes
$\Phi_{\mathrm{FD}}(x_i, t_n)$ on the
$1001\times 501$ grid to disk, which is the
single source used for all field-accuracy metrics in
Sec.~\ref{sec:res-fwd} and as the noise-free truth for the
inverse-PINN observation generator of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:noisy-obs}.

\section{Leaver's continued-fraction method}\label{app:leaver}

This appendix records the analytic procedure that produces the
reference QNM frequencies
$M\omega_n$ used throughout the paper for benchmarking and
percentage-error reporting. The derivation follows
\citet{Leaver1985}; the only Schwarzschild-specific algebra needed
beyond the ansatz is collected in Table~I of that paper. We
restate the steps here so the analytic baseline of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-ref} is self-contained.

\subsection{Master equation and QNM boundary conditions}

The Regge--Wheeler and Zerilli equations of
Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-problem} share the time-harmonic radial form
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}^2 \psi(r)}{\mathrm{d} r_*^2}
  + \bigl[\omega^2 - V_\ell(r)\bigr]\,\psi(r) = 0,
  \qquad
  \psi(x,t) = \psi(r)\,e^{-i\omega t},
  \label{eq:lv-rw}
\end{equation}
with $r_*$ the tortoise coordinate of Eq.~\eqref{eq:tortoise} and
$V_\ell$ either the Regge--Wheeler or the Zerilli potential. In
the asymptotic regions $V_\ell \to 0$, so $\psi$ behaves as a
free wave, and the QNM boundary conditions select the modes that
are purely ingoing at the horizon and purely outgoing at
infinity,
\begin{equation}
  \psi(r) \sim e^{-i\omega r_*} \;\;\text{as}\;\; r \to 2M^+,
  \qquad
  \psi(r) \sim e^{+i\omega r_*} \;\;\text{as}\;\; r \to \infty.
  \label{eq:lv-bc}
\end{equation}
For complex $\omega = \omega_R - i/\tau$ with $\tau > 0$ the
outgoing solution diverges at infinity in $r$, so QNMs cannot be
captured by direct numerical shooting; \citet{Leaver1985} replaces
the shooting problem by a convergence condition on a power
series, which is the construction below.

\subsection{Frobenius--Jaff\'e ansatz}

We work in Leaver's units $2M = 1$, so $r$ is now dimensionless
and $f(r) = 1 - 1/r$. Setting $\rho \equiv -i\omega$ and applying
the standard Jaff\'e construction \citep{Leaver1985,Nollert1993},
the outgoing solution of Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rw} is written as
\begin{equation}
  \psi(r) \;=\;
  r^{1+s}\,(r-1)^{\rho}\,e^{-\rho r}
  \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_{n}\,(2\rho + 1)_{n}\,
       U\!\bigl(s + 1 + 2\rho + n,\; 2s + 1,\; 2\rho r\bigr),
  \label{eq:lv-ansatz}
\end{equation}
where $(z)_{n} = \Gamma(z+n)/\Gamma(z)$ is the Pochhammer symbol
and $U(\cdot,\cdot,\cdot)$ is the irregular confluent
hypergeometric function. The three factors in front of the sum
encode the QNM asymptotics analytically:
$(r-1)^{\rho} = (r-1)^{-i\omega}$ reproduces the ingoing
$e^{-i\omega r_{\!*}}$ behaviour at the horizon
($r_{\!*} \to -\infty$), and the large-$r$ asymptotics of $U$,
\begin{equation}
  U(a, b, z) \;\sim\; z^{-a}
  \qquad (|z| \to \infty,\; \arg z\;\text{fixed}),
  \label{eq:lv-U-asymp}
\end{equation}
combine with $r^{1+s} e^{-\rho r}$ to yield the outgoing form
$\psi(r) \sim r^{-1-s+2i\omega} e^{i\omega r}$ at infinity, in
agreement with the QNM boundary condition
Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-bc}. The QNM problem therefore reduces to
fixing the coefficients $a_{n}$ so that the series in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-ansatz} is convergent for all $r \ge 1$, in
particular at $r = \infty$, which by Pincherle's theorem
\citep{KonoplyaZhidenko2011} is equivalent to the
continued-fraction equation derived below.

\subsection{Three-term recurrence (explicit Schwarzschild form)}

Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-ansatz} into Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rw},
expanding the contiguous-function relations of $U$ and matching
coefficients of $U(s + 1 + 2\rho + n, 2s + 1, 2\rho r)$ at fixed
$n$ gives the three-term recurrence
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \alpha_{0}\,a_{1} + \beta_{0}\,a_{0} &= 0,
      \quad a_{n<0} = 0,\\
    \alpha_{n}\,a_{n+1}
    + \beta_{n}\,a_{n}
    + \gamma_{n}\,a_{n-1} &= 0,
    \qquad n \ge 1,
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:lv-rec}
\end{equation}
with $a_{0}$ arbitrary and the coefficients given explicitly by
\citep[Eqs.~(104)--(105)]{Nollert1993}
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\;
  \begin{aligned}
    \alpha_{n} &= (n+1)\,(n + 2\rho + 1),\\[2pt]
    \beta_{n}  &= -\bigl[\,
                   2 n^{2} + (8\rho + 2)\,n
                   + 8\rho^{2} + 4\rho
                   + \ell(\ell+1) + (1 - s^{2})\,\bigr],\\[2pt]
    \gamma_{n} &= n^{2} + 4\rho\,n + 4\rho^{2} - s^{2}.
  \end{aligned}
  \;}
  \label{eq:lv-coeffs}
\end{equation}
Specialising to $s = 2$ and $\ell = 2$, used throughout this paper,
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    \alpha_{n} &= (n+1)\,(n + 1 - 2 i\omega),\\
    \beta_{n}  &= -\bigl[\,
                   2 n^{2} + (2 - 16 i \omega)\,n
                   - 8 \omega^{2} - 4 i \omega + 3\,\bigr],\\
    \gamma_{n} &= n^{2} - 4 i \omega\,n - 4 \omega^{2} - 4,
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:lv-coeffs-l2}
\end{equation}
where we used $\rho = -i\omega$, so $2\rho = -2i\omega$,
$8\rho = -8i\omega$, $4\rho = -4i\omega$, $8\rho^{2} = -8\omega^{2}$,
$4\rho^{2} = -4\omega^{2}$, $\ell(\ell+1) = 6$, $s^{2} = 4$, and
$\ell(\ell+1) + (1 - s^{2}) = 6 + (1 - 4) = 3$.
The Regge--Wheeler ($s = 2$) and Zerilli potentials produce
identical $\alpha_{n}, \gamma_{n}$ and identical $\beta_{n}$ at
fixed $\ell$, hence the same QNM spectrum
\citep{Chandrasekhar1975,ReggeWheeler1957,Moncrief1974}; the
two potentials are intertwined by a first-order Darboux
transformation (Chandrasekhar isospectrality).

\subsection{Asymptotic analysis of the recurrence}

For $n \to \infty$, the leading behaviour of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-coeffs} is
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_{n} \sim n^{2},
  \qquad
  \beta_{n} \sim -2 n^{2},
  \qquad
  \gamma_{n} \sim n^{2},
  \label{eq:lv-asy-coef}
\end{equation}
so the recurrence Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rec} becomes, in this limit,
$a_{n+1} - 2 a_{n} + a_{n-1} \approx 0$ at leading order, with
characteristic roots $t = 1$ (a double root). Inserting the
$O(n^{-1/2})$ subleading terms into the indicial equation gives
\begin{equation}
  t_{\pm} \;=\; 1 \pm \sqrt{\frac{-2 i \omega}{n}}
              + O(n^{-1}),
  \qquad
  \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_{n}} \;\sim\; t_{\pm},
  \label{eq:lv-poincare}
\end{equation}
i.e.\ Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rec} is a Birkhoff-irregular three-term
recurrence with two linearly independent asymptotic solutions
$a_{n}^{(\pm)} \sim n^{-1+2\rho} t_{\pm}^{n}$. Only the
\emph{minimal} branch $t_{-}$ corresponds to a series
$\sum a_{n}$ that converges for $r \to \infty$ and therefore to
a QNM solution of Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rec} with the boundary
condition Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-bc}. Pincherle's theorem fixes the
condition that selects this minimal branch, and is the content
of the next subsection.

\subsection{Continued-fraction equation (Pincherle)}

Define the ratios $r_{n} \equiv a_{n+1}/a_{n}$. Dividing
Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rec} by $a_{n}$ gives
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_{n}\,r_{n}
  + \beta_{n}
  + \gamma_{n}\,r_{n-1}^{-1}
  \;=\; 0
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  r_{n} \;=\;
  -\,\frac{\gamma_{n+1}/\alpha_{n}}
         {\beta_{n+1}/\alpha_{n+1}\bigl/\,\,\;
          + r_{n+1}}\,,
  \label{eq:lv-rn-step}
\end{equation}
where the second form follows from Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rec} shifted
$n \to n+1$. Iterating Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rn-step} downward gives
\begin{equation}
  r_{0} \;=\; \frac{a_{1}}{a_{0}}
  \;=\; -\cfrac{\gamma_{1}}{\beta_{1}
       - \cfrac{\alpha_{1}\gamma_{2}}{\beta_{2}
         - \cfrac{\alpha_{2}\gamma_{3}}{\beta_{3}
           - \cfrac{\alpha_{3}\gamma_{4}}{\beta_{4} - \cdots}}}},
  \label{eq:lv-cf-rhs}
\end{equation}
and the $n=0$ row of Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rec} fixes the same ratio
algebraically as
\begin{equation}
  r_{0} \;=\; \frac{a_{1}}{a_{0}}
        \;=\; -\frac{\beta_{0}}{\alpha_{0}}.
  \label{eq:lv-cf-lhs}
\end{equation}
Pincherle's theorem \citep{KonoplyaZhidenko2011} states that
Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-cf-rhs} converges to the ratio of the minimal
solution of Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rec}; equating
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:lv-cf-lhs} and~\eqref{eq:lv-cf-rhs} therefore
yields the continued-fraction equation
\begin{equation}
  0 \;=\; \beta_{0}
  - \cfrac{\alpha_{0}\gamma_{1}}{\beta_{1}
       - \cfrac{\alpha_{1}\gamma_{2}}{\beta_{2}
         - \cfrac{\alpha_{2}\gamma_{3}}{\beta_{3}
           - \cfrac{\alpha_{3}\gamma_{4}}{\beta_{4} - \cdots}}}},
  \label{eq:lv-cf}
\end{equation}
whose roots in the complex $\omega$ plane are the QNM frequencies.

\subsection{$n$-th inversion for overtones}

Numerical evaluation of Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-cf} converges
geometrically for the fundamental mode but loses precision for
higher overtones, because the $n$-th overtone is the
$(n{+}1)$-th-stable root rather than the most stable one of the
truncated continued fraction. The fix is to invert the recurrence
$n$ times before truncation. Algebraically, replacing the
backward iteration in Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-rn-step} by an explicit
forward iteration starting from $r_{n-1}$ gives, after $n$
shifts and equating the result with Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-cf},
\begin{equation}
  \beta_{n} - \cfrac{\alpha_{n-1}\gamma_{n}}{\beta_{n-1}
    - \cfrac{\alpha_{n-2}\gamma_{n-1}}{\beta_{n-2} - \cdots
      - \cfrac{\alpha_{0}\gamma_{1}}{\beta_{0}}}}
  \;=\;
  \cfrac{\alpha_{n}\gamma_{n+1}}{\beta_{n+1}
    - \cfrac{\alpha_{n+1}\gamma_{n+2}}{\beta_{n+2} - \cdots}},
  \label{eq:lv-inv}
\end{equation}
which is algebraically equivalent to Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-cf} but
numerically isolates the $n$-th overtone as the most stable root
of the truncated equation, allowing reliable extraction up to
$n \sim 100$ for Schwarzschild
\citep{Leaver1985,Nollert1993,KonoplyaZhidenko2011}.

\subsection{Numerical evaluation: modified Lentz and Newton--Raphson}

Denote the truncated left-hand side of Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-cf} (or
of its $n$-th inversion Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-inv}) by
$F_N(\omega)$, where $N$ is the truncation depth. The continued
fraction is evaluated by the modified Lentz algorithm: start from
$f_0 = \beta_0$, $C_0 = f_0$, $D_0 = 0$ and iterate, for
$j = 1, 2, \dots, N$,
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    D_j &= \bigl(\beta_j - \alpha_{j-1}\gamma_j\,D_{j-1}\bigr)^{-1}, &
    C_j &= \beta_j - \frac{\alpha_{j-1}\gamma_j}{C_{j-1}}, \\
    \Delta_j &= C_j\,D_j, &
    f_j &= f_{j-1}\,\Delta_j,
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:lv-lentz}
\end{equation}
with the standard guard $D_j \to \varepsilon$ if any denominator
falls below $|\varepsilon| = 10^{-30}$
\citep{KonoplyaZhidenko2011}. The iteration is stopped at the
first $j$ for which $|\Delta_j - 1| < 10^{-14}$, which gives
$F_N(\omega) = f_N$. The QNM frequency is then a complex root
of $F_N(\omega) = 0$, located by Newton--Raphson iteration
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{(k+1)} = \omega_{(k)} - \frac{F_N(\omega_{(k)})}
                    {F_N'(\omega_{(k)})},
  \qquad
  F_N'(\omega) \approx
    \frac{F_N(\omega + h) - F_N(\omega - h)}{2 h},
  \label{eq:lv-newton}
\end{equation}
with complex step $h = 10^{-8}$ and convergence tolerance
$|\omega_{(k+1)} - \omega_{(k)}| < 10^{-12}$. For the
fundamental mode the seed $\omega_{(0)}$ is taken from the
sixth-order WKB estimate of \citet{IyerWill1987}; for the first
overtone the seed is the fundamental shifted by
$\Delta\omega = -i\,(2 n + 1)/(4 r_{\rm ph})$ along the imaginary
axis, with $r_{\rm ph} = 3M$ the photon-sphere radius (the
asymptotic-overtone spacing of
\citealp{SchutzWill1985,IyerWill1987}). Convergence of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-newton} is quadratic in the immediate
neighbourhood of each root and typically requires fewer than ten
iterations from the WKB seed.

\subsection{Reference frequencies used in this paper}

We solve Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-cf} for $n = 0$ and Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-inv}
for $n = 1$ at $\ell = 2$, $s = 2$, with both continued fractions
truncated at depth $N = 200$, evaluated by the Lentz recursion of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-lentz} and bracketed by the Newton--Raphson
iteration of Eq.~\eqref{eq:lv-newton}. The truncation depth is
chosen empirically: doubling $N$ from $100$ to $200$ shifts
$M\omega_0$ by less than $10^{-10}$ and $M\omega_1$ by less than
$10^{-7}$, well below the four significant figures used in
benchmarking. The result is cross-checked against the public
tables of \citet{Berti2009review} and agrees to all quoted
digits,
\begin{equation}
  M\omega_0 = 0.3737 - 0.0890\,i,
  \qquad
  M\omega_1 = 0.3467 - 0.2739\,i,
  \label{eq:lv-values}
\end{equation}
giving the damping times $\tau_0/M = 1/|\Im(M\omega_0)| = 11.241$
and $\tau_1/M = 1/|\Im(M\omega_1)| = 3.6508$. These are the
reference values quoted in Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-ref} and used as
ground truth for every percentage-error figure in
Sec.~\ref{sec:results}.

\section{Derivation of the Regge--Wheeler and Zerilli equations}
\label{app:zerilli-derivation}

This appendix gives a self-contained derivation of the master
equation Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} and the Zerilli potential
Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli} that the PINN of
Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn} solves. The derivation follows the
standard route of \citet{ReggeWheeler1957} for the axial
(odd-parity) sector and \citet{Zerilli1970} for the polar
(even-parity) sector, with the gauge-invariant reformulation of
\citet{Moncrief1974}; the algebraic conventions match those
reviewed in \citet{Berti2009review}. We adopt geometric units
$G = c = 1$ and the metric signature $(-,+,+,+)$.

\subsection{Schwarzschild background and linearised Einstein
equations}
\label{sec:rw-background}

The exterior Schwarzschild geometry of mass $M$ is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{d}s^{2}_{(0)} = -f(r)\,\mathrm{d}t^{2}
       + f(r)^{-1}\,\mathrm{d}r^{2}
       + r^{2}\bigl(\mathrm{d}\theta^{2}
                    + \sin^{2}\!\theta\,\mathrm{d}\phi^{2}\bigr),
  \qquad
  f(r) \equiv 1 - \frac{2M}{r},
  \label{eq:schw-line}
\end{equation}
on the manifold $r > 2M$. We write the perturbed metric as
\begin{equation}
  g_{\mu\nu}(t,r,\theta,\phi)
  = g_{\mu\nu}^{(0)}(r,\theta)
  + h_{\mu\nu}(t,r,\theta,\phi),
  \qquad |h_{\mu\nu}| \ll |g_{\mu\nu}^{(0)}|,
  \label{eq:perturbation}
\end{equation}
and demand that $g_{\mu\nu}$ satisfy the vacuum Einstein equations
$R_{\mu\nu}[g] = 0$ to first order in $h$. Since the background is
already Ricci-flat, the linear perturbation must satisfy
\begin{equation}
  \delta R_{\mu\nu}[h]
  \;=\;
  -\tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(
    \nabla^{\alpha}\nabla_{\alpha} h_{\mu\nu}
    + \nabla_{\mu}\nabla_{\nu} h^{\alpha}{}_{\alpha}
    - \nabla^{\alpha}\nabla_{\mu} h_{\alpha\nu}
    - \nabla^{\alpha}\nabla_{\nu} h_{\alpha\mu}\bigr)
  \;=\; 0,
  \label{eq:dR-vacuum}
\end{equation}
with all covariant derivatives taken with respect to the
background of Eq.~\eqref{eq:schw-line}. The remainder of this
appendix solves Eq.~\eqref{eq:dR-vacuum} mode by mode in a
spherical-harmonic basis.

\subsection{Tensor spherical harmonic decomposition}
\label{sec:rw-tensor-harmonics}

Spherical symmetry of the background allows a separation of
variables. Each component of $h_{\mu\nu}$ is expanded in the
ten tensor spherical harmonics of \citet{ReggeWheeler1957}, which
split under spatial parity into seven \emph{polar} (even, parity
$(-1)^{\ell}$) and three \emph{axial} (odd, parity
$(-1)^{\ell+1}$) basis tensors at each $(\ell,m)$. With
$Y_{\ell m}(\theta,\phi)$ the standard scalar spherical harmonics,
the axial perturbation in the original \citet{ReggeWheeler1957}
parametrisation is
\begin{equation}
  h_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{axial},\ell m}(t,r,\theta,\phi)
  = h_{0}(t,r)\,a_{\mu\nu}^{(0),\ell m}(\theta,\phi)
  + h_{1}(t,r)\,a_{\mu\nu}^{(1),\ell m}(\theta,\phi)
  + h_{2}(t,r)\,a_{\mu\nu}^{(2),\ell m}(\theta,\phi),
  \label{eq:axial-h}
\end{equation}
with the three angular basis tensors
$a_{\mu\nu}^{(0),(1),(2)}$ given explicitly by Eq.~(10) of
\citet{ReggeWheeler1957}; their support is in the
$(t,\theta), (t,\phi), (r,\theta), (r,\phi)$ blocks for
$a^{(0)}, a^{(1)}$ and in the
$(\theta,\theta), (\theta,\phi), (\phi,\phi)$ angular block for
$a^{(2)}$. The polar perturbation is parametrised analogously by
seven radial functions $H_{0},H_{1},H_{2},h_{0}^{\mathrm{e}},
h_{1}^{\mathrm{e}}, K, G$ of $(t,r)$ multiplying the seven polar
basis tensors; the explicit matrix is given by
\citet[Eq.~(13)]{ReggeWheeler1957} and reproduced in
\citet[Sec.~II]{Zerilli1970}. The angular eigenvalue that appears
throughout is
\begin{equation}
  \nabla^{2}_{S^{2}} Y_{\ell m}
  = -\ell(\ell+1)\,Y_{\ell m},
  \qquad
  n \equiv \tfrac{1}{2}(\ell-1)(\ell+2)
        = \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(\ell^{2}+\ell-2\bigr),
  \label{eq:n-def}
\end{equation}
so that the angular Laplacian eigenvalue $\ell(\ell+1) = 2(n+1)$.
The axial and polar sectors decouple at the linearised level
because Eq.~\eqref{eq:dR-vacuum} preserves parity, and modes of
different $(\ell,m)$ decouple by orthogonality of the spherical
harmonics. The two sectors can therefore be treated independently
at fixed $(\ell,m)$.

\subsection{Regge--Wheeler gauge}
\label{sec:rw-gauge}

The first-order metric perturbation transforms under
infinitesimal coordinate changes
$x^{\mu} \to x^{\mu} + \xi^{\mu}$ as
\begin{equation}
  h_{\mu\nu} \;\to\; h_{\mu\nu}
   - \nabla_{\mu}\xi_{\nu} - \nabla_{\nu}\xi_{\mu},
  \label{eq:gauge-transf}
\end{equation}
and the four components of $\xi_{\mu}$ split into one axial and
three polar pieces in the harmonic decomposition. For
$\ell \ge 2$ this gauge freedom can be used to set
\begin{equation}
  h_{2} = 0
  \quad\text{(axial)},
  \qquad
  h_{0}^{\mathrm{e}} = h_{1}^{\mathrm{e}} = G = 0
  \quad\text{(polar)},
  \label{eq:rw-gauge}
\end{equation}
which is the \emph{Regge--Wheeler gauge}
\citep{ReggeWheeler1957}. The axial sector is then described by
the two functions $(h_{0}, h_{1})(t,r)$ and the polar sector by
the four functions $(H_{0}, H_{1}, H_{2}, K)(t,r)$. The
$\ell = 0,1$ modes carry no dynamical degrees of freedom (they
correspond to mass and angular-momentum perturbations, gauged
away to give shifts in the asymptotic mass and Kerr-like
rotation \citep{Zerilli1970}) and are excluded from what follows.

\subsection{Axial sector: Regge--Wheeler equation}
\label{sec:rw-axial-derivation}

\input{sections/axial_projection}

Following the original derivation of \citet{ReggeWheeler1957} and
adopting the form recorded by
\citet[App.~A, Eqs.~(A22)--(A24)]{Sago2003} as their Z\,(C6a--c),
the three independent radial equations derived above read
\begin{align}
  &\partial_{r}^{2} h_{0}
   - \partial_{t}\partial_{r} h_{1}
   - \frac{2}{r}\,\partial_{t} h_{1}
   + \!\left[\frac{4M}{r^{2}} - \frac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r}\right]\!
       \frac{h_{0}}{\,r-2M\,} \;=\; 0,
  \label{eq:rw-A}\\[2pt]
  &\partial_{t}^{2} h_{1}
   - \partial_{t}\partial_{r} h_{0}
   + \frac{2}{r}\,\partial_{t} h_{0}
   + \frac{(\ell-1)(\ell+2)\,f(r)}{r^{2}}\, h_{1} \;=\; 0,
  \label{eq:rw-B}\\[2pt]
  &\partial_{r}\!\bigl[f(r)\, h_{1}\bigr]
   - f(r)^{-1}\,\partial_{t} h_{0} \;=\; 0.
  \label{eq:rw-C}
\end{align}
Equation \eqref{eq:rw-C} is the Hamiltonian constraint of the
axial sector and is preserved by the time evolution generated by
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:rw-A}--\eqref{eq:rw-B} as a consequence of the
twice-contracted Bianchi identity. Using the tortoise coordinate
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:tortoise}, with
$\partial_{r_{\!*}} = f\,\partial_{r}$, the constraint
\eqref{eq:rw-C} can be written as
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{r_{\!*}}\!\bigl[f(r)\, h_{1}\bigr]
  \;=\; \partial_{t} h_{0}.
  \label{eq:rw-C-tortoise}
\end{equation}

Define the \emph{Regge--Wheeler function}
\begin{equation}
  Q(t,r) \;\equiv\; \frac{f(r)}{r}\,h_{1}(t,r),
  \label{eq:rw-Q}
\end{equation}
so that $f h_{1} = r Q$ and $h_{1} = rQ/f$. The constraint
\eqref{eq:rw-C-tortoise} then reads
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{t} h_{0} \;=\; \partial_{r_{\!*}}(rQ)
                   \;=\; f\,Q + r\,\partial_{r_{\!*}} Q,
  \label{eq:rw-Q-constraint}
\end{equation}
where the second equality uses
$\partial_{r_{\!*}} r = f$. Apply $\partial_{r_{\!*}}$ to
Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-Q-constraint} and commute it with $\partial_{t}$
on the right:
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{r_{\!*}}^{2}(rQ)
  \;=\; \partial_{r_{\!*}} \partial_{t} h_{0}
  \;=\; f\,\partial_{t} \partial_{r} h_{0}.
  \label{eq:rw-step1}
\end{equation}
Now use Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-B}, solved for $\partial_{t}\partial_{r}h_{0}$,
to eliminate the cross derivative on the right of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-step1}:
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{r_{\!*}}^{2}(rQ)
  \;=\; f\,\partial_{t}^{2} h_{1}
      + \frac{2f}{r}\,\partial_{t} h_{0}
      + \frac{(\ell-1)(\ell+2)\,f^{2}}{r^{2}}\, h_{1}.
  \label{eq:rw-step2}
\end{equation}
Substitute $h_{1} = rQ/f$ and
$\partial_{t} h_{0} = fQ + r\partial_{r_{\!*}} Q$ from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-Q-constraint}; the three terms on the right of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-step2} become
\begin{align}
  f\,\partial_{t}^{2} h_{1}
    &= r\,\partial_{t}^{2} Q,
  \label{eq:rw-piece1}\\
  \frac{2f}{r}\,\partial_{t} h_{0}
    &= \frac{2f^{2}}{r}\,Q + 2f\,\partial_{r_{\!*}} Q,
  \label{eq:rw-piece2}\\
  \frac{(\ell-1)(\ell+2)\,f^{2}}{r^{2}}\, h_{1}
    &= \frac{(\ell-1)(\ell+2)\,f}{r}\,Q.
  \label{eq:rw-piece3}
\end{align}
The left-hand side of Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-step2} expands as
\begin{align}
  \partial_{r_{\!*}}^{2}(rQ)
    &= \partial_{r_{\!*}}\!\bigl[f Q + r\,\partial_{r_{\!*}} Q\bigr]
  \nonumber\\
    &= (\partial_{r_{\!*}} f)\,Q
       + 2 f\,\partial_{r_{\!*}} Q
       + r\,\partial_{r_{\!*}}^{2} Q,
  \label{eq:rw-lhs-expand}
\end{align}
where $\partial_{r_{\!*}} f = f f' = f \cdot (2M/r^{2})
                              = 2Mf/r^{2}$.
Inserting Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-lhs-expand} and
Eqs.~\eqref{eq:rw-piece1}--\eqref{eq:rw-piece3} into
Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-step2} gives
\begin{equation}
  \frac{2Mf}{r^{2}}\,Q
  + 2f\,\partial_{r_{\!*}} Q
  + r\,\partial_{r_{\!*}}^{2} Q
  =
  r\,\partial_{t}^{2} Q
  + \frac{2 f^{2}}{r}\,Q
  + 2f\,\partial_{r_{\!*}} Q
  + \frac{(\ell-1)(\ell+2)\,f}{r}\,Q.
  \label{eq:rw-master-raw}
\end{equation}
The two $2f\,\partial_{r_{\!*}} Q$ terms cancel. Dividing through
by $r$ and rearranging yields
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{t}^{2} Q - \partial_{r_{\!*}}^{2} Q
  + \!\left[\frac{(\ell-1)(\ell+2)\,f}{r^{2}}
            + \frac{2 f^{2}}{r^{2}}
            - \frac{2Mf}{r^{3}}\right]\! Q
  \;=\; 0.
  \label{eq:rw-master-raw2}
\end{equation}
The bracketed potential simplifies algebraically. Using
$2f = 2 - 4M/r$, the leading terms inside the bracket combine as
\begin{equation}
  (\ell-1)(\ell+2) + 2f
  = (\ell^{2}+\ell-2) + 2 - \tfrac{4M}{r}
  = \ell(\ell+1) - \tfrac{4M}{r},
  \label{eq:rw-bracket}
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}
  \frac{(\ell-1)(\ell+2)\,f}{r^{2}}
  + \frac{2 f^{2}}{r^{2}}
  - \frac{2Mf}{r^{3}}
  = f\,\frac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r^{2}}
   - f\,\frac{4M}{r^{3}}
   - \frac{2Mf}{r^{3}}
  = f\!\left[\frac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r^{2}} - \frac{6M}{r^{3}}\right].
  \label{eq:rw-V-collapse}
\end{equation}
Substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-V-collapse} into
Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-master-raw2} gives the Regge--Wheeler equation
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\;
  \partial_{t}^{2} Q - \partial_{r_{\!*}}^{2} Q
  + V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{RW}}(r)\,Q \;=\; 0,
  \qquad
  V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{RW}}(r)
  = f(r)\!\left[\frac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r^{2}} - \frac{6M}{r^{3}}\right],
  \;}
  \label{eq:rw-eqn}
\end{equation}
which agrees with Eq.~(7) of \citet{ReggeWheeler1957}. We have not
yet used Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-A}; it is the
Bianchi-implied consequence of Eqs.~\eqref{eq:rw-B}
and~\eqref{eq:rw-C} and serves as an algebraic identity once
Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-eqn} is satisfied, which provides an internal
consistency check on the reduction.

\subsection{Polar sector: Zerilli equation}
\label{sec:rw-polar-derivation}

The polar sector is algebraically heavier because four
RW-gauge functions $(H_{0}, H_{1}, H_{2}, K)$ are subject to
seven nontrivial components of $\delta R_{\mu\nu} = 0$, organised
by \citet{Zerilli1970}. After algebraic elimination using the
constraint components $\delta R_{tr} = \delta R_{tt} = 0$, the
system reduces to two coupled first-order partial differential
equations in $r$ and $t$ for two independent linear combinations
of $K$ and $H_{1}$. \citet{Moncrief1974} introduced the
gauge-invariant combination
\begin{equation}
  \psi_{\mathrm{Z}}(t,r) \;\equiv\;
  \frac{r}{n+1}\!\left[
    K(t,r)
    + \frac{f(r)}{n + 3M/r}
      \bigl(H_{2}(t,r) - r\,\partial_{r} K(t,r)\bigr)
  \right],
  \label{eq:moncrief-psi}
\end{equation}
with $n = (\ell-1)(\ell+2)/2$ as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:n-def}.
Eq.~\eqref{eq:moncrief-psi} is gauge-invariant in the sense that
its value is unchanged under the polar piece of the residual
gauge freedom of Eq.~\eqref{eq:gauge-transf} that survives
Eq.~\eqref{eq:rw-gauge}, so the same $\psi_{\mathrm{Z}}$ is
extracted in any gauge \citep{Moncrief1974}. Substituting
Eq.~\eqref{eq:moncrief-psi} into the polar reduction of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:dR-vacuum} and using the constraint components to
eliminate the remaining metric functions yields the
\emph{Zerilli equation}
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{t}^{2} \psi_{\mathrm{Z}}
  - \partial_{r_{\!*}}^{2} \psi_{\mathrm{Z}}
  + V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r)\,\psi_{\mathrm{Z}} \;=\; 0,
  \label{eq:zerilli-eqn-app}
\end{equation}
with the Zerilli potential
\begin{equation}
  V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r) \;=\;
  \frac{2 f(r)}{r^{3}}\,
  \frac{n^{2}(n+1)\,r^{3} + 3 n^{2} M r^{2}
        + 9 n M^{2} r + 9 M^{3}}{(n r + 3M)^{2}}.
  \label{eq:V-zerilli-app}
\end{equation}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli-app} agrees identically with
Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli} of the main text. The intermediate
elimination from the polar system to
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli-eqn-app} is algebraically lengthy and is
performed in full in \citet[Eqs.~(II.10)--(II.18)]{Zerilli1970}
and \citet[Sec.~II]{Moncrief1974}; we do not reproduce it here.
The two derivations agree on
Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli-app}, and a third independent check is
provided by the Chandrasekhar--Detweiler intertwining relation,
under which $V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{RW}}$ and $V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}$
are related by a first-order operator \citep{Berti2009review} and
share the same QNM spectrum.

\subsection{Verification of the potential}
\label{sec:rw-verify}

A non-trivial sanity check on Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli-app} comes
from its asymptotic behaviour at the horizon and at infinity, and
from its $\ell = 2$ specialisation used throughout the paper.

At the horizon, $f(r) \to 0$ as $r \to 2M$, and the rational
factor in Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli-app} is regular at $r = 2M$
because $n r + 3M = 2nM + 3M > 0$ for $\ell \ge 2$. Therefore
$V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r) \to 0$ as $r \to 2M$, that is, as
$x \to -\infty$ in the tortoise coordinate of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:tortoise}. The decay is exponential in $x$ because
$f \sim e^{x/(2M)}$ near the horizon, recovering the
$V \propto e^{x/(2M)}$ behaviour quoted in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-asymptotic}.

At infinity, $f \to 1$ and the leading behaviour of the rational
factor is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{n^{2}(n+1)\,r^{3} + 3 n^{2} M r^{2} + 9 n M^{2} r + 9 M^{3}}
       {(n r + 3 M)^{2}}
  \;=\;
  (n+1)\,r + O(1)
  \quad \text{as } r \to \infty,
  \label{eq:V-asym-r}
\end{equation}
so that
\begin{equation}
  V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r)
  \;\xrightarrow[r\to\infty]{}\;
  \frac{2(n+1)}{r^{2}}
  \;=\; \frac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r^{2}},
  \label{eq:V-asym-l}
\end{equation}
which is the centrifugal barrier of a free wave on a flat
$3{+}1$ background, as expected from the spherical decomposition
of Sec.~\ref{sec:rw-tensor-harmonics}. This matches the
$x \to +\infty$ limit recorded in Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-asymptotic}.

For the dipole multipole $\ell = 2$ used in this paper, the
parameter is $n = (1)(4)/2 = 2$, and
Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli-app} reduces to
\begin{equation}
  V_{2}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r)
  = \frac{2 f(r)}{r^{3}}\,
    \frac{12\,r^{3} + 12 M r^{2} + 18 M^{2} r + 9 M^{3}}
         {(2r + 3M)^{2}}.
  \label{eq:V2-explicit}
\end{equation}
Setting $M = 1$ and evaluating at the photon-sphere radius
$r = 3M = 3$ gives $f(3) = 1/3$, $2 r + 3 M = 9$, and
\begin{equation}
  V_{2}^{\mathrm{Z}}(3)
  = \frac{2/3}{27}\,
    \frac{12{\cdot}27 + 12{\cdot}9 + 18{\cdot}3 + 9}
         {81}
  = \frac{2}{81}\cdot \frac{324+108+54+9}{81}
  = \frac{2 \cdot 495}{81 \cdot 81}
  = \frac{990}{6561}
  \approx 0.151,
  \label{eq:V2-photon}
\end{equation}
which is finite, positive, and of the same order of magnitude as
the peak of $V_{2}^{\mathrm{Z}}$ near the photon sphere quoted by
\citet{Berti2009review}; the exact peak of the polar potential
lies slightly outside $r = 3M$ and is found by setting
$\mathrm{d}V_{2}^{\mathrm{Z}}/\mathrm{d}r = 0$. Combining
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli-eqn-app} with Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli-app}
and the identification $\Phi(t,x) \equiv \psi_{\mathrm{Z}}(t,r(x))$
recovers exactly the form
$\partial_{t}^{2} \Phi - \partial_{x}^{2} \Phi
  + V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}\,\Phi = 0$
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} in the main text, closing the
derivation.

applied to the forward PINN field
$\Phi_\theta(x_{\mathrm{obs}},t)$ on the QNM window
$t \in [10,\,50]\,M$ with model order $K = 6$, returns the six

$0.3737$) and $\tau = 7.84\,M$ ($30.3\%$ below $11.241\,M$); this is

($|A_6| = 9.2\times 10^{-41}$). Both candidate amplitudes lie more than forty orders of magnitude below the
signal scale ($\Phi \sim 10^{-1}$), so the entire spectrum is in
the numerical-noise floor of the Hankel decomposition and any
amplitude-based selection is unstable. Two further components
($k=2,4$) sit at the origin with $\tau \to \infty$; component $k=1$
is a degenerate machine-zero pole at $\tau = 0.025\,M$ with $|A_1|=0$,
and $k=3$ has negative $\omega$ with sub-Planckian amplitude. All
four are unphysical numerical artefacts. ESPRIT is
therefore reported only here, in the appendix, and is not used in
any primary number of Sec.~\ref{sec:results}.
