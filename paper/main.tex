\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Time-domain quasi-normal-mode (QNM) extraction is limited by the
accuracy of the underlying field solution and by fit-window
systematics that are rarely quantified. We benchmark
physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) and a hybrid surrogate
that trains a small Fourier neural operator (FNO) to correct a
coarse finite-difference (FD) solve, on the Schwarzschild
$\ell = 2$ master equations against Leaver's continued-fraction
values. A faithful, architecture-matched reproduction of the
most closely comparable prior study --- the only previous
time-domain PINN treatment of these equations
\citep{Patel2024pinn} --- reduces its reported field error by an
order of magnitude on both isospectral potentials;
residual-greedy sampling and a doubled quasi-Newton budget then
reach $0.58\%$ relative field error, from which the fundamental
QNM frequency is recovered to $0.029\%$ using a new plateau-scan
estimator that quantifies the fit-window systematic and flags
its own validity. The hybrid surrogate, trained once over
$1{,}000$ initial-data draws, serves any new draw at the cost of
one coarse solve plus one forward pass, reducing the population
mean-squared field error by a factor of $46$ at one
sixty-fourth of the fine-solve cost. Finally, the same
solver-correction design, retrained on the Teukolsky equation,
carries the approach to rotating (Kerr) black holes: across
spins $a/M \in [0, 0.95]$ it cuts the median field error by a
factor of $77$ and recovers the near-extremal fundamental
frequency to $0.38\%$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

The ringdown phase of a perturbed black hole carries a discrete
spectrum of complex frequencies, the quasi-normal modes (QNMs)
\citep{Vishveshwara1970}, fixed by the background geometry
alone and hence a direct spectroscopic probe of the spacetime
\citep{Berti2009review,KonoplyaZhidenko2011}. The ringdown
analysis of GW150914 \citep{LIGO2016ringdown} made QNM
spectroscopy an observational discipline: tests of the no-hair
conjecture and of strong-field general relativity turn on how
accurately the QNM frequencies are extracted and how reliably
the associated systematics are quantified
\citep{Berti2007ringdown,Isi2021priors}. In the frequency
domain, Leaver's continued-fraction method \citep{Leaver1985}
tabulates QNMs to arbitrary precision and calibrates every
other approach; in the time domain --- adopted here, mirroring
gravitational-wave data analysis
\citep{Buonanno2007ringdown,LIGO2016ringdown,Isi2021priors} ---
the master equation is integrated numerically and the QNMs are
read off the late-time waveform by fitting damped sinusoids
\citep{Berti2009review}.

The principal methodological issue in the time-domain pipeline
is the choice of fit window: the damped-sinusoid template is
misspecified outside the QNM regime, so the recovered
$(\omega,\tau)$ carry a window-dependent bias that no amount of
data removes and that existing analyses rarely quantify --- in
some cases comparable to the quoted statistical errors
\citep{Isi2021priors}.

Two limitations of the prior PINN-for-QNM literature
(Sec.~\ref{sec:bg-pinn}) motivate the present work:
\citet{Patel2024pinn}, the only prior PINN study of the
time-domain Zerilli problem, report a $28.06\%$ relative field
error that dominates their QNM error budget, with a fit window
fixed by hand; and every PINN treatment retrains from scratch
for each black hole, with no amortization across the parameter
space.

We address both points on the Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$ master
equations, evaluating three models under a single extraction
protocol. The architecture-matched reproduction of
\citet{Patel2024pinn} reduces their reported field errors by an
order of magnitude on both isospectral potentials; the
extensions below are reported on the Zerilli equation. An enhanced forward PINN --- doubled L-BFGS
budget and residual-greedy adaptive sampling --- reaches
$0.58\%$ relative field error, from which a new plateau-scan
estimator that reports its own fit-window systematic recovers
the fundamental frequency to $0.029\%$. Finally, a
hybrid surrogate, trained once across the initial-data
distribution, corrects a $1/64$-cost coarse FD solve with a
small FNO on a label-free, Richardson-extrapolated estimate of
its discretisation error, confined to the under-resolved
wavefronts by a parameter-free gradient gate; it then serves
any new draw at one coarse solve plus one forward pass, cutting
the population field MSE by $46\times$ with no fine-grid field
in its training loss.

The three models above all treat a Schwarzschild black hole,
where the metric perturbation separates into the
Regge--Wheeler and Zerilli wave equations. Astrophysical
remnants rotate, and rotation couples the radial and angular
sectors: the governing equation becomes the Teukolsky master
equation \citep{Teukolsky1973}, whose spectrum depends on the
spin. As a final test of whether the hybrid concept is tied to
a particular geometry, we retrain the surrogate on a family of
rotating (Kerr) black holes spanning dimensionless spins
$a/M \in [0, 0.95]$. Prior neural-network treatments of the
Kerr spectrum solve the frequency-domain eigenvalue problem
for isolated modes \citep{Luna2022teukolskypinn,
Cornell2022pinnqnm} and never produce a waveform; our
surrogate instead emits the full time-domain ringdown at null
infinity and, amortised once across spin, recovers the median
field to $0.78\%$ (a $77\times$ reduction over the coarse
prior) and the near-extremal fundamental frequency to
$0.38\%$.

A remark on scope. This is a controlled extraction benchmark
against known continued-fraction reference values, not an
observational pipeline: the fields are simulated, the ground
truth is known, and the objective is the accuracy with which
each model recovers it. Observer position and extraction window
are configuration parameters of the benchmark, fixed per
pipeline by a validity criterion evaluated on the FD reference
alone (the plateau flag of Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}), never by
proximity of any extracted value to the known answer.

The remainder of this section reviews the perturbation problem,
time-domain extraction, and the prior machine-learning
literature. Section~\ref{sec:methods} states the three
Schwarzschild models and their generalisation to Kerr,
together with the extraction protocol, Sec.~\ref{sec:results}
reports their results, Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses
them, and
Sec.~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes; hyperparameter tables and
supporting scans are collected in the appendices.
Table~\ref{tab:framework} summarises the framework: each model
produces an observer time series, which a single

\subsection{Schwarzschild QNMs and time-domain extraction}
\label{sec:bg-zerilli}

Throughout this paper we work with a non-rotating, uncharged,
asymptotically flat black hole of mass $M$, described by the
Schwarzschild line element
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{d}s^{2} \;=\;
  -f(r)\,\mathrm{d}t^{2}
  + f(r)^{-1}\,\mathrm{d}r^{2}
  + r^{2}\bigl(\mathrm{d}\theta^{2}
              + \sin^{2}\!\theta\,\mathrm{d}\varphi^{2}\bigr),
  \qquad f(r) \;=\; 1 - \frac{2M}{r},
  \label{eq:schwarzschild}
\end{equation}
in geometric units $G = c = 1$. The horizon is at $r = 2M$ and
spatial infinity at $r \to \infty$.

The ringdown is described by linear perturbations
$g_{\mu\nu} = g_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{Schw}} + h_{\mu\nu}$ of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:schwarzschild}, expanded in tensor spherical
harmonics,
\begin{equation}
  h_{\mu\nu}(t,r,\theta,\varphi)
  \;=\; \sum_{\ell, m}
  h_{\mu\nu}^{(\ell m)}(t,r)\;Y^{(\ell m)}_{\mu\nu}(\theta,\varphi),
  \label{eq:harmonic-expansion}
\end{equation}
which decouples the linearised vacuum equations between
multipoles $(\ell, m)$ and, under parity, between the
\emph{axial} (odd) and \emph{polar} (even) sectors
\citep{ReggeWheeler1957,Berti2009review}. Each sector reduces
to a single gauge-invariant master scalar $\Phi(t,r)$ --- for
the polar sector via the Moncrief reduction
\citep{Moncrief1974} --- from which the metric perturbation is
reconstructed as
\begin{equation}
  h_{\mu\nu}(t,r,\theta,\varphi)
  \;=\; \frac{1}{r}\sum_{\ell, m}
  \Phi^{(\ell m)}(t,r)\;
  T^{(\ell m)}_{\mu\nu}(\theta,\varphi)
  \label{eq:moncrief-ansatz}
\end{equation}
with $T^{(\ell m)}_{\mu\nu}$ a known tensor-harmonic template.
The axial reduction yields the Regge--Wheeler equation and the
polar reduction the Zerilli equation
\citep{Zerilli1970,Chandrasekhar1975}; both take the form
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{t}^{2}\Phi
  - \partial_{x}^{2}\Phi
  + V_{\ell}\bigl(r(x)\bigr)\,\Phi \;=\; 0,
  \label{eq:zerilli}
\end{equation}
in the tortoise coordinate $x$, defined by
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}r} \;=\; \frac{1}{f(r)}
  \;=\; \left(1 - \frac{2M}{r}\right)^{-1}
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  x \;=\; r + 2M\,\ln\!\left(\frac{r}{2M} - 1\right).
  \label{eq:tortoise}
\end{equation}
The substitution absorbs the metric factor $f(r)$, leaving the
flat-space d'Alembertian of Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}, and sends
the horizon to $x \to -\infty$, so the exterior $r > 2M$ covers
the full real line. The Zerilli potential for the polar sector
at multipole $\ell$ is
\begin{equation}
  V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r) \;=\;
  \frac{2 f(r)}{r^{3}}\,
  \frac{n^{2}(n+1)\,r^{3} + 3 n^{2} M r^{2}
        + 9 n M^{2} r + 9 M^{3}}{(n r + 3 M)^{2}},
  \qquad n \;\equiv\; \tfrac{1}{2}(\ell - 1)(\ell + 2),
  \label{eq:V-zerilli}
\end{equation}
and vanishes in both asymptotic regions,
\begin{equation}
  V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r(x)) \;\sim\;
  \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r^{2}}
      \;\xrightarrow[x\to+\infty]{}\; \dfrac{\ell(\ell+1)}{x^{2}} \;\to\; 0,
      & x \to +\infty,\\[2pt]
    \;\propto\; e^{x/(2M)}
      \;\xrightarrow[x\to-\infty]{}\; 0,
      & x \to -\infty,
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:V-asymptotic}
\end{equation}
so Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} is a wave equation on a finite
barrier peaked near the photon sphere $r \simeq 3M$
(Figs.~\ref{fig:bg-potential} and~\ref{fig:bg-tortoise}), with
free propagation on either side --- the barrier that gives the
ringdown its QNM structure. This paper studies the polar
$\ell = 2$ sector with $M = 1$ as the unit of mass and time;
the classical reduction to the master equations
\citep{ReggeWheeler1957,Zerilli1970,Moncrief1974,Chandrasekhar1975}
is used as stated, with a self-contained derivation reproduced
in the repository documentation.

A quasi-normal mode is a separated solution
$\Phi(t,x) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega t}\,\psi(x)$ of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}, which turns it into the
time-independent Schr\"odinger-like equation
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}^{2}\psi}{\mathrm{d}x^{2}}
  + \bigl(\omega^{2} - V_{\ell}(r(x))\bigr)\psi(x) \;=\; 0.
  \label{eq:zerilli-freq}
\end{equation}
In the asymptotic regions Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli-freq} has
plane-wave solutions $\mathrm{e}^{\pm\mathrm{i}\omega x}$, and
the QNM boundary conditions select modes purely ingoing at the
horizon and purely outgoing at infinity,
\begin{equation}
  \psi(x) \;\to\; \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega x}\;\;(x \to -\infty),
  \qquad
  \psi(x) \;\to\; \mathrm{e}^{+\mathrm{i}\omega x}\;\;(x \to +\infty).
  \label{eq:qnm-bc}
\end{equation}
Radiation leaves but never enters, so the operator is
non-Hermitian and the admissible frequencies form a discrete
complex spectrum \citep{Berti2009review,KonoplyaZhidenko2011},
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{n} \;=\; \omega_{n}^{R} - \mathrm{i}/\tau_{n},
  \qquad \omega_{n}^{R}, \tau_{n} > 0,
  \label{eq:qnm-omega}
\end{equation}
ordered by overtone index $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ in increasing
damping rate $1/\tau_n$: the real part $\omega_n^{R}$ is the
oscillation frequency and $\tau_n$ the damping time.

For Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$ the fundamental ($n = 0$) and
first overtone ($n = 1$) are
\begin{equation}
  M\omega_{0} = 0.3737, \quad \tau_{0}/M = 11.241,
  \qquad
  M\omega_{1} = 0.3467, \quad \tau_{1}/M = 3.651,
  \label{eq:qnm-ref}
\end{equation}
computed to arbitrary numerical precision by Leaver's
continued-fraction method \citep{Leaver1985} and tabulated by
\citet{Berti2009review}; every extraction reported in this
paper is benchmarked against them. The fundamental $\ell = 2$
mode dominates the observed ringdown of binary mergers and is
the primary spectroscopy target; the first overtone enters at
sufficient signal-to-noise ratio
\citep{Buonanno2007ringdown,LIGO2016ringdown,Isi2021priors}.
WKB expansions
\citep{SchutzWill1985,IyerWill1987,Konoplya2003wkb} and the
asymptotic-iteration method \citep{Cho2010aim} provide
semi-analytic alternatives \citep{Leaver1985,Nollert1993}.

\label{sec:bg-extraction}
A ringdown waveform $\Phi(\xq, t)$ measured at a fixed observer
is, between the prompt response and the late-time power-law
tail \citep{Berti2009review}, a sum of damped sinusoids
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(\xq, t) \;\approx\;
  \sum_{n} A_n\,\mathrm{e}^{-(t-t_0)/\tau_n}
  \cos\bigl(\omega_n^{R}(t-t_0) + \phi_n\bigr).
  \label{eq:qnm-template}
\end{equation}
Three families of methods recover $(\omega_n,\tau_n)$ from
samples of $\Phi$. Spectral methods locate $\omega_n^{R}$ as
peaks in the discrete Fourier transform and recover $\tau_n$
from the log-envelope slope at the waveform extrema,
\begin{equation}
  \log|\Phi(\xq, t_k)| \;\approx\;
  \log A_{0} - \frac{t_k - t_0}{\tau_{0}}.
  \label{eq:logline}
\end{equation}
Nonlinear least squares (NLS) fits Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-template}
directly, jointly optimising amplitudes, phases, frequencies
and decay times for a user-specified number of modes
\citep{Buonanno2007ringdown}. Subspace methods --- Prony
\citep{Berti2007prony}, matrix-pencil \citep{HuaSarkar1990} and
ESPRIT \citep{Roy1989esprit} --- model the uniformly sampled
analytic signal as a sum of complex exponentials,
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(\xq, k\Delta t)
  \;\approx\; \sum_{n=0}^{K-1} c_{n}\, z_{n}^{\,k},
  \qquad
  z_{n} \;=\; e^{(-1/\tau_{n} + \mathrm{i}\omega_{n}^{R})\Delta t},
  \label{eq:esprit-model}
\end{equation}
and recover the poles $z_{n}$ --- hence
$(\omega_{n}^{R},\tau_{n})$ --- from a generalised
eigendecomposition of a Hankel data matrix, without nonlinear
optimisation. All three families share the hyperparameter that
dominates the systematic error budget: the fit window
$[t_0, \tend]$. Existing analyses generally quote a single
fixed window \citep{Buonanno2007ringdown,LIGO2016ringdown};
Bayesian priors over the start time address the issue
probabilistically \citep{Isi2021priors}, but a deterministic
plateau-based diagnostic reported alongside the primary
estimate is not in common use. Methods M4 and M5
(Secs.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods}--\ref{sec:plateau}) implement
plateau scans over the start time alone and over both window
edges respectively, reporting the plateau standard deviation
$\sigma_\omega$ as a fit-window systematic uncertainty;
Fig.~\ref{fig:bg-plateau-cartoon} illustrates the shape of
$\omega(t_0)$ that motivates the construction.

\subsection{PINNs and prior PINN-for-QNM work}
\label{sec:bg-pinn}

A physics-informed neural network (PINN) \citep{Raissi2017} is
a mesh-free PDE solver: a network $\Phi_\theta(x,t)$ is trained
to minimise a weighted composite of mean-squared PDE,
initial-condition and boundary-condition residuals on sampled
collocation points, with all derivatives supplied exactly by
automatic differentiation \citep{Baydin2018ad}. How to balance
the residual weights is an active research area
\citep{Wang2021gradientpath,Wang2022ntk,McClenny2023sapinn};
the concrete seven-term loss used in this work is
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-fwd} of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-pinn-arch}). Training standardly proceeds in
two phases --- Adam \citep{Kingma2015adam}, then L-BFGS
\citep{Liu1989lbfgs} to exploit the near-quadratic local
landscape --- and two refinements are relevant here:
gradient-enhanced PINNs penalise the gradient of the PDE
residual so that it vanishes smoothly \citep{Yu2022gpinn}, and
residual-adaptive sampling concentrates collocation points
where the residual is currently largest \citep{Wu2023adaptive}
--- on wave-like problems, the moving front. DeepXDE
\citep{Lu2021deepxde} implements all of these as standard
options.

\label{sec:bg-prior}
Five prior works apply PINNs to the QNM problem, all in the
forward direction. \citet{Ovgun2021qnmnn} compute dS and AdS
QNMs from the shooting form of the eigenvalue equation;
\citet{Cornell2022pinnqnm} train the complex eigenvalue
$\omega$ alongside a frequency-domain network and reach
sub-permille accuracy on the fundamental $\ell = 2$ mode ---
an eigenvalue computation not directly comparable to a
time-domain post-hoc fit; \citet{Luna2022teukolskypinn} solve
the time-domain Teukolsky equation with hard-enforced boundary
conditions and sequential warm-starts in spin;
\citet{Luna2024qnm} treat frequency-domain QNMs in modified
gravity. The closest comparator is \citet{Patel2024pinn}, who
solve the time-domain Zerilli and Regge--Wheeler equations with
a fully-connected PINN; their reported field error and single
fixed-window fit are the two limitations identified at the
start of the introduction.
No prior PINN study reports a fit-window systematic on the
recovered $(\omega,\tau)$; the plateau criterion of
Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting} supplies one and doubles as a
deterministic validity flag.

\subsection{Neural operators and prior operator-learning work}
\label{sec:bg-fno}

Rather than training one network per PDE instance, a
\emph{neural operator}
\citep{Lu2021deeponet,Li2021fno,Kovachki2023neuralop} learns the
solution operator
$\mathcal{G}_{\theta}\colon \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{U}$ of a
parametric family, amortising every instance into a single
forward pass.
DeepONet \citep{Lu2021deeponet} factorises $\mathcal{G}_{\theta}$
into branch and trunk networks; the Fourier neural
operator (FNO) \citep{Li2021fno,Kovachki2023neuralop}
parameterises the operator by global convolutions in
Fourier space, making it discretisation-invariant and
well-suited to hyperbolic problems on regular grids.
Operator learning spans the Burgers, Darcy and Navier--Stokes
benchmarks
\citep{Li2021fno,Lu2021deeponet,Kovachki2023neuralop} but, to
our knowledge, has not been applied to the time-domain
black-hole perturbation problem.

A third strand keeps a classical solver in the loop and learns
only a correction to it: solver-in-the-loop training
\citep{Um2020solverloop} and learned corrections to
under-resolved fluid solvers \citep{Kochkov2021mlcfd} show that
a small network correcting a cheap classical solve can match a
much finer classical solution at a fraction of its cost. The
hybrid surrogate of Sec.~\ref{sec:hyb-method} belongs to this
strand, carrying the FNO of \citet{Li2021fno} as its residual
operator, with two distinctions: its training target is built
label-free by classical Richardson extrapolation
\citep{Richardson1911} of two coarse solves, so no fine-grid
field enters the loss, and a parameter-free gradient gate
confines the learned correction to the under-resolved
wavefronts.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We applied three models --- a faithful-reproduction and an
enhanced forward PINN, and a hybrid coarse-FD
+ neural-residual surrogate --- to the Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$
master equations, under a single post-hoc extraction protocol
benchmarked against Leaver's continued-fraction values. The
architecture-matched reproduction of \citet{Patel2024pinn}
reduces their reported field error by an order of magnitude on
both isospectral potentials, with damping-time errors symmetric
across the pair ($1.50\%$/$1.56\%$) as isospectrality requires;
doubling the L-BFGS budget and adding residual-greedy sampling
then reaches $0.58\%$, from which the fundamental mode is
recovered to $0.029\%$ in $\omega$ (M4) and $1.99\%$ in $\tau$ (M2).

The main methodological contribution is the plateau-based
extraction of Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}: the plateau standard
deviation $\sigma_\omega$ quantifies the fit-window systematic
and doubles as a deterministic validity flag, so that no quoted
number depends on a fixed-by-hand window.

The hybrid surrogate amortises what the PINNs pay per black
hole: trained once on $1{,}000$ draws, it runs a $1/64$-cost
coarse solve and repairs its discretisation error with a small
gated network trained on a label-free Richardson target,
serving any new draw at one coarse solve plus one forward pass
and cutting the population field MSE by $46\times$. At the
common observer the hybrid tracks the fine reference's frequency
to $0.15\%$; its damping time is limited by the network's
late-ring residual rather than the estimator, so its
contribution is field accuracy, not a sharper spectroscopy.

The hybrid concept proves geometry-agnostic. Retrained on
rotating (Kerr) black holes through the Teukolsky equation
across spins $a/M \in [0, 0.95]$, the same coarse-plus-residual
operator --- evolved on a hyperboloidal slice and corrected
with no fine-grid field in its loss --- cuts the median field
error by $77\times$ and recovers the near-extremal fundamental
frequency to $0.38\%$, an order of magnitude over the coarse
prior, exactly where that prior fails fastest. This
generalisation across a family of black holes is the structural
advantage of an amortised operator over a per-instance PINN,
which must be retrained from scratch for each spin.

Follow-up directions --- a Bayesian
posterior around the deterministic estimate and
overtone-exciting initial data --- are set out in
Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\section{Principled choice of the gradient-gate scale}\label{app:gate-scale}

The gate scale $s$ is the only free gate parameter. Sweeping it
label-free from the single trained model
(Table~\ref{tab:gate-scale-sweep}), the global RMSD varies by
$<2\%$ across a flat basin that contains the deployed
$s = 10^{-3}$. That value equals the $68$th percentile of the
prior-gradient distribution, giving the label-free rule
$s \simeq Q_{0.68}(|\nabla\,\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}|)$, a
function of the coarse prior alone. The re-gating identity is
derived in the repository documentation.
