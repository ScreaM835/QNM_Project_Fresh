\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Accurate numerical solutions of the Regge--Wheeler, Zerilli and
Teukolsky equations are required to extract quasinormal modes across
the black-hole and initial-data parameters considered here, but
repeated high-resolution evolutions are computationally expensive. We
first reproduce the Schwarzschild $\ell=2$ Regge--Wheeler and Zerilli
calculations of \citet{Patel2024pinn} using their physics-informed
neural network (PINN), then improve its collocation sampling and
optimisation. Because the forward PINN represents one
initial-boundary-value problem and must be retrained for a new
configuration, we introduce separate Schwarzschild and Kerr hybrid
Fourier neural operators
(hFNOs). At inference, each new configuration only requires one cheap
numerical-prior evolution, interpolation to the output grid and one
FNO forward pass, without per-configuration neural-network
retraining. Across $100$ Schwarzschild configurations, the hFNO
achieves a mean relative $L^2$ field error of $1.85\%$ against the
fine finite-difference reference. Its $k=4$ numerical-prior evolution
uses approximately one-sixteenth as many finite-difference point
updates as the fine-grid evolution, while the median fundamental QNM
frequency error against Leaver is $0.152\%$. Across $128$ Kerr
configurations, the hFNO for the $(\ell,m,n)=(4,2,0)$ radial
Teukolsky evolution achieves a median relative $L^2$ field error of
$0.78\%$ against the $N=801$ reference and a median frequency error
of $0.33\%$ against Leaver. These results establish the hybrid
framework as a route to accurate, lower-cost ringdown modelling
across black-hole parameter spaces.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
\enlargethispage{\baselineskip}

The ringdown phase of a perturbed black hole consists of damped
oscillations at a discrete set of complex frequencies, the
quasinormal modes (QNMs) \citep{Vishveshwara1970}. For each mode
of a Kerr remnant, the frequency and damping time are fixed by
the remnant mass and spin. The ringdown analysis of GW150914
opened black-hole spectroscopy as an observational programme
\citep{LIGO2016ringdown,Berti2009review,KonoplyaZhidenko2011},
in which multiple QNMs test the consistency of the remnant with
the Kerr prediction of general relativity \citep{Isi2019tests}.
Accurate time-domain extraction of even the fundamental mode is
therefore a necessary benchmark.

Leaver's continued-fraction method provides high-accuracy
reference values \citep{Leaver1985}; alternative frequency-domain
calculations include WKB, asymptotic-iteration and pseudospectral
methods \citep{Berti2009review,KonoplyaZhidenko2011}. We instead
work in the time domain, where the perturbation equation is
evolved and QNMs are fitted from the resulting waveform
\citep{Buonanno2007ringdown,LIGO2016ringdown,Isi2021priors}.

Time-domain extraction also depends on the interval over which the
late-time waveform is fitted. The damped-sinusoid template is most
appropriate only after the prompt response has decayed and before
late-time tails, boundary effects or numerical residuals dominate.
The recovered $(\omega,\tau)$ can therefore vary with the chosen
window. We quantify this dependence by scanning the fitting interval
and reporting the variation across its most stable contiguous region
\citep{Isi2021priors}.

Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) approximate differential
equations by training a differentiable ansatz against the PDE,
initial and boundary residuals at collocation points
\citep{Raissi2017,Baydin2018ad,Karniadakis2021piml}. Their accuracy
depends on the balance among loss terms and on the placement of
collocation points \citep{Wang2021gradientpath,Wu2023adaptive}.
Gradient-enhanced losses and residual-adaptive sampling address these
difficulties \citep{Yu2022gpinn,Wu2023adaptive} and directly motivate
the enhanced forward PINN examined here.

Neural QNM calculations have primarily treated the spectral problem.
Feed-forward networks have predicted QNMs of de Sitter and anti-de
Sitter black holes \citep{Ovgun2021qnmnn}, while
\citet{Cornell2022pinnqnm} formulate QNM recovery as a PINN
eigenvalue problem for non-rotating backgrounds. This approach was
extended to the coupled radial and angular Teukolsky equations, with
the frequency and angular separation constant determined simultaneously
\citep{Luna2022teukolskypinn}. Supervised and unsupervised PINNs have
also been compared for Regge--Wheeler and Teukolsky problems
\citep{Cornell2024rwteukolsky}. Beyond general relativity,
\citet{Luna2024qnm} compute QNMs for families of numerically generated
black-hole backgrounds through sequential, warm-started PINN solves.
These studies compute spectral quantities and eigenfunctions rather
than solving the corresponding time-domain initial-value problems.

\citet{Patel2024pinn} solve the Regge--Wheeler and Zerilli
initial-boundary-value problems with a PINN and extract QNMs from an
observer waveform. Their field error and methods define our
reproduction baseline. Unlike the spectral calculations above,
their PINN approximates solutions of the time-domain Regge--Wheeler
and Zerilli initial-boundary-value problems for one Schwarzschild
configuration.

Neural operators instead learn maps between function spaces across
parametric families \citep{Lu2021deeponet,Li2021fno,Kovachki2023neuralop}.
DeepONet represents this map through branch and trunk networks,
whereas the Fourier neural operator parameterises its integral kernel
in Fourier space \citep{Lu2021deeponet,Li2021fno}. A related line of
work combines learned corrections with classical solvers.
Solver-in-the-loop training learns stable corrections through a
differentiable numerical method \citep{Um2020solverloop}, while
learned corrections to under-resolved fluid simulations can recover
the accuracy of substantially finer calculations
\citep{Kochkov2021mlcfd}. These results motivate retaining a numerical
evolution as the numerical prior and learning a correction to its
discretisation error.

\citet{Patel2024pinn} report a $28.06\%$ relative $L^2$ field error
and use one manually selected fit window. Spectral PINNs compute QNM
frequencies and eigenfunctions rather than solving time-domain
initial-value problems. The Schwarzschild and Kerr problems considered
here depend on low-dimensional sets of black-hole and initial-data
parameters and have analytically specified boundary conditions.
QNM frequency and damping-time errors are measured against Leaver's
continued-fraction values.

The principal contribution of this work is a pair of hybrid Fourier
neural operators for the Schwarzschild Zerilli equation and a Kerr
radial Teukolsky evolution of the $(4,2,0)$ mode. The models are
trained separately across Schwarzschild mass and initial-data
parameters and across Kerr spin and initial-pulse parameters. At
inference, each new configuration requires one cheap numerical-prior
evolution, interpolation to the output grid and one FNO forward pass,
without per-configuration neural-network retraining. The Schwarzschild
$k=4$ prior uses approximately one-sixteenth as many finite-difference
point updates as the fine-grid evolution. We first reproduce and
improve the Schwarzschild PINN of \citet{Patel2024pinn}, then evaluate
the hFNO fields and extract QNMs using fit-window scans. Across the
tested Schwarzschild and Kerr parameter sets, the hFNOs achieve mean
and median relative $L^2$ field errors of $1.85\%$ and $0.78\%$
against their fine references.

\subsection{Schwarzschild perturbations and QNM spectrum}
\label{sec:bg-zerilli}

We first consider the non-rotating, uncharged, asymptotically
flat Schwarzschild black hole of mass $M$, described by the
line element
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{d}s^{2} \;=\;
  -f(r)\,\mathrm{d}t^{2}
  + f(r)^{-1}\,\mathrm{d}r^{2}
  + r^{2}\bigl(\mathrm{d}\theta^{2}
              + \sin^{2}\!\theta\,\mathrm{d}\varphi^{2}\bigr),
  \qquad f(r) \;=\; 1 - \frac{2M}{r},
  \label{eq:schwarzschild}
\end{equation}
in geometric units $G = c = 1$. The horizon is at $r = 2M$ and
spatial infinity at $r \to \infty$.

Writing
$g_{\mu\nu}=g_{\mu\nu}^{\mathrm{Schw}}+h_{\mu\nu}$ and
decomposing $h_{\mu\nu}$ into tensor spherical harmonics
decouples the linearised equations by multipole and parity
\citep{ReggeWheeler1957,Zerilli1970,Moncrief1974,
MartelPoisson2005}. For radiative multipoles $\ell\ge2$, the
axial and polar sectors admit gauge-invariant master functions
satisfying the Regge--Wheeler and Zerilli equations,
respectively \citep{Chandrasekhar1975,MartelPoisson2005}. Both
are wave equations of the form
\begin{equation}
  \partial_{t}^{2}\Phi
  - \partial_{x}^{2}\Phi
  + V_{\ell}\bigl(r(x)\bigr)\,\Phi \;=\; 0,
  \label{eq:zerilli}
\end{equation}
with different effective potentials $V_{\ell}$, in the tortoise
coordinate $x$, defined by
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}r} \;=\; \frac{1}{f(r)}
  \;=\; \left(1 - \frac{2M}{r}\right)^{-1}
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  x \;=\; r + 2M\,\ln\!\left(\frac{r}{2M} - 1\right).
  \label{eq:tortoise}
\end{equation}
The tortoise coordinate absorbs the metric factor $f(r)$ into
the radial derivative, leaving the flat-space d'Alembertian of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}, and sends the horizon to
$x \to -\infty$, so the exterior $r > 2M$ covers the full real
line. The Zerilli potential for the polar sector
at multipole $\ell$ is
\begin{equation}
  V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r) \;=\;
  \frac{2 f(r)}{r^{3}}\,
  \frac{n^{2}(n+1)\,r^{3} + 3 n^{2} M r^{2}
        + 9 n M^{2} r + 9 M^{3}}{(n r + 3 M)^{2}},
  \qquad n \;\equiv\; \tfrac{1}{2}(\ell - 1)(\ell + 2),
  \label{eq:V-zerilli}
\end{equation}
and vanishes in both asymptotic regions,
\begin{equation}
  V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}(r(x)) \;\sim\;
  \begin{cases}
    \dfrac{\ell(\ell+1)}{r^{2}}
      \;\xrightarrow[x\to+\infty]{}\; \dfrac{\ell(\ell+1)}{x^{2}} \;\to\; 0,
      & x \to +\infty,\\[2pt]
    \;\propto\; e^{x/(2M)}
      \;\xrightarrow[x\to-\infty]{}\; 0,
      & x \to -\infty,
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:V-asymptotic}
\end{equation}
Thus Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} describes scattering from a finite
potential barrier near the photon sphere
(Figs.~\ref{fig:bg-potential} and~\ref{fig:bg-tortoise}), with
asymptotically free propagation at the horizon and infinity.
Its resonant response appears as quasinormal ringdown. We use
this classical reduction without modification
\citep{ReggeWheeler1957,Zerilli1970,Moncrief1974,Chandrasekhar1975,MartelPoisson2005}.

A quasinormal mode is a separated solution
$\Phi(t,x) = \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega t}\,\psi(x)$ of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}. Substitution gives the time-independent
Schr\"odinger-like equation
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\mathrm{d}^{2}\psi}{\mathrm{d}x^{2}}
  + \bigl(\omega^{2} - V_{\ell}(r(x))\bigr)\psi(x) \;=\; 0.
  \label{eq:zerilli-freq}
\end{equation}
In the asymptotic regions, the QNM boundary conditions select
solutions purely ingoing at the horizon and purely outgoing at
infinity,
\begin{equation}
  \psi(x) \;\to\; \mathrm{e}^{-\mathrm{i}\omega x}\;\;(x \to -\infty),
  \qquad
  \psi(x) \;\to\; \mathrm{e}^{+\mathrm{i}\omega x}\;\;(x \to +\infty).
  \label{eq:qnm-bc}
\end{equation}
These radiative conditions define a non-Hermitian problem with
a discrete complex spectrum
\citep{Berti2009review,KonoplyaZhidenko2011},
\begin{equation}
  \omega_{n} \;=\; \omega_{n}^{R} - \mathrm{i}/\tau_{n},
  \qquad \omega_{n}^{R}, \tau_{n} > 0,
  \label{eq:qnm-omega}
\end{equation}
ordered by overtone index $n=0,1,2,\ldots$ in increasing
damping rate $1/\tau_n$. The real part $\omega_n^{R}$ is the
oscillation frequency and $\tau_n$ the damping time.

For Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$ the fundamental ($n = 0$) and
first overtone ($n = 1$) are
\begin{equation}
  M\omega_{0}^{R} = 0.3737, \quad \tau_{0}/M = 11.241,
  \qquad
  M\omega_{1}^{R} = 0.3467, \quad \tau_{1}/M = 3.651,
  \label{eq:qnm-ref}
\end{equation}
computed to high numerical precision by Leaver's
continued-fraction method \citep{Leaver1985} and tabulated by
\citet{Berti2009review}. These values provide the Schwarzschild
references used below. The fundamental $\ell = 2$
mode dominates the observed ringdown of binary mergers and is
the primary spectroscopy target; the first overtone enters at
sufficient signal-to-noise ratio
\citep{Buonanno2007ringdown,LIGO2016ringdown,Isi2021priors}.
Alternative calculations use WKB and asymptotic-iteration
methods, while Nollert's refinement improves the
continued-fraction benchmark
\citep{SchutzWill1985,IyerWill1987,Konoplya2003wkb,
Cho2010aim,Nollert1993}.

\subsection{Kerr perturbations and the Teukolsky equation}
\label{sec:teukolsky-problem}

Astrophysical remnants rotate, and a rotating black hole of mass $M$ and spin
$a = J/M$ is described, in Boyer--Lindquist coordinates, by the
Kerr line element
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
  \mathrm{d}s^{2} ={}&
    -\left(1 - \frac{2Mr}{\Sigma}\right)\mathrm{d}t^{2}
    - \frac{4 M a r \sin^{2}\!\theta}{\Sigma}\,
       \mathrm{d}t\,\mathrm{d}\varphi \\
    &+ \frac{\Sigma}{\Delta}\,\mathrm{d}r^{2}
    + \Sigma\,\mathrm{d}\theta^{2} \\
    &+ \left(r^{2} + a^{2}
       + \frac{2 M a^{2} r \sin^{2}\!\theta}{\Sigma}\right)
       \sin^{2}\!\theta\,\mathrm{d}\varphi^{2},
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:kerr-metric}
\end{equation}
with $\Sigma = r^{2} + a^{2}\cos^{2}\theta$ and
$\Delta = r^{2} - 2Mr + a^{2}$. This reduces to the
Schwarzschild metric Eq.~\eqref{eq:schwarzschild} as
$a \to 0$. The roots of $\Delta$ are the outer and inner
horizons
\begin{equation}
  r_{\pm} = M \pm \sqrt{M^{2} - a^{2}},
  \label{eq:kerr-horizons}
\end{equation}
which merge at extremality $a \to M$. The off-diagonal
$\mathrm{d}t\,\mathrm{d}\varphi$ term is the frame dragging that
distinguishes Kerr from Schwarzschild. We study spins
$a/M \in [0, 0.95]$, stopping short of extremality.

Kerr gravitational perturbations do not decouple into axial and
polar sectors. They are instead governed by the spin-weight
$s=-2$ Teukolsky master equation \citep{Teukolsky1973}. For
modal analysis, the separable ansatz
$\psi = e^{-i\omega t + i m\varphi}\,
{}_{-2}S_{\ell m}(\theta)\,R(r)$ yields a spheroidal angular
equation, whose eigenfunctions are the spin-weighted spheroidal
harmonics ${}_{-2}S_{\ell m}(\theta; a\omega)$ with separation
constant $\lambda$, and the radial equation
\begin{equation}
  \Delta^{2}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}r}\!
    \left(\Delta^{-1}\frac{\mathrm{d}R}{\mathrm{d}r}\right)
  + \left[\frac{K^{2} + 4 i (r - M) K}{\Delta}
          - 8 i \omega r - \lambda\right] R = 0,
  \qquad
  K = (r^{2} + a^{2})\,\omega - a m .
  \label{eq:teukolsky}
\end{equation}
Rotation enters through both $a\omega$ in the angular eigenvalue
and the $a m$ term in $K$, coupling the complex spectrum to the
spin and azimuthal number. As $a \to 0$,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:teukolsky} reduces to the Bardeen--Press form and
recovers the Regge--Wheeler and Zerilli QNM spectrum. For the
prograde modes shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-spectrum}, both
$M\omega_R$ and $\tau/M$ increase with spin, so an hFNO trained
across spin must represent this dependence.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We reproduced and improved the published Schwarzschild PINN
calculation, then introduced separately trained Schwarzschild and Kerr
hFNOs for inexpensive evaluation across their respective parameter
sets. At inference, each new configuration requires one cheap
numerical-prior evolution, interpolation to the output grid and one
FNO forward pass, without per-configuration neural-network
retraining. The Schwarzschild and Kerr hFNOs achieve mean and median
relative $L^2$ field errors of $1.85\%$ and $0.78\%$ against their
fine references. Their median fundamental-frequency errors against
Leaver are $0.152\%$ and $0.33\%$, respectively.

The principal remaining limitation is the low-amplitude Kerr
ringdown, for which the median damping-time error against Leaver is
$1.52\%$. Late-time-sensitive training, broader harmonic and
initial-data coverage, and end-to-end timing and likelihood studies
are therefore the next priorities. Together, the hFNOs provide
accurate ringdown fields across the tested parameter spaces using one
cheap reduced-resolution numerical evolution per configuration.
