\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Relation to prior work.}
The faithful reproduction of \citet{Patel2024pinn} establishes
the time-domain PINN baseline used throughout this study. It
retains the published network, loss construction, optimiser
schedule and collocation strategy, and provides a controlled
comparator for the enhanced forward PINN and the hFNO. Direct
eigenvalue PINNs such as those of \citet{Cornell2022pinnqnm} and
\citet{Luna2022teukolskypinn} solve a different problem because
the QNM frequency is itself a trainable variable. The present
models instead approximate the time-domain field, from which
the QNM is measured afterwards.

\paragraph{A learned correction to a numerical propagator.}
The principal contribution is the hFNO, which is best
interpreted as a learned defect correction for a coarse
numerical solver. The finite-difference prior carries
characteristic propagation, potential scattering and the
imposed boundary treatment. The neural operator is therefore
not required to reconstruct the complete solution map from the
initial data alone. It learns the structured difference between
the under-resolved trajectory and a higher-order Richardson
estimate. This division of labour follows the broader strategy
of combining numerical solvers with learned corrections
\citep{Um2020solverloop,Kochkov2021mlcfd,Liu2022ppnn}, while
differing in two important respects. The hFNO predicts the
complete spatiotemporal correction rather than inserting a
learned update at every numerical step, and its target is
constructed from two coarse solves rather than fine-grid
training fields. Multi-fidelity operator learning provides a
close methodological parallel because it likewise uses a
low-fidelity field as both an input and an anchor for residual
learning \citep{Thakur2023mfwno}.

\paragraph{Evidence from Schwarzschild and Kerr.}
On the Schwarzschild test population, the hFNO reduces the mean
field RL2 error from $13.39\%$ to $1.85\%$ and the field MSE by
a factor of $46$. The gradient correlation shows why this
decomposition is effective. The largest coarse-grid errors
occur near the propagating wavefronts, allowing the learned
correction to concentrate on a structured and spatially
restricted defect. On Kerr, the same coarse-prior-plus-residual
construction is retained after adapting the representation to
the complex hyperboloidal Teukolsky field and retraining across
spin. The median field error falls from $59.5\%$ to $0.78\%$,
while the high-spin frequency error falls from $4.71\%$ to
$0.38\%$. The Richardson target itself has a median field error
of $0.008\%$, showing that the remaining discrepancy is
dominated by approximation of the correction rather than by
the target construction. The Kerr experiment therefore
demonstrates portability of the hybrid design across two
perturbation formulations, not transfer of one trained model
between geometries.

\paragraph{What the QNM results reveal.}
The QNM measurements diagnose which parts of the corrected
field are retained most accurately. The hFNO recovers the
Schwarzschild population frequency to $0.152\%$ and the Kerr
population frequency to $0.33\%$, but the Kerr damping-time
error increases from $0.52\%$ for the coarse prior to $1.52\%$
after correction. This separation is consistent with the field
loss being dominated by the high-amplitude burst and
wavefronts, while the damping time depends strongly on the much
smaller late-time envelope. It identifies a limitation of the
present objective rather than an intrinsic limitation of
learned field correction. The extraction suite remains a
downstream diagnostic, and the plateau spread measures
fit-window sensitivity rather than model uncertainty.

\paragraph{Scope and limitations.}
The reported population tests are in distribution, use one
perturbative harmonic in each geometry and require separate
training for the Schwarzschild and Kerr formulations. The
fixed-grid Fourier representation has not been tested under
changes of domain, resolution or boundary treatment. The
gradient gate is effective globally but does not guarantee
pointwise improvement wherever the prior is already accurate.
Richardson extrapolation also relies on the two coarse
discretisations lying within the expected convergence regime.
Controlled overtone-rich initial data and additional harmonics
are required before extending the physical claims beyond the
fundamental modes studied here. Finally,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:hyb-work-ratio} counts only the finite-difference
point updates in the Schwarzschild coarse solve. A wall-clock
comparison including interpolation and neural inference is still
required to establish the realised speedup and training
break-even point.

\paragraph{Further amortisation.}
The present hFNO amortises the correction but still evaluates a
coarse solve for every new black hole. Multi-fidelity operator
learning suggests a second stage in which a low-fidelity
operator approximates the coarse solver and the residual
operator supplies the fine correction
\citep{Thakur2023mfwno}. Retaining the numerical solver as a
fallback would preserve the physical anchor when predictive
uncertainty is large. A complementary route is
multi-resolution active learning, which selects both the next
parameter sample and the resolution at which it should be
generated according to expected information gain per unit cost
\citep{Li2024mrafno}. Applied here, this could allocate the more
expensive Richardson pairs to spins, initial data and wavefront
structures that contribute most to the correction error.

\paragraph{Long-time and structure-preserving operators.}
The damping-time result motivates replacing the single global
correction with a temporally decomposed or rollout-aware
operator. Solver-in-the-loop training and long-time operator
learning show that exposing a model to its own corrected states
can reduce accumulated phase and amplitude error
\citep{Um2020solverloop,Wang2023longtime,McCabe2023stability}.
A Koopman residual operator offers a related hypothesis. Since
the fixed-background perturbation equations are already linear,
its role would be to learn a compact parameter-dependent
evolution subspace rather than to linearise the dynamics. The
latent evolution could be tested directly for preservation of
the damped modal spectrum across prompt response, ringdown and
tail regimes \citep{Brunton2022koopman,Xiong2024kno}.

For Schwarzschild, a second structure-preserving direction
follows from writing the semidiscrete wave equation in the
canonical variables $(\Phi,\Pi)$. SympFlow constructs
time-continuous symplectic maps from exact Hamiltonian subflows
and permits backward-error analysis of the learned evolution
\citep{Canizares2026sympflow}. A corresponding hybrid model
could preserve the Hamiltonian structure of the interior update
while treating radiation through the boundaries as an explicit
energy flux. Such a construction should first be established
for the Schwarzschild system. Extension to the complex Kerr
evolution would require a separate derivation of the
appropriate conservative and radiative structure.

\paragraph{From waveforms to inference.}
The amortised waveform family can also support an amortised
inference pipeline. Reduced bases and reduced-order quadratures
compress repeated gravitational-wave likelihood evaluations
into a small set of empirically selected samples while retaining
the accuracy of the original likelihood
\citep{Canizares2013roq,Canizares2015roq}. Building such a basis
from the hFNO waveform family would connect rapid field
generation to Bayesian inference over black-hole and ringdown
parameters. This would provide a more complete test of
practical value than detector-unit conversion alone and would
allow detector noise, calibrated hFNO discrepancy and
fit-window dependence to be treated as distinct contributions.
