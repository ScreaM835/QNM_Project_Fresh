\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

Section~\ref{sec:res-fwd} reports the forward PINN field
accuracy against the FD reference and the five-method extraction
of $(\omega,\tau)$ from $\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$.
Section~\ref{sec:res-inv} reports the six inverse PINN variants:
mass recovery, the primary post-hoc QNM estimate, and the
$A_{\mathrm{ring}}$--$\tau$ degeneracy of the template loss.
Section~\ref{sec:res-cur} reports the three curriculum-PINN
configurations against the single-network adaptive-sampling
baseline. Section~\ref{sec:res-fno} reports the Fourier neural
operator surrogate of Sec.~\ref{sec:fno-method} on a single
canonical BH and on a $100$-BH population drawn from the
training distribution, including the three-version ablation
that motivates the final v4 loss design.
Section~\ref{sec:res-hyb} reports the hybrid coarse-FD plus
neural-residual surrogate of Sec.~\ref{sec:hyb-method} on the
same canonical BH and on a $100$-BH Sobol test population,
including the field-accuracy reduction relative to a quintic-spline
upsample of the weak $k = 4$ coarse FD solution and the
honest finding that QNM extraction at $\xq = 2\,M$ is already
limited by the plateau extractor floor and not by the coarse-up
baseline.

Throughout this paper the \emph{forward PINN} refers to the
network of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn} that solves the Zerilli
equation forward at fixed $M$ from PDE residual plus IC/BC
losses, the \emph{inverse PINN} refers to the joint
PDE-plus-data network of Sec.~\ref{sec:inv-pinn} that
simultaneously recovers $M$ and a single-mode template
$(\omega_{\mathrm{learned}},\tau_{\mathrm{learned}},
A_{\mathrm{ring}},\phi_0)$ from $\Nobs$ noisy field samples,
and the \emph{FNO surrogate} refers to the operator-learning
network of Sec.~\ref{sec:fno-method} that maps initial data and
mass to the full spacetime field in a single forward pass after
amortised training across a population of $1{,}000$ BHs.

\subsection{Forward PINN}
\label{sec:res-fwd}

The converged forward PINN matches the FD reference to high
accuracy on the full simulation domain. With the architecture and
training schedule of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn} the field-accuracy
metrics, evaluated on the FD grid
$x \in [-50,150]\,M$, $t \in [0,50]\,M$, are
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{RMSD} = 8.17\times 10^{-4}, \qquad
  \mathrm{MAD}  = 4.99\times 10^{-4}, \qquad
  \mathrm{RL2}  = 5.79\times 10^{-3},
  \label{eq:fwd-metrics}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{RMSD}$ and $\mathrm{MAD}$ are the root-mean-square
and mean-absolute differences $\Phi_\theta - \Phi_{\mathrm{FD}}$ in
absolute units (peak $|\Phi| \approx 0.7$ on the domain), and
$\mathrm{RL2}$ is their relative $L^2$ norm.
This $\mathrm{RL2} = 0.58\%$ is a factor of $\sim\!50$ smaller
than the $\mathrm{RL2} = 28.06\%$ of
\citet[Table~2, Zerilli row]{Patel2024pinn} on the same problem,
and the corresponding $\mathrm{MAD}$ improvement is
$5.0\times 10^{-4}$ versus their $1.82\times 10^{-2}$, a factor
of~$\sim\!36$. We attribute the improvement to the larger number
of L-BFGS iterations and the greedy adaptive sampling of
Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}; see also the discussion in
Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}. Field accuracy at the snapshot times
is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-field}, the global pointwise error
in Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-heatmap}, and the per-component loss
histories underlying Eq.~\eqref{eq:fwd-metrics} in
Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-loss}.

Three points stand out. First, the M4 plateau
estimate of $\omega$ achieves $0.029\%$ accuracy, more than an
order of magnitude tighter than any single-window method, and the
plateau standard deviation $\sigma_\omega \sim 2\times 10^{-4}$ is
comparable to the residual bias of the plateau mean against the
Leaver value ($|0.37359 - 0.3737| \approx 1.1\times 10^{-4}$).
Second, M2 on the M4 plateau window reduces the
$\omega$ error of M2 on the wide window by a factor of five (from
$1.54\%$ to $0.31\%$) but increases the $\tau$ error fourfold (from
$1.99\%$ to $7.38\%$), motivating the asymmetric reporting
convention of Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}. Third, M3
returns frequencies at machine-precision amplitudes ($|c_k| \sim
10^{-23}$), reflecting the fact that the analytic ESPRIT problem
is ill-conditioned on a $\Phi(\xq,t)$ whose dominant component is
already a clean damped sinusoid; we quote M3 in
Appendix~\ref{app:esprit} for completeness only. The M5 plateau in
the $(t_0,\tend)$ plane spans the corner of the grid closest to
the QNM regime, $t_0 \in [10,16.7]\,M$ and $\tend \in [42,50]\,M$,
exactly as expected from Eq.~\eqref{eq:timescales}, since the
plateau abuts the lower-$t_0$ edge once the prompt burst has
passed ($t_0 \gtrsim t_{\mathrm{burst}}$) and extends to the
upper-$\tend$ edge of the simulation domain since
$\tend \ll t_{\mathrm{tail}}$ on $[0,50]\,M$.

The cross-method picture for variant D mirrors the forward run
of Table~\ref{tab:fwd-qnm}: M4 and M5 yield mutually consistent
$\omega$ estimates at the $0.30\%$ level with plateau standard deviations
$\sim 3\text{--}4\times 10^{-5}$, M1 is competitive on $\omega$
($0.29\%$) but loses on $\tau$ as expected from the
log-line slope's sensitivity to the prompt burst, and M2 on the
wide window is the best $\tau$ estimator ($1.59\%$). For
$\tau$, M4 and M5 collapse to the template-degeneracy value
$\sim 9.48\,M$ (the same value the trained scalar
$\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$ converged to in
Fig.~\ref{fig:inv-conv}), while M2 on the wide window recovers
$11.06\,M$ from the network's own field $\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$;
this is the $A_{\mathrm{ring}}$--$\tau$ degeneracy of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring}, discussed quantitatively in the
paragraph below (with Tables~\ref{tab:inv-learned}
and~\ref{tab:tau-degeneracy}) and visualised in
Fig.~\ref{fig:inv-conv}. The reporting convention of
Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting} therefore takes $\omega$ from M4 and
$\tau$ from M2-wide, as already used in
Table~\ref{tab:inv-summary}.

behave as expected: $M$ (top-left) overshoots to $\sim 1.15$
during Adam, returns to $\sim 1.04$, and converges to its
final value $1.003$ ($0.34\%$ from the true mass) under L-BFGS;
$\omega_{\mathrm{learned}}$ (top-right) climbs from $0.30$ to
$\sim 0.355$ during Adam, drifts down to $\sim 0.31$, and
recovers to its final value $0.3748$ ($0.30\%$ from the Leaver
value) under L-BFGS, and the apparent ``ends low'' behaviour is
an artefact of the logged-sample axis rather than the data;
$A_{\mathrm{ring}}$ (middle-right) rises from $0.5$ to a final
value $2.93$, overshooting to $\sim 3.29$ during early L-BFGS
before settling back; and $\phi$ (bottom-left) settles to
$-0.85$. The exception is $\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$
(middle-left), which drifts from $\tau_{\mathrm{init}} = 8.0\,M$
down to $\sim 7.3\,M$ during Adam, climbs to $9.48\,M$ via
L-BFGS, and finishes $15.7\%$ below the Leaver value. This is
the $A_{\mathrm{ring}}$--$\tau$ degeneracy of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring} introduced in Sec.~\ref{sec:inv-pinn};
the bottom-right panel confirms it visually, since the template
at the final learned scalars is visually indistinguishable from
the FD truth at $\xq=10\,M$ on $t \gtrsim 18\,M$ even with
$\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$ off by $1.8\,M$. The network field
$\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$ is constrained by the PDE residual and the
noisy data and exhibits the physical decay rate; the
post-hoc M2 wide-window fit on $\Phi_\theta$ recovers
$\tau = 11.06\,M$ ($1.6\%$ from the Leaver value) as listed in
Table~\ref{tab:inv-summary}. The learned and post-hoc values of
$\tau$ are tabulated side by side for all six variants in
Table~\ref{tab:tau-degeneracy}.

All six variants reduce the $20\%$ mass initialisation error
$|M_{\mathrm{init}}-M_{\mathrm{true}}|/M_{\mathrm{true}} = 0.20$ to
$\le 1.5\%$ within the allotted $25{,}000$ optimiser iterations. The single-mode-template
variants (baseline, A, C, D) recover $M$ to within $0.5\%$;
variants B and E are worse, with $1.25\%$ and $1.44\%$
respectively. Variant B places weight $100$ on a template the
optimiser cannot satisfy with a single damped sinusoid (the
prompt burst is still present at $\tring = 10\,M$,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:timescales}), so field and mass accuracy are
sacrificed to template accuracy. In E the four additional learnable parameters of the
overtone slow mass convergence, but the structural information
added by the second mode improves the primary $\tau$ estimate
substantially (1.3\% vs.\ 5.3\% for baseline; see below).

We quote $\omega$ from the M4 plateau mean (Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}).
The plateau standard deviation $\sigma_\omega$ is also a useful
diagnostic. For variants A, D
and E the plateau is tight ($\sigma_\omega/\omega \le 1\%$) and
$\omega$ is recovered to $0.3$--$2.6\%$. For baseline and C the
plateau is wide ($\sigma_\omega/\omega \approx 0.44$ and $0.34$
respectively), signalling that the two-mode NLS does not find a
stable $(\omega_0,\tau_0)$ across $t_0$; the mean is not a
trustworthy estimate, and the M2-wide value of $\omega$ would in
fact be more accurate. For B the two-mode NLS does not converge to
a finite minimum on any window in the scan, so M4 is undefined.
$\sigma_\omega$ is therefore a quality diagnostic. A small
$\sigma_\omega$ accepts the estimate, a large or undefined
$\sigma_\omega$ rejects it (cf.\ Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}).
Variant D ranks first on the combination of $M$, $\omega$ and
$\tau$. The single-factor changes A and
C are individually beneficial (A improves $\tau$ and the M4
plateau; C improves $M$); B is strictly worse than baseline on
$\tau$ and M4 plateau quality at no benefit to $M$; E is
competitive on the primary QNM numbers but pays a $\sim 1\%$
penalty on $M$ and $\omega$ for the extra template freedom.
Variant D combines the three single-factor improvements of A--C:
it has the tightest plateau ($\sigma_\omega = 4\times 10^{-5}$)
and the smallest $\omega$ error ($0.30\%$) of the six runs.

The pattern in Table~\ref{tab:tau-degeneracy} groups by
$\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}}$. For baseline and C the learned and
post-hoc $\tau$ agree to within $0.06\,M$, but \emph{both} are
biased high by $\sim 0.6\,M$ relative to the Leaver value; the post-hoc M2 fit is constrained by the
network's own field, which has been trained against the inverse
template, so when the template is weak ($\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}} =
1$) the network does not correct the template's late-time
overshoot. For variant D, raising $\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}}$ to
$100$ \emph{and} increasing $\tring$ to $18\,M$ shifts
$\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$ down to $9.48\,M$ along the
degeneracy with $A_{\mathrm{ring}} = 2.93$, while the post-hoc M2
fit to the converged $\Phi_\theta$ recovers $11.06\,M$. The
network-level field $\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$ has therefore learned the
correct decay rate from the noisy data and the PDE, despite the
template parameters drifting in the opposite direction; the
post-hoc extraction recovers the field's own decay rate, not the
template's. This separation of the trained network's field from
the trained template parameters is the reason for the reporting
convention of Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}, and it is also the reason
variant D wins on $\omega$. With the template at $\tring = 18\,M$
the prompt-burst contamination of the M4 plateau scan is
suppressed (cf.\ Eq.~\eqref{eq:timescales}), and the network's
$\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$ converges to a QNM-dominated waveform on
which the M4 two-mode NLS finds a tight plateau, despite the
drift of the template parameters themselves.

The 2-window hard partition (Cur-2w) is essentially tied with the
single-window baseline on the full-domain RMSD
($8.45 \times 10^{-4}$ versus $8.17 \times 10^{-4}$, a $3\%$
increase) and recovers the same headline QNM at the M5 plateau
($M\omega = 0.3729$, error $0.22\%$ versus $0.23\%$ for the
baseline). The M4 plateau, by contrast, degrades from
$\sigma_\omega = 2\times 10^{-4}$ to
$\sigma_\omega = 9\times 10^{-4}$ and the M4 mean drifts from
$0.029\%$ to $0.79\%$, a 27-fold loss of plateau quality. The
per-window breakdown of Table~\ref{tab:cur-summary} is
informative: $W_{1} = [0,25]\,M$ alone
reaches RMSD $5.16\times 10^{-4}$, the lowest sub-domain RMSD
seen anywhere in this work and a $37\%$ improvement on the
baseline RMSD evaluated over the same sub-domain (which we read
off the baseline's pointwise error map; the time-restricted
baseline RMSD on $[0,25]\,M$ is $8.2\times 10^{-4}$).
The curriculum strategy does therefore deliver the predicted
local benefit on a smaller temporal sub-domain. The benefit is
neutralised by $W_{2} = [25,50]\,M$, which sees RMSD
$10.8\times 10^{-4}$, larger than the full-domain baseline and
roughly $2\times$ larger than $W_{1}$. The $W_{2}$ error
inherits both (i) the residual error of $W_{1}$ at $t = 25\,M$
through the IC transfer of Eq.~\eqref{eq:cur-ic} and (ii) the
greater dynamic-range demand of resolving the late-time
exponentially-decaying ringdown after the prompt burst has
exited.

The 3-window hard partition (Cur-3w) makes the same diagnostic
sharper. Per-window RMSD grows monotonically with window index,
$W_{1} \to W_{2} \to W_{3} = 5.5 \to 9.2 \to 13.2$ (units of
$10^{-4}$). Each additional stitching boundary contributes a
fresh interpolation error to the IC of the next window, and the
errors compound. The full-domain RMSD ends $19\%$ above the
single-window baseline, and the M1 $\tau$ error worsens from
$2.95\%$ (Cur-2w) to $3.83\%$ (Cur-3w), tracking the additional
late-time stitching error. The M4 and M5 plateaux of
Table~\ref{tab:cur-qnm} continue to form (no plateau failure)
but with $\sigma_\omega = 5\!-\!7 \times 10^{-4}$ and a mean
bias $0.64\%$, two-and-a-half times the bias of the
single-window baseline. The M2 wide-window $\omega$ and $\tau$
are largely insensitive to which curriculum variant is used,
which is consistent with the wide window averaging over
the per-window errors.

The soft-overlap variant (Cur-soft) does not improve the
hard-partition result. It performs worse than Cur-2w on every
extraction column except M2 wide-window $\tau$, which it
marginally ties. The full-domain RMSD is $\sim 4\times$ the
single-window baseline; both $W_{1}$ and the overlap region
$[20,30]\,M$ are individually worse than the corresponding
hard-partition entries. Most strikingly, the M4 plateau scan
fails to identify any contiguous block of size
$\lfloor N/4 \rfloor$ for which
$\mathrm{std}(\omega)/|\bar\omega| < 3\%$, i.e.\ no plateau
forms; the table reports the relative-scatter floor
($\sigma_\omega/|\omega| > 0.06$) and the corresponding
single-best two-mode fit, $M\omega = 0.278$ at $25.7\%$ error,
which is the standard manifestation of the no-plateau condition
of Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}. M5 finds a small valid 2-D plateau
($t_0 \in [10,16.7]\,M$, $\tend \in [30,38]\,M$) but with
$\sigma_\omega = 4\times 10^{-2}$, two orders of magnitude
larger than the baseline, signalling that even the 2-D scan
struggles to find a stable region. Two factors plausibly
contribute. First, the continuity-anchor loss
Eq.~\eqref{eq:cur-anchor} imposes
$201 \times 11 = 2{,}211$ additional point constraints on
$\Phi_{\theta_{2}}$ across the overlap, drawn from the trained
$\Phi_{\theta_{1}}$, which itself carries non-zero error
($\sim 1.7\times 10^{-3}$ on the overlap as measured against
the FD reference); the anchors therefore propagate an error
floor into $W_{2}$ that the PDE constraint cannot reduce below.
Second, the optimiser has to balance the PDE loss, the
two IC residuals at $t = 20\,M$, and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cont}}$
simultaneously, and the additional loss term modifies the
relative weighting from the
$\boldsymbol{\lambda} = (100,100,100,1,100,1,1)$ baseline that
was tuned for the single-window setup.

The curriculum strategy therefore meets its narrow design
objective (lower per-window error on the early sub-domain) but
does not translate into a lower full-domain RMSD or a tighter
QNM extraction once the windows are stitched. The dominant cost
is the IC-transfer interpolation error, which compounds linearly
with the number of stitching boundaries and grows at late times
as the signal amplitude decays. A useful baseline against which
the curriculum should be compared is the
greedy-adaptive-sampling forward PINN of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}
itself, which delivers the same factor-of-two reduction in
late-time error without paying the per-stitch interpolation cost,
by concentrating $0.3\,N_{r}$ of the collocation budget on the
high-residual late-time band within a single network. We discuss
the implications for the broader curriculum-PINN literature in
Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Fourier neural operator surrogate}
\label{sec:res-fno}

The FNO surrogate of Sec.~\ref{sec:fno-method} trains once on
a population of $1{,}000$ $(M,x_{0},\sigma)$ triples and then
predicts $\Phi(x,t)$ for an unseen draw with a single forward
pass, in contrast to the PINNs of
Secs.~\ref{sec:res-fwd}--\ref{sec:res-cur} which solve one
Zerilli problem at a time. We evaluate the surrogate in two
settings that together quantify its competitiveness with the
PINN baseline. The \emph{canonical} draw $(M,x_{0},\sigma) = (1,4,5)$
fixes the same single-BH benchmark used for the forward,
inverse, and curriculum PINNs and so allows a direct,
same-target comparison of field accuracy and post-hoc QNM
extraction precision. The $100$-BH in-distribution
\emph{population} probes the regime in which per-BH PINN
training is prohibitive and amortisation across draws is the
relevant cost figure of merit. FNO numbers in this subsection
are primarily extracted at $\xq = 2\,M$ for the reasons given in
Sec.~\ref{sec:fno-method}; the four estimators of
Sec.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods} and the reporting convention of
Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting} apply unchanged.

\paragraph{Field accuracy on the canonical BH.} The v4 FNO
reproduces the FD reference field on the canonical draw with
gridwise $\mathrm{RMSD} = 9.30\times 10^{-4}$ (in field
units, $|\Phi|_{\max} \approx 1.0$ at $t = 0$), with the
residual concentrated near the prompt pulse and at late times
where the signal amplitude itself has decayed by three orders
of magnitude.
Figure~\ref{fig:fno-snapshots} compares the v4 FNO prediction
against the FD reference at four time slices across

line thickness; the residual peaks near $6\times 10^{-3}$ in
the vicinity of the moving wave packet and sits at

sign change in the QNM window, M1 becomes the strongest
estimator on both the FNO field and the FD reference at
$\omega$ error $\sim\!10^{-2}\%$, and the longer time

absence of M4 bars at $\xq = 10$ for both colours and motivates
our choice of $\xq = 2\,M$ as the primary FNO extraction
position, the only observer in our window where M4 forms a
plateau on the FD reference field as well as on the FNO
prediction.

\paragraph{Version ablation.}
The v4 design is the third of three FNO configurations that
we trained on the same population. v2 and v3 were superseded
because field RMSD on the training distribution is not a
sufficient figure of merit for QNM extraction.
Table~\ref{tab:fno-versions} summarises the architectural and
training differences and reports canonical-BH field RMSD,
canonical-BH M4 $\omega$ error, and population-median M4
$(\omega,\tau)$ error across the three versions.
\textbf{v2} uses a shorter time window $t \in [0,50]\,M$ and
modest spectral resolution ($n_{t} = 32$ Fourier modes along
$t$). It has the lowest field RMSD on the canonical BH of the
three ($5.80\times 10^{-4}$), but $50\,M$ is fewer than five
fundamental damping times, and the M4 plateau scan returns a
$\tau$ error of $344\%$ on the canonical BH and a population
median of $6.95\%$. The field is well-fit on average but the
late-time decay is not resolved well enough for QNM extraction.
\textbf{v3} extends $t_{\max}$ to $100\,M$ and doubles the temporal
spectral resolution to $n_{t} = 64$. The population-median QNM
errors drop to $0.44\%$ in $\omega$ and $1.31\%$ in $\tau$, a
factor of $\sim 5$ improvement. The canonical-BH M4 numbers
remain poor ($19.25\%$ in $\omega$, $35.01\%$ in $\tau$). The
population-average loss therefore does not control the
worst-case behaviour on the 1-D slice that the QNM extractor
actually reads.
\textbf{v4} adds two loss terms targeted at exactly that
worst-case. The first is the 1-D slice loss
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{obs}}$ at $x = \xq$. The second is the
time-weighted field loss $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{tw}}$. Both are
combined with a bump of $\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}}$ from $2$ to

population median improves modestly to $0.34\%$ in

floor. The canonical-BH field RMSD rises slightly relative to
v3 ($9.30\times 10^{-4}$ vs $7.48\times 10^{-4}$), the
expected trade-off when training pressure is reallocated from
the bulk field to the 1-D observer slice. This trade-off is
favourable here because $\Phi(\xq,t)$ is the only quantity the
downstream QNM pipeline reads. The v4 configuration is the
primary FNO reported in the rest of this paper.

\paragraph{Cost.} Training the v4 FNO once on $1{,}000$
$(M,x_{0},\sigma)$ triples takes $\sim\!10$ A100-hours for
$300$ epochs; subsequent inference on an unseen triple is a
single forward pass costing $\mathcal{O}(\mathrm{seconds})$ on
the same hardware (and a few minutes on CPU). The forward PINN
of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn} requires a separate
$30{,}000$-iteration L-BFGS run per BH, costing
$\sim\!1.5$~A100-hours per BH. Writing $T_{\mathrm{train}}^{\mathrm{FNO}}\approx 10$~h,
$T_{\mathrm{infer}}^{\mathrm{FNO}}\!\lesssim\!10^{-3}$~h and
$T^{\mathrm{PINN}}\approx 1.5$~h per BH, the total wall-clock to
deliver predictions for $N_{\mathrm{BH}}$ unseen triples scales as
$N_{\mathrm{BH}} T^{\mathrm{PINN}}$ versus
$T_{\mathrm{train}}^{\mathrm{FNO}} + N_{\mathrm{BH}} T_{\mathrm{infer}}^{\mathrm{FNO}}$,
which break even at $N_{\mathrm{BH}}\!\approx\!7$ and diverge by two
orders of magnitude by $N_{\mathrm{BH}}\!=\!100$
(Table~\ref{tab:fno-pinn-cost}). The comparison is fair only on
draws that lie inside the FNO training distribution; the FNO has
no generalisation guarantee outside
$(M,x_{0},\sigma) \in [0.8,1.2]\times[-10,30]\times[3,8]$,
whereas the PINN solves each new BH from scratch.

\paragraph{Zero-shot resolution transfer.}
A discretisation-invariant operator should produce a
predictor whose physical output is independent of the grid on
which one chooses to evaluate it. Because the spectral
convolution kernels of the FNO truncate to a fixed number of
Fourier modes regardless of the input resolution, this is one
of the standard advertised properties of the architecture
\citep{Li2021fno} and a test we should subject our trained v4
checkpoint to directly. We take the canonical
$M=1,\,x_{0}=4,\,\sigma=5$ BH and evaluate the v4 model at
five evaluation grids
$(N_{t}, N_{x}) \in \{201, 301, 401, 601, 801\}^{2}$ over the
same physical box $x \in [-50, 150]\,M$, $t \in [0, 100]\,M$
as was used in training ($401\times 401$), with no retraining
and no fine-tuning. The FD reference is computed independently
at each resolution. Table~\ref{tab:fno-resolution} reports
the late-time field RMSD and the M3 ESPRIT $\omega$
extraction at $x_{q} = 2\,M$ on the FNO field. The QNM
observable transfers \emph{cleanly}: $M\omega_{0}$ \% error
sits in the range $[0.56, 0.61]$\% across all five grids, a
spread of only $0.05$ percentage points and entirely
consistent with the canonical-BH numbers reported elsewhere
in this subsection. The field RMSD, by contrast, has a clear
V-shape with its minimum ($8.4\times 10^{-4}$) at the
$401^{2}$ training resolution and rises to
$\sim\!7\times 10^{-3}$ at half ($201^{2}$) or double
($801^{2}$) resolution: the operator has absorbed a small
training-grid bias into its spectral weights that the
band-limited interpolation step cannot remove. The two
findings together establish that the surrogate is
discretisation-invariant at the level of the \emph{physical
QNM observable} it is being used to predict, even though the
pointwise field carries a residual grid signature.

\subsection{Hybrid coarse-FD + neural-residual surrogate}
\label{sec:res-hyb}

The hybrid surrogate of Sec.~\ref{sec:hyb-method} keeps a
coarse-grid FD solve as the workhorse and asks the network only
for the pointwise residual against the fine-grid FD solution to
the same problem. We evaluate it on the same two settings used
for the end-to-end FNO of Sec.~\ref{sec:res-fno}: the
canonical draw $(M, x_{0}, \sigma) = (1, 4, 5)$ provides
single-BH diagnostics directly comparable to the PINN and FNO
baselines, and the $100$-BH in-distribution Sobol test set
provides the population picture. All hybrid numbers in this
subsection are reported at the coarsening factor $k = 4$ (i.e.\
coarse $(\Delta x, \Delta t) = (0.8, 0.4)\,M$, fine
$(0.2, 0.1)\,M$), with the coarse prior solved on the
default second-order centred stencil of
Sec.~\ref{sec:hyb-method} and the network trained on the
label-free Richardson target $\delta\Phi_{\mathrm{R}}$
(Eqs.~\eqref{eq:hyb-richardson}--\eqref{eq:hyb-residual}). At
inference a single $k = 4$ coarse solve is the only classical
work, costing $k^{-3} = 1/64$ of a fine FD solve. The reference
object on the canonical BH is
$\Phi^{\mathrm{f}}$, the fine-grid FD solution: the question
the hybrid surrogate is built to answer is whether the gated
network can close the discretisation-error gap between
$\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}$ (the quintic-spline-upsampled
$k = 4$ coarse FD solution, henceforth ``coarse-up baseline'')
and $\Phi^{\mathrm{f}}$. The $k = 4$ prior is deliberately
weak --- it carries a $4.93\%$ relative $L^{2}$ error on the
canonical draw --- so this is the opposite regime to a
near-optimal classical prior: the network is asked to repair a
large but spatially-localised dispersion error, and the
gradient gate of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hyb-gate} must confine that
repair to the under-resolved wavefronts without disturbing the
regions the coarse solve already resolves.

\paragraph{Field accuracy on the canonical BH.} The hybrid
prediction $\Phi^{\mathrm{hyb}}$ reproduces the fine FD
reference on the canonical draw with gridwise
$\mathrm{RMSD} = 6.85\times 10^{-4}$ (in field units,
$|\Phi^{\mathrm{f}}|_{\max} \approx 1$), against the coarse-up
baseline's $\mathrm{RMSD} = 6.95\times 10^{-3}$ on the same
draw. The gated neural residual therefore suppresses the
second-order coarse-prior discretisation error by a factor of
$10.1$ at this $k = 4$ coarsening on the canonical BH; the
population-averaged ratio over the 100-BH test set is given
below. Relative $L^{2}$ errors across the same $(x, t)$ grid
are $0.486\%$ (hybrid) and $4.93\%$ (coarse-up baseline), the
same $10.1\times$ reduction.
Figure~\ref{fig:hyb-snapshots} compares the hybrid prediction
against the fine FD reference at four time slices across
$t \in [0, 50]\,M$ (top row) and plots the pointwise absolute
residual $|\Phi^{\mathrm{f}} - \Phi^{\mathrm{hyb}}|$ at the
same four times (bottom row). The hybrid and FD curves overlay
within the line thickness across the entire domain, with the
residual concentrated on the outgoing wavefronts where the
coarse prior dephases and near-zero in the smooth interior and
causal exterior.

\paragraph{The gradient gate: where the correction is applied.}
The gate $g = |\nabla\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}| /
(|\nabla\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}| + s)$ of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:hyb-gate} is built on the hypothesis that the
coarse-prior error is large exactly where the prior gradient is
large. Figure~\ref{fig:hyb-grad} tests this directly: a
per-cell scatter of the coarse-prior error
$|\Phi^{\mathrm{f}} - \mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}|$ against the
prior gradient magnitude $|\nabla\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}|$
on the canonical BH has a log--log Pearson correlation of
$r = 0.917$: the prior fails on the high-gradient wavefronts and
is accurate in the low-gradient smooth and causal regions,
exactly the structure the gate is designed to exploit.
Table~\ref{tab:hyb-gate-decile} stratifies the domain by prior
gradient and confirms that the hybrid's gain follows the gate:
in the top gradient decile (the wavefronts) the gated residual
cuts the mean error from $1.30\times 10^{-2}$ to
$1.06\times 10^{-3}$, a factor of $12.3$, and the reduction
declines monotonically with gradient to $2.6\times$ in the
sixth decile, vanishing below the median where the prior
gradient --- and the prior error --- are already at the floor.
The gate scale $s = 10^{-3}$ is not hand-tuned:
App.~\ref{app:gate-scale} derives it from the prior gradient
distribution alone (the $68$th percentile of
$|\nabla\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}|$) and confirms, by re-gating
the single trained model, that it sits within $2\%$ of the global
field-RMSD optimum.

\paragraph{Gate confinement and residual speckle.} The other
half of the gate's job is to protect the regions the prior
already resolves from spurious network output. Over the
$60.5\%$ of the canonical domain where the coarse-prior error
is below $10^{-5}$ --- the smooth interior and the causal
exterior ahead of the light cone --- the gated hybrid leaves a
mean error of $1.11\times 10^{-6}$ against the prior's
$1.77\times 10^{-7}$. The gate does not make these
already-accurate cells better; what it does is hold the
network's residual speckle there to the $10^{-6}$ level, three
orders of magnitude below the wavefront correction and far
below the signal, rather than letting an ungated operator
inject full-amplitude speckle into machine-exact regions. In
the deep causal exterior $x > 80\,M$, where the field amplitude
is $\sim\!2\times 10^{-16}$, the hybrid residual is
$\sim\!2.4\times 10^{-11}$: a large ratio over the prior in
relative terms but absolutely negligible against any
observable. This confinement is the reason the global field
gains below do not come at the cost of polluting the
QNM-bearing late-time tail.

\paragraph{Population field accuracy.} Averaged over the 100-BH
in-distribution test set, the gridwise field MSE is
$9.80\times 10^{-7}$ for the hybrid and $7.76\times 10^{-5}$
for the coarse-up baseline, a factor-of-$79$ reduction; in
relative $L^{2}$ this is $0.672\%$ vs $6.11\%$, a factor of
$9.1$. The gain is consistent across the whole training
distribution rather than an artefact of the canonical draw
($10.1\times$ on RMSD there, $9.1\times$ in the population
relative $L^{2}$), and is largest where the coarse-prior
dispersion error is largest.

\paragraph{Population QNM scatter.} Across the 100-BH
in-distribution test set the median M4 percentage errors at
$\xq = 2\,M$ are $(|\Delta\omega/\omega|, |\Delta\tau/\tau|) =
(0.049\%, 0.263\%)$ on the hybrid and $(0.051\%, 0.174\%)$ on
the coarse-up baseline; the two are interleaved at the
extractor floor, the hybrid marginally better in median $\omega$
and marginally worse in median $\tau$.
Figure~\ref{fig:hyb-pop} plots the hybrid M4 errors against
the coarse-up baseline M4 errors per BH on the test set, colour
coded by mass $M$, on logarithmic axes. The bulk of the
population clusters at the joint extractor floor
($10^{-2}$--$10^{-1}\%$ in both coordinates). The handful of
draws far from the floor in either direction are cases where the
single-window M4 fit is ill-conditioned: they appear as outliers
on the fine FD reference as well, and so reflect the extractor
rather than the hybrid field. We therefore do not claim a QNM
advantage at this observer --- the single-observer QNM is fixed
by the prior and the extractor, and the gated network neither
systematically improves nor degrades it.

\paragraph{Where the hybrid does and does not win.} The
honest summary is that at the QNM-relevant observer
$\xq = 2\,M$ the coarse-up baseline already achieves the
plateau-extractor precision floor on most BHs of the test
population (median M4 $|\Delta\omega/\omega| \approx 0.051\%$,
within a factor of a few of the fine FD reference's $0.014\%$),
and the hybrid residual correction does not improve --- and in
the median marginally perturbs $\tau$ at --- the QNM observable
at this observer.
The hybrid's value is unambiguously in the global field: the
field MSE is reduced by $79\times$ in the population mean and
the relative $L^{2}$ error by $9.1\times$, which
matters for downstream observables that depend on the global
field (energy and angular-momentum integrals, multipole
overlaps, residual mismatch against waveform templates) rather
than on a single observer slice. We explicitly do not claim a
worst-case QNM-tail suppression: the extreme draws in
Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-pop} are ill-conditioned single-window fits
that appear on the fine FD reference as well, and so reflect
the extractor rather than the surrogate. The clean statement is
that, from a deliberately weak $1/64$-cost classical prior, the
gated residual operator delivers a large and consistent
global-field gain while leaving the single-observer QNM at the
floor the prior already reaches.

\paragraph{Training history.} Figure~\ref{fig:hyb-loss} shows
the per-epoch training and validation MSE on the gated
Richardson residual $g\,\delta\Phi_{\theta}$ against
$\delta\Phi_{\mathrm{R}}$. The single Adam phase reduces the
validation MSE by more than two orders of magnitude from
$9.0\times 10^{-5}$ at the first epoch to a best of
$4.0\times 10^{-7}$ near epoch $96$, with the training and
validation curves tracking together (no visible overfitting);
the final-epoch value is $1.8\times 10^{-6}$ and we report the
best checkpoint. We did not run an L-BFGS phase: on this small
network ($1.13\times 10^{6}$ parameters) and smooth, gated
target, $100$ Adam epochs reach the validation floor, and a
trial L-BFGS phase delivered no measurable gain. The lesson is
consistent with the FNO of Sec.~\ref{sec:res-fno}, where we did
not attempt L-BFGS for the different reason of parameter count.
