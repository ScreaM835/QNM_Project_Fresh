\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

We report four numerical tests of the pipeline. The first two revisit
the Schwarzschild finite-radius problem, first as a faithful
reproduction of \citet{Patel2024pinn} and then with the enhanced
forward PINN. The third test evaluates the Schwarzschild hFNO on the
canonical configuration and on a $100$-BH test population. The fourth
test applies the hFNO construction to the hyperboloidal Kerr
Teukolsky problem and targets the spin-dependent $(4,2,0)$ mode at
$\scri$.

Table~\ref{tab:results-summary} summarises the main field and QNM
errors. Field errors are measured against the FD reference for the
Schwarzschild experiments and against the fine hyperboloidal solve for
Kerr. QNM errors are reported under the convention of
Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}, with the selected estimator shown in
parentheses. The Schwarzschild QNM errors use the Leaver values
$M\omega_0^R = 0.3737$ and $\tau_0/M = 11.241$ at $\xq = 10\,M$.
The Kerr errors use the spin-dependent Leaver value of the target
$(4,2,0)$ mode and are read at $\scri$. Per-estimator breakdowns and
plateau diagnostics are given in the corresponding subsections and in
Appendix~\ref{app:supp}.

\subsection{Faithful reproduction of the prior-work baseline}
\label{sec:res-repro}

We first reproduce the prior-work baseline of
\citet{Patel2024pinn}, the closest previous time-domain PINN study
of the Schwarzschild $\ell=2$ Regge--Wheeler and Zerilli problems.
This check is made before introducing the enhanced training schedule
of Sec.~\ref{sec:res-fwd}. The reproduction keeps the architecture,
loss weights, optimiser schedule and collocation strategy of
\citet{Patel2024pinn}, with the implementation-level differences
stated in Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}. It reduces the reported RL2 field
errors by factors of about $11$ for Zerilli and $9$ for
Regge--Wheeler. Table~\ref{tab:repro-summary} gives the field and
QNM errors for both potentials.

\paragraph{Field accuracy.} At matched settings the reproduction
reaches $\mathrm{RL2} = 2.61\%$ (RMSD $3.68\times 10^{-3}$) on
the Zerilli potential and $\mathrm{RL2} = 2.67\%$
(RMSD $3.77\times 10^{-3}$) on the Regge--Wheeler potential,
against $28.06\%$ and $23.59\%$ respectively in
\citet[Table~2]{Patel2024pinn}, a factor of $\sim\!11$ (Zerilli)
and $\sim\!9$ (Regge--Wheeler) at identical architecture, loss
weights and optimiser budget. The remaining differences are
implementation-level, from double-precision training and the
outgoing-pulse initial velocity of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}. The
snapshot overlays of Figs.~\ref{fig:repro-zer-field}
and~\ref{fig:repro-rw-field} are visually indistinguishable from
the reference, and the pointwise error peaks at
$\sim\!1.6\times 10^{-2}$ on the outgoing wavefront.

\paragraph{QNM accuracy.} Table~\ref{tab:repro-qnm} applies the
two extraction methods used by \citet{Patel2024pinn} (M1 and M2,
their ``PINN1''/``PINN2'') to the reproduced fields at
$\xq = 10\,M$, with the ringdown waveforms and damped-cosine
reconstructions in Fig.~\ref{fig:repro-ringdown}. The frequencies agree at the
one-to-two-percent level on both potentials; the damping time,
the observable most sensitive to field quality, improves
several-fold, from $14.42\%$ to $1.27\%$ (Zerilli) and from
$5.39\%$ to $1.56\%$ (Regge--Wheeler, M2).

\paragraph{Isospectral consistency.} The two potentials share
the same QNM spectrum \citep{Chandrasekhar1975}. The reproduced
PINNs recover compatible damping times from both sectors, with M2
errors of $1.27\%$ for Zerilli and $1.56\%$ for Regge--Wheeler.
This contrasts with the larger spread in \citet[Table~3]{Patel2024pinn},
where the corresponding PINN2 errors are $14.42\%$ and $5.39\%$.
Having established the same behaviour in both sectors, the remainder
of this paper uses the Zerilli equation as the representative
Schwarzschild problem.

\subsection{Enhanced forward PINN}
\label{sec:res-fwd}

We next test whether improved optimisation and residual-greedy
sampling reduce the forward-field error at fixed network architecture,
and whether the improved field yields more accurate QNM extraction.
Starting from the faithful reproduction of Sec.~\ref{sec:res-repro},
the enhanced forward PINN doubles the L-BFGS budget and replaces
uniform resampling with residual-greedy sampling
(Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}), while keeping the architecture and loss
weights unchanged. Table~\ref{tab:fwd-summary} gives the field and
QNM errors, and Figs.~\ref{fig:fwd-field}--\ref{fig:fwd-ringdown}
show the field, training history and observer waveform.

\paragraph{Field accuracy.} On the FD grid $x \in [-50,150]\,M$,
$t \in [0,50]\,M$ the enhanced PINN reaches
$\mathrm{RMSD} = 8.17\times 10^{-4}$ and $\mathrm{RL2} = 0.58\%$
against the FD reference (mean-absolute difference
$4.99\times 10^{-4}$; peak $|\Phi| \approx 0.7$ on the domain).
This is a factor of $\sim\!50$ below the
$\mathrm{RL2} = 28.06\%$ of
\citet[Table~2, Zerilli row]{Patel2024pinn}. It also improves on
the faithful reproduction of Sec.~\ref{sec:res-repro}, reducing the
RL2 error from $2.61\%$ to $0.58\%$, and lowers the mean-absolute
difference by a factor of $\sim\!36$ against their
$1.82\times 10^{-2}$. The snapshots of Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-field} are
visually indistinguishable from the reference. The pointwise error is
concentrated along the wavefronts in the snapshot panels and in the
global error map of Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-heatmap}.

\paragraph{QNM accuracy.} We apply the five extraction methods
of Sec.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods} to the observer time series
$\Phi_\theta(\xq = 10\,M, t)$ of Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-ringdown}.
Table~\ref{tab:fwd-qnm} collects the result, and the plateau
diagnostics behind the M4 and M5 estimates are in
Appendix~\ref{app:supp-plateau}.

The extraction results show two distinct behaviours. The M4 plateau estimate reaches
$0.029\%$ in $\omega$, an order of magnitude tighter than any
single-window method and the reported frequency, whereas the
Leaver-closest damping time is M2 ($1.99\%$), so $\omega$ and
$\tau$ are taken from different estimators under the selection
rule of Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}. M3 is ill-conditioned on this
waveform, selecting poles whose fitted amplitudes are near numerical
roundoff.

\paragraph{Error budget.} For the reported frequency, the M4
central-value bias is $1.1\times10^{-4}$ in $M\omega$
($0.029\%$), while the M4 plateau spread is
$\sigma_\omega = 2.0\times10^{-4}$ ($0.054\%$). The frequency
measurement is therefore limited by fit-window sensitivity rather
than by the field error alone. For the M4 damping time the ordering
is reversed. Its plateau spread is only
$\sigma_\tau/\tau \simeq 0.2\%$, while the central-value bias is
$2.65\%$. The M4 damping time is therefore limited by the field and
template rather than by window selection. The Leaver-closest damping
time is instead the M2 value, $1.99\%$, as reported in
Table~\ref{tab:fwd-summary}.

\subsection{Schwarzschild hFNO}
\label{sec:res-hyb}

The model keeps a $k=4$ coarse FD solve as the propagation prior,
upsamples it to the fine grid and learns only the coarse-to-fine
residual. The training target is obtained label-free by Richardson
extrapolation from two coarse solves, and the fine FD solution is used
only for evaluation. We test the canonical configuration
$(M,x_0,\sigma)=(1,4,5)$ and a $100$-BH Sobol test population drawn
from the same parameter ranges as the training set. Extraction is
performed at $\xq=10\,M$ over the model's $[10,100]\,M$ evolution.
Table~\ref{tab:hyb-summary} gives the field and QNM errors, and
Figs.~\ref{fig:hyb-snapshots}--\ref{fig:hyb-ringdown-xq2} show the
field, training history and observer waveform.

\paragraph{Field accuracy.} On the canonical configuration the
Schwarzschild hFNO reaches $\mathrm{RMSD}=1.70\times10^{-3}$ and
$\mathrm{RL2}=1.24\%$ against the fine FD reference, compared with
$1.53\times10^{-2}$ and $11.20\%$ for the coarse-up baseline. This
is a factor of $9.0$ in RMSD. Over the $100$-BH test population the
hFNO has mean $\mathrm{RL2}=1.85\%$, compared with $13.39\%$ for
the coarse-up baseline, and reduces the mean-square field error by a
factor of $46$. The error maps show that the hFNO removes most of the
coherent dispersion bands of the coarse prior, while a smaller
residual remains along the wavefronts and in the late ring.

\paragraph{QNM accuracy.} Table~\ref{tab:hyb-canonical} reports
population-median QNM errors at $\xq=10\,M$ over the $[10,100]\,M$
evolution. On the M4 plateau the hFNO reaches $0.152\%$ in frequency
and $0.528\%$ in damping time. The fine FD reference gives the
extractor floor, $0.064\%$ and $0.092\%$. The hFNO therefore tracks
the fine-reference frequency closely, but its damping-time error
remains above the extractor floor. The canonical waveform in
Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-ringdown-xq2} shows the same mechanism
qualitatively, namely a small late-ring residual that the plateau fit
cannot average out. The canonical QNM entry in

comes from the two-edge scan, while its M4 damping time uses the
usable-signal cutoff at $t\simeq80\,M$. The population statistics use
the full $[10,100]\,M$ extraction protocol.

\paragraph{The gradient gate.} The gate of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hyb-gate}
is justified by the coarse-prior error being largest where the prior
gradient is large. The canonical scatter plot confirms this relation
with log-log Pearson $r=0.921$, and the error reduction is strongest
in the high-gradient region, reaching a factor $14.1$ in the top
gradient decile (Appendix~\ref{app:supp-gate}). The scale
$s=10^{-3}$ is fixed label-free from the prior-gradient distribution
(Appendix~\ref{app:gate-scale}). The gate suppresses unnecessary
corrections in smooth regions, but it does not remove the residual
late-ring error that limits the damping-time extraction.

\paragraph{Training history.} Over $100$ Adam epochs, the validation
MSE on the gated Richardson residual falls from $3.59\times10^{-4}$
to a best value of $1.79\times10^{-6}$, with train and validation
curves tracking closely (Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-loss}).

\paragraph{Field accuracy.} Across the test set, the median relative
$L^{2}$ error decreases from $59.5\%$ for the coarse prior to
$0.78\%$ for the Kerr hFNO. The ratio of the population medians is
$77$. The hFNO bin median rises modestly from $0.70\%$ at low spin
to $1.08\%$ in the high-spin bin. The Richardson target has a median
error of $0.008\%$, well below the hFNO error, showing that the
remaining discrepancy is set by the learned model rather than by
the accuracy of the training target.

\paragraph{QNM accuracy.} The frequency improves in every spin bin.
In the high-spin bin, its median error decreases from $4.71\%$ for
the coarse prior to $0.38\%$ for the hFNO, a factor of $12$. The
fine-solve extraction reaches $0.02\%$, so the hFNO remains above
the estimator floor. Across the full test set, the median frequency
error decreases from $0.59\%$ to $0.33\%$, compared with $0.05\%$
for the fine solve.

The damping time does not show the same improvement. Its median
error increases from $0.52\%$ for the coarse prior to $1.52\%$ for
the hFNO, and the hFNO is less accurate than the prior in every spin
bin. The late-time floor in Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-ringdown} is
consistent with the gridwise MSE giving greater weight to the
high-amplitude burst than to the low-amplitude exponential tail.

\paragraph{Framework extension.} Taken together, these results show that
this hybrid concept can also be applied to Kerr after retraining on
Teukolsky data and adapting the model to the complex hyperboloidal
field. The Kerr hFNO reduces the median field error in every spin bin
and gives sub-percent median frequency errors across the sampled spin
range without using fine-grid fields in its training loss. It does
not improve the damping time, which remains limited by the late-time
residual.
