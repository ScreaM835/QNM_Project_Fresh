\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

Section~\ref{sec:res-fwd} reports the forward PINN field
accuracy against the FD reference and the five-method extraction
of $(\omega,\tau)$ from $\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$.
Section~\ref{sec:res-inv} reports the six inverse PINN variants:
mass recovery, the primary post-hoc QNM estimate, and the
$A_{\mathrm{ring}}$--$\tau$ degeneracy of the template loss.

Throughout this paper the \emph{forward PINN} refers to the
network of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn} that solves the Zerilli
equation forward at fixed $M$ from PDE residual plus IC/BC
losses, and the \emph{inverse PINN} refers to the joint
PDE-plus-data network of Sec.~\ref{sec:inv-pinn} that
simultaneously recovers $M$ and a single-mode template
$(\omega_{\mathrm{learned}},\tau_{\mathrm{learned}},
A_{\mathrm{ring}},\phi_0)$ from $\Nobs$ noisy field samples.

\subsection{Forward PINN}
\label{sec:res-fwd}

The converged forward PINN matches the FD reference to high
accuracy on the full simulation domain. With the architecture and
training schedule of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn} the field-accuracy
metrics, evaluated on the FD grid
$x \in [-50,150]\,M$, $t \in [0,50]\,M$, are
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{RMSD} = 8.17\times 10^{-4}, \qquad
  \mathrm{MAD}  = 4.99\times 10^{-4}, \qquad
  \mathrm{RL2}  = 5.79\times 10^{-3},
  \label{eq:fwd-metrics}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{RMSD}$ and $\mathrm{MAD}$ are the root-mean-square
and mean-absolute differences $\Phi_\theta - \Phi_{\mathrm{FD}}$ in
absolute units (peak $|\Phi| \approx 0.7$ on the domain), and
$\mathrm{RL2}$ is their relative $L^2$ norm.
This $\mathrm{RL2} = 0.58\%$ is a factor of $\sim\!50$ smaller
than the $\mathrm{RL2} = 28.06\%$ of
\citet[Table~2, Zerilli row]{Patel2024pinn} on the same problem,
and the corresponding $\mathrm{MAD}$ improvement is
$5.0\times 10^{-4}$ versus their $1.82\times 10^{-2}$, a factor
of~$\sim\!36$. We attribute the improvement to the larger number
of L-BFGS iterations and the greedy adaptive sampling of
Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}; see also the discussion in
Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}. Field accuracy at the snapshot times
is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-field}, the global pointwise error
in Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-heatmap}, and the per-component loss
histories underlying Eq.~\eqref{eq:fwd-metrics} in
Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-loss}.

Three points stand out. First, the M4 plateau
estimate of $\omega$ achieves $0.029\%$ accuracy, more than an
order of magnitude tighter than any single-window method, and the
plateau standard deviation $\sigma_\omega \sim 2\times 10^{-4}$ is
comparable to the residual bias of the plateau mean against the
Leaver value ($|0.37359 - 0.3737| \approx 1.1\times 10^{-4}$).
Second, M2 on the M4 plateau window reduces the
$\omega$ error of M2 on the wide window by a factor of five (from
$1.54\%$ to $0.31\%$) but increases the $\tau$ error fourfold (from
$1.99\%$ to $7.38\%$), motivating the asymmetric reporting
convention of Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}. Third, M3
returns frequencies at machine-precision amplitudes ($|c_k| \sim
10^{-23}$), reflecting the fact that the analytic ESPRIT problem
is ill-conditioned on a $\Phi(\xq,t)$ whose dominant component is
already a clean damped sinusoid; we quote M3 in
Appendix~\ref{app:esprit} for completeness only. The M5 plateau in
the $(t_0,\tend)$ plane spans the corner of the grid closest to
the QNM regime, $t_0 \in [10,16.7]\,M$ and $\tend \in [42,50]\,M$,
exactly as expected from Eq.~\eqref{eq:timescales}, since the
plateau abuts the lower-$t_0$ edge once the prompt burst has
passed ($t_0 \gtrsim t_{\mathrm{burst}}$) and extends to the
upper-$\tend$ edge of the simulation domain since
$\tend \ll t_{\mathrm{tail}}$ on $[0,50]\,M$.

The cross-method picture for variant D mirrors the forward run
of Table~\ref{tab:fwd-qnm}: M4 and M5 yield mutually consistent
$\omega$ estimates at the $0.30\%$ level with plateau standard deviations
$\sim 3\text{--}4\times 10^{-5}$, M1 is competitive on $\omega$
($0.29\%$) but loses on $\tau$ as expected from the
log-line slope's sensitivity to the prompt burst, and M2 on the
wide window is the best $\tau$ estimator ($1.59\%$). For
$\tau$, M4 and M5 collapse to the template-degeneracy value
$\sim 9.48\,M$ (the same value the trained scalar
$\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$ converged to in
Fig.~\ref{fig:inv-conv}), while M2 on the wide window recovers
$11.06\,M$ from the network's own field $\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$;
this is the $A_{\mathrm{ring}}$--$\tau$ degeneracy of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring}, discussed quantitatively in the
paragraph below (with Tables~\ref{tab:inv-learned}
and~\ref{tab:tau-degeneracy}) and visualised in
Fig.~\ref{fig:inv-conv}. The reporting convention of
Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting} therefore takes $\omega$ from M4 and
$\tau$ from M2-wide, as already used in
Table~\ref{tab:inv-summary}.

Figure~\ref{fig:inv-conv} shows the parameter histories of variant
D on a logged-sample horizontal axis, so the L-BFGS phase
($10{,}000\to 25{,}000$ raw iterations) is compressed into the
rightmost few sample points and appears as the apparent step at
the right edge of each panel. Four of the five learned parameters
behave as expected: $M$ (top-left) overshoots to $\sim 1.15$
during Adam, returns to $\sim 1.04$, and converges to its
final value $1.003$ ($0.34\%$ from the true mass) under L-BFGS;
$\omega_{\mathrm{learned}}$ (top-right) climbs from $0.30$ to
$\sim 0.355$ during Adam, drifts down to $\sim 0.31$, and
recovers to its final value $0.3748$ ($0.30\%$ from the Leaver
value) under L-BFGS, and the apparent ``ends low'' behaviour is
an artefact of the logged-sample axis rather than the data;
$A_{\mathrm{ring}}$ (middle-right) rises from $0.5$ to a final
value $2.93$, overshooting to $\sim 3.29$ during early L-BFGS
before settling back; and $\phi$ (bottom-left) settles to
$-0.85$. The exception is $\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$
(middle-left), which drifts from $\tau_{\mathrm{init}} = 8.0\,M$
down to $\sim 7.3\,M$ during Adam, climbs to $9.48\,M$ via
L-BFGS, and finishes $15.7\%$ below the Leaver value. This is
the $A_{\mathrm{ring}}$--$\tau$ degeneracy of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring} introduced in Sec.~\ref{sec:inv-pinn};
the bottom-right panel confirms it visually, since the template
at the final learned scalars is visually indistinguishable from
the FD truth at $\xq=10\,M$ on $t \gtrsim 18\,M$ even with
$\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$ off by $1.8\,M$. The network field
$\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$ is constrained by the PDE residual and the
noisy data and exhibits the physical decay rate; the
post-hoc M2 wide-window fit on $\Phi_\theta$ recovers
$\tau = 11.06\,M$ ($1.6\%$ from the Leaver value) as listed in
Table~\ref{tab:inv-summary}. The learned and post-hoc values of
$\tau$ are tabulated side by side for all six variants in
Table~\ref{tab:tau-degeneracy}.

All six variants reduce the $20\%$ mass initialisation error
$|M_{\mathrm{init}}-M_{\mathrm{true}}|/M_{\mathrm{true}} = 0.20$ to
$\le 1.5\%$ within the allotted $25{,}000$ optimiser iterations. The single-mode-template
variants (baseline, A, C, D) recover $M$ to within $0.5\%$;
variants B and E are worse, with $1.25\%$ and $1.44\%$
respectively. Variant B places weight $100$ on a template the
optimiser cannot satisfy with a single damped sinusoid (the
prompt burst is still present at $\tring = 10\,M$,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:timescales}), so field and mass accuracy are
sacrificed to template accuracy. In E the four additional learnable parameters of the
overtone slow mass convergence, but the structural information
added by the second mode improves the primary $\tau$ estimate
substantially (1.3\% vs.\ 5.3\% for baseline; see below).

We quote $\omega$ from the M4 plateau mean (Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}).
The plateau standard deviation $\sigma_\omega$ is also a useful
diagnostic. For variants A, D
and E the plateau is tight ($\sigma_\omega/\omega \le 1\%$) and
$\omega$ is recovered to $0.3$--$2.6\%$. For baseline and C the
plateau is wide ($\sigma_\omega/\omega \approx 0.44$ and $0.34$
respectively), signalling that the two-mode NLS does not find a
stable $(\omega_0,\tau_0)$ across $t_0$; the mean is not a
trustworthy estimate, and the M2-wide value of $\omega$ would in
fact be more accurate. For B the two-mode NLS does not converge to
a finite minimum on any window in the scan, so M4 is undefined.
$\sigma_\omega$ is therefore a quality diagnostic. A small
$\sigma_\omega$ accepts the estimate, a large or undefined
$\sigma_\omega$ rejects it (cf.\ Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}).
Variant D ranks first on the combination of $M$, $\omega$ and
$\tau$. The single-factor changes A and
C are individually beneficial (A improves $\tau$ and the M4
plateau; C improves $M$); B is strictly worse than baseline on
$\tau$ and M4 plateau quality at no benefit to $M$; E is
competitive on the primary QNM numbers but pays a $\sim 1\%$
penalty on $M$ and $\omega$ for the extra template freedom.
Variant D combines the three single-factor improvements of A--C:
it has the tightest plateau ($\sigma_\omega = 4\times 10^{-5}$)
and the smallest $\omega$ error ($0.30\%$) of the six runs.

The pattern in Table~\ref{tab:tau-degeneracy} groups by
$\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}}$. For baseline and C the learned and
post-hoc $\tau$ agree to within $0.06\,M$, but \emph{both} are
biased high by $\sim 0.6\,M$ relative to the Leaver value; the post-hoc M2 fit is constrained by the
network's own field, which has been trained against the inverse
template, so when the template is weak ($\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}} =
1$) the network does not correct the template's late-time
overshoot. For variant D, raising $\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}}$ to
$100$ \emph{and} increasing $\tring$ to $18\,M$ shifts
$\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$ down to $9.48\,M$ along the
degeneracy with $A_{\mathrm{ring}} = 2.93$, while the post-hoc M2
fit to the converged $\Phi_\theta$ recovers $11.06\,M$. The
network-level field $\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$ has therefore learned the
correct decay rate from the noisy data and the PDE, despite the
template parameters drifting in the opposite direction; the
post-hoc extraction recovers the field's own decay rate, not the
template's. This separation of the trained network's field from
the trained template parameters is the reason for the reporting
convention of Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}, and it is also the reason
variant D wins on $\omega$. With the template at $\tring = 18\,M$
the prompt-burst contamination of the M4 plateau scan is
suppressed (cf.\ Eq.~\eqref{eq:timescales}), and the network's
$\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$ converges to a QNM-dominated waveform on
which the M4 two-mode NLS finds a tight plateau, despite the
drift of the template parameters themselves.

The 2-window hard partition (Cur-2w) is essentially tied with the
single-window baseline on the full-domain RMSD
($8.45 \times 10^{-4}$ versus $8.17 \times 10^{-4}$, a $3\%$
increase) and recovers the same headline QNM at the M5 plateau
($M\omega = 0.3729$, error $0.22\%$ versus $0.23\%$ for the
baseline). The M4 plateau, by contrast, degrades from
$\sigma_\omega = 2\times 10^{-4}$ to
$\sigma_\omega = 9\times 10^{-4}$ and the M4 mean drifts from
$0.029\%$ to $0.79\%$, a 27-fold loss of plateau quality. The
per-window breakdown of Table~\ref{tab:cur-summary} is
informative: $W_{1} = [0,25]\,M$ alone
reaches RMSD $5.16\times 10^{-4}$, the lowest sub-domain RMSD
seen anywhere in this work and a $37\%$ improvement on the
baseline RMSD evaluated over the same sub-domain (which we read
off the baseline's pointwise error map; the time-restricted
baseline RMSD on $[0,25]\,M$ is $8.2\times 10^{-4}$).
The curriculum strategy does therefore deliver the predicted
local benefit on a smaller temporal sub-domain. The benefit is
neutralised by $W_{2} = [25,50]\,M$, which sees RMSD
$10.8\times 10^{-4}$, larger than the full-domain baseline and
roughly $2\times$ larger than $W_{1}$. The $W_{2}$ error
inherits both (i) the residual error of $W_{1}$ at $t = 25\,M$
through the IC transfer of Eq.~\eqref{eq:cur-ic} and (ii) the
greater dynamic-range demand of resolving the late-time
exponentially-decaying ringdown after the prompt burst has
exited.

The 3-window hard partition (Cur-3w) makes the same diagnostic
sharper. Per-window RMSD grows monotonically with window index,
$W_{1} \to W_{2} \to W_{3} = 5.5 \to 9.2 \to 13.2$ (units of
$10^{-4}$). Each additional stitching boundary contributes a
fresh interpolation error to the IC of the next window, and the
errors compound. The full-domain RMSD ends $19\%$ above the
single-window baseline, and the M1 $\tau$ error worsens from
$2.95\%$ (Cur-2w) to $3.83\%$ (Cur-3w), tracking the additional
late-time stitching error. The M4 and M5 plateaux of
Table~\ref{tab:cur-qnm} continue to form (no plateau failure)
but with $\sigma_\omega = 5\!-\!7 \times 10^{-4}$ and a mean
bias $0.64\%$, two-and-a-half times the bias of the
single-window baseline. The M2 wide-window $\omega$ and $\tau$
are largely insensitive to which curriculum variant is used,
which is consistent with the wide window averaging over
the per-window errors.

The soft-overlap variant (Cur-soft) does not improve the
hard-partition result. It performs worse than Cur-2w on every
extraction column except M2 wide-window $\tau$, which it
marginally ties. The full-domain RMSD is $\sim 4\times$ the
single-window baseline; both $W_{1}$ and the overlap region
$[20,30]\,M$ are individually worse than the corresponding
hard-partition entries. Most strikingly, the M4 plateau scan
fails to identify any contiguous block of size
$\lfloor N/4 \rfloor$ for which
$\mathrm{std}(\omega)/|\bar\omega| < 3\%$, i.e.\ no plateau
forms; the table reports the relative-scatter floor
($\sigma_\omega/|\omega| > 0.06$) and the corresponding
single-best two-mode fit, $M\omega = 0.278$ at $25.7\%$ error,
which is the standard manifestation of the no-plateau condition
of Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}. M5 finds a small valid 2-D plateau
($t_0 \in [10,16.7]\,M$, $\tend \in [30,38]\,M$) but with
$\sigma_\omega = 4\times 10^{-2}$, two orders of magnitude
larger than the baseline, signalling that even the 2-D scan
struggles to find a stable region. Two factors plausibly
contribute. First, the continuity-anchor loss
Eq.~\eqref{eq:cur-anchor} imposes
$201 \times 11 = 2{,}211$ additional point constraints on
$\Phi_{\theta_{2}}$ across the overlap, drawn from the trained
$\Phi_{\theta_{1}}$, which itself carries non-zero error
($\sim 1.7\times 10^{-3}$ on the overlap as measured against
the FD reference); the anchors therefore propagate an error
floor into $W_{2}$ that the PDE constraint cannot reduce below.
Second, the optimiser has to balance the PDE loss, the
two IC residuals at $t = 20\,M$, and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cont}}$
simultaneously, and the additional loss term modifies the
relative weighting from the
$\boldsymbol{\lambda} = (100,100,100,1,100,1,1)$ baseline that
was tuned for the single-window setup.

The curriculum strategy therefore meets its narrow design
objective (lower per-window error on the early sub-domain) but
does not translate into a lower full-domain RMSD or a tighter
QNM extraction once the windows are stitched. The dominant cost
is the IC-transfer interpolation error, which compounds linearly
with the number of stitching boundaries and grows at late times
as the signal amplitude decays. A useful baseline against which
the curriculum should be compared is the
greedy-adaptive-sampling forward PINN of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}
itself, which delivers the same factor-of-two reduction in
late-time error without paying the per-stitch interpolation cost,
by concentrating $0.3\,N_{r}$ of the collocation budget on the
high-residual late-time band within a single network. We discuss
the implications for the broader curriculum-PINN literature in
Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}.
