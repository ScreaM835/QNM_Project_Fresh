\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

The four experiments below are presented in a single uniform
format so that the reader can compare the same metrics directly
across models. Each subsection opens with a short statement of
the question it addresses and a summary table giving the field
accuracy (RMSD and relative $L^{2}$ norm against the FD
reference) together with the best-of-suite QNM error under the
reporting convention of Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}, each labelled
with the estimator of Sec.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods} that attains
it. Where a coarse prior and a fine reference are defined (the
hybrid and Kerr surrogates) they appear as additional rows of
the same table. The supporting plots follow, and each
subsection closes with a paragraph on the field accuracy and a
paragraph on the QNM accuracy, the latter carrying the
per-estimator breakdown, with any model-specific diagnostics
after them. Sections~\ref{sec:res-repro}--\ref{sec:res-fwd}
treat one black hole at a time (reproduction, enhanced forward);
Sec.~\ref{sec:res-hyb} evaluates the hybrid surrogate on the
canonical BH and on a $100$-BH test population; and
Sec.~\ref{sec:res-kerr} generalises the hybrid to a population
of rotating (Kerr) black holes. All QNM errors are relative to
the Leaver continued-fraction values $M\omega_{0} = 0.3737$,
$\tau_{0}/M = 11.241$ and are read at the common observer
$\xq = 10\,M$. Supporting scans and diagnostics are collected in
Appendix~\ref{app:supp}. Table~\ref{tab:results-summary} collects
the principal numbers for all models at a glance, and the
per-model detail follows in the subsections below.

\subsection{Faithful reproduction of the prior-work baseline}
\label{sec:res-repro}

We first establish that the pipeline reproduces the prior-work
baseline of \citet{Patel2024pinn} --- the only previous
time-domain PINN treatment of these $\ell = 2$ master
equations --- before any methodological change is introduced;
it does, improving on the published field errors by an order
of magnitude. The forward PINN is trained at their exact
settings (Table~\ref{tab:fwd-hyper}: uniform collocation
resampling, their loss weights and optimiser budget), i.e.\
without the enhancements of Sec.~\ref{sec:res-fwd}, on both
the even-parity (Zerilli) and odd-parity (Regge--Wheeler)
potentials. Table~\ref{tab:repro-summary} collects the field and
QNM accuracy for both potentials, and
Figs.~\ref{fig:repro-zer-field}--\ref{fig:repro-loss} show the
fields, the ringdown waveforms, and the training histories.

\paragraph{Field accuracy.} At matched settings the reproduction
reaches $\mathrm{RL2} = 2.61\%$ (RMSD $3.68\times 10^{-3}$) on
the Zerilli potential and $\mathrm{RL2} = 2.67\%$
(RMSD $3.77\times 10^{-3}$) on the Regge--Wheeler potential,
against $28.06\%$ and $23.59\%$ respectively in
\citet[Table~2]{Patel2024pinn}, a factor of $\sim\!11$ (Zerilli)
and $\sim\!9$ (Regge--Wheeler) at identical architecture, loss
weights and optimiser budget. The remaining differences are
implementation-level, from double-precision training and the
outgoing-pulse initial velocity of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}. The
snapshot overlays of Figs.~\ref{fig:repro-zer-field}
and~\ref{fig:repro-rw-field} are visually indistinguishable from
the reference, and the pointwise error peaks at
$\sim\!1.6\times 10^{-2}$ on the outgoing wavefront.

\paragraph{QNM accuracy.} Table~\ref{tab:repro-qnm} applies the
two extraction methods used by \citet{Patel2024pinn} (M1 and M2,
their ``PINN1''/``PINN2'') to the reproduced fields at
$\xq = 10\,M$, with the ringdown waveforms and damped-cosine
reconstructions in Figs.~\ref{fig:repro-zer-ringdown}
and~\ref{fig:repro-rw-ringdown}. The frequencies agree at the
one-to-two-percent level on both potentials; the damping time,
the observable most sensitive to field quality, improves
several-fold, from $14.42\%$ to $1.27\%$ (Zerilli) and from
$5.39\%$ to $1.56\%$ (Regge--Wheeler, M2).

\paragraph{Isospectral consistency.} The two potentials share
the same QNM spectrum \citep{Chandrasekhar1975}, so a
consistent pipeline must recover compatible $(\omega,\tau)$
from both --- and it does: the M2 damping-time errors agree to
within $0.06$ percentage points ($1.50\%$ versus $1.56\%$),
against a nine-point spread in \citet[Table~3]{Patel2024pinn}.
Having established equivalent behaviour on both potentials,
the remainder of this paper reports on the Zerilli equation.

architecture, and what QNM accuracy does that buy? Starting
from the faithful reproduction of Sec.~\ref{sec:res-repro}, the

and Figs.~\ref{fig:fwd-field}--\ref{fig:fwd-ringdown} show the

\paragraph{Field accuracy.} On the FD grid $x \in [-50,150]\,M$,
$t \in [0,50]\,M$ the enhanced PINN reaches
$\mathrm{RMSD} = 8.17\times 10^{-4}$ and $\mathrm{RL2} = 0.58\%$

snapshots of Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-field} are visually
indistinguishable from the reference, and the pointwise error
stays below $\sim\!5\times 10^{-3}$, concentrated near the
potential barrier, both at the four snapshot times and across
the full domain (Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-heatmap}).

Two points stand out. The M4 plateau estimate reaches
$0.029\%$ in $\omega$, an order of magnitude tighter than any
single-window method and the reported frequency, whereas the
Leaver-closest damping time is M2 ($1.99\%$), so $\omega$ and
$\tau$ are taken from different estimators under the selection
rule of Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}. M3 returns frequencies at
machine-precision amplitudes and is unreliable on these
near-clean waveforms.

\paragraph{Error budget.} The two measured contributions to
the frequency error rank as follows: the M4 bias against the
Leaver value is $1.1\times 10^{-4}$ in $M\omega$ ($0.029\%$),
and the fit-window systematic is $\sigma_\omega = 2\times 10^{-4}$
($0.054\%$; a lower bound, Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}). The
frequency measurement is therefore
window-limited, not field-limited. For the
M4 damping time the ordering reverses: its plateau scatter
$\sigma_\tau/\tau \approx 0.2\%$ sits against a $2.65\%$ bias, so
the M4 $\tau$ is field- and template-limited rather than
window-limited; the Leaver-closest damping time is instead M2
($1.99\%$), the value reported in Table~\ref{tab:fwd-summary}.

\subsection{Hybrid coarse-FD + neural-residual surrogate}
\label{sec:res-hyb}

The final model asks how much of the fine solver's
accuracy can be recovered from a classical solve at $1/64$ of
its cost. The hybrid surrogate of Sec.~\ref{sec:hyb-method}
keeps a coarse-grid FD solve to carry the wave propagation and
asks the network only for the pointwise coarse-to-fine residual,
estimated label-free by Richardson extrapolation of two coarse
solves (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:hyb-richardson}--\eqref{eq:hyb-residual}),
so that no fine-grid field enters training. The fine solve
appears only as the evaluation reference below, the true
high-resolution answer against which the surrogate is measured;
at deployment neither it nor the Richardson target is needed, the
trained network correcting a fresh coarse solve in a single pass.
It is evaluated on the canonical draw
$(M,x_{0},\sigma) = (1,4,5)$ and on a $100$-BH in-distribution
Sobol test set (drawn from the hybrid's own training box,
Sec.~\ref{sec:hyb-method}), at coarsening factor $k = 4$;
extraction is at the common observer $\xq = 10\,M$ shared with
the PINNs, over the model's full $[10,100]\,M$ evolution.
Quintic-spline upsampling of that $k = 4$ coarse solve to the
fine grid (the \emph{coarse-up baseline}) leaves a large but
spatially-localised dispersion error, $11.20\%$ in relative
$L^{2}$ on the canonical draw, for the gated network to repair.
Table~\ref{tab:hyb-summary} collects the field and QNM accuracy,
and Figs.~\ref{fig:hyb-snapshots}--\ref{fig:hyb-ringdown-xq2}
show the field at the snapshot times, the global error maps, the
training history, and the ringdown at the observer.

\paragraph{Field accuracy.} On the canonical BH the hybrid
reproduces the fine FD reference with gridwise
$\mathrm{RMSD} = 1.70\times 10^{-3}$ ($\mathrm{RL2} = 1.24\%$),
against $1.53\times 10^{-2}$ ($11.20\%$) for the coarse-up
baseline, a factor of $9.0$ in RMSD. Averaged over the $100$-BH
test population the same comparison is a factor of $46$ in
mean-square error ($1.85\%$ versus $13.39\%$ in relative
$L^{2}$), so the gain holds across the training distribution and
is not an artefact of the canonical draw. The residual is
concentrated on the outgoing wavefronts where the coarse prior
dephases (Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-snapshots}), and the global error
maps (Figs.~\ref{fig:hyb-heatmap} and~\ref{fig:hyb-heatmap-base})
show the hybrid suppressing the baseline's dispersion
characteristics by roughly an order of magnitude while leaving
the smooth interior and causal exterior at the prior.

\paragraph{QNM accuracy.} The relevant test for a surrogate is
whether it reproduces the spectroscopy of the fine reference it
approximates. Table~\ref{tab:hyb-canonical} reports the
population-median extraction against that reference at the
common observer over the model's full $[10,100]\,M$ evolution,
read from the ringdown of Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-ringdown-xq2}; the
M4 and M5 plateau constructions are in
Appendix~\ref{app:supp-plateau}. On the primary M4 plateau the
hybrid recovers $\omega$ to $0.152\%$ and $\tau$ to $0.528\%$,
against the reference's own $0.064\%$ and $0.092\%$, the
\emph{extractor floor} that the method reaches on the exact
field. The hybrid tracks the reference in frequency to within a
tenth of a percentage point; its damping-time error sits above
that floor, because the network's residual leaves a small
incoherent component in the late ring that the plateau fit
cannot average out, the same limitation seen in the late-ring
divergence of Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-ringdown-xq2}. The M4 plateau is
read over the usable-signal window up to $t \approx 80\,M$,
beyond which the ring reaches the noise floor.

\paragraph{The gradient gate.} The gate of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:hyb-gate} rests on the coarse-prior error being
large exactly where the prior gradient is large. The per-cell
scatter confirms this on the canonical BH (log--log Pearson
$r = 0.921$), and the hybrid's gain follows the gate, reaching a
factor $14.1$ error reduction in the top gradient decile
(Appendix~\ref{app:supp-gate}). The scale $s = 10^{-3}$ is fixed
label-free from the prior gradient distribution
(Appendix~\ref{app:gate-scale}), and the gate leaves the
already-resolved interior at the prior floor, so the field gain
does not pollute the QNM-bearing late-time tail.

\paragraph{Training history.} A single Adam phase reduces the
validation MSE on the gated Richardson residual from
$3.6\times 10^{-4}$ to $1.8\times 10^{-6}$ over $100$ epochs,
with train and validation tracking together
(Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-loss}).

\paragraph{Field accuracy.} Across the test set the coarse prior
carries a median relative $L^{2}$ field error of $59.5\%$: at
low resolution the hyperboloidal wavefront is badly
under-resolved. The hybrid reduces this to $0.78\%$, a factor of
$77$, and the error stays nearly flat across spin, from $0.70\%$
at low spin to $1.08\%$ near extremality
(Table~\ref{tab:kerr-summary}), so a single amortised operator
absorbs almost the entire spin dependence of the field. The
label-free Richardson target itself sits at $0.008\%$,
confirming that the FNO, not the reference, sets the floor. The
coarse solve's error is concentrated in the high-amplitude burst
(Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-pointwise}), which the residual correction
removes, and the reconstructed ringdown at $\scri$ overlays the
fine reference through the burst (Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-ringdown}).

\paragraph{QNM accuracy.} The amortisation is clearest at high
spin, where the coarse prior degrades fastest. For
$a/M \ge 0.8$ ($20$ black holes) the best-of-suite prior
frequency error is $4.71\%$, which the hybrid cuts to $0.38\%$,
a $12\times$ improvement approaching the fine-solve floor of
$0.02\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:kerr-summary},
Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-vs-spin}b). Pooled over all spins the median
frequency error falls from $0.59\%$ to $0.33\%$, with the fine
solve at $0.05\%$; near extremality, where the near-horizon
oscillation of Eq.~\eqref{eq:kerr-rescale} is strongest and the
coarse grid fails, the hybrid recovers more than an order of
magnitude. The damping time is the harder channel
(Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-vs-spin}c), exactly as for the Zerilli
surrogate (Sec.~\ref{sec:res-hyb}): pooled over spin the hybrid
$\tau$ error ($1.52\%$) exceeds that of the coarse prior
($0.52\%$), while the fine solve ($0.27\%$) shows that the
residual correction, tuned to the field and its leading
oscillation, does not consistently sharpen the exponential
envelope. The operator restores the phase and amplitude of the
field far better than its slowly varying decay rate, so $\tau$
remains a fine-solve quantity and the surrogate is a
frequency-and-field accelerator rather than a damping-time
estimator.

\paragraph{Verdict.} Taken together, the Kerr experiment shows
that the hybrid concept is geometry-agnostic. The same
coarse-plus-residual construction, retrained without a single
fine-grid field in its loss, transfers from a static
Schwarzschild wave equation to a rotating, complex,
hyperboloidal Teukolsky field and still delivers an
order-of-magnitude field improvement and sub-percent
frequencies across the astrophysical spin range --- a
generalisation that a per-black-hole PINN, retrained from
scratch for every spin, does not provide.
