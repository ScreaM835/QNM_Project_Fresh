\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

We state the FD reference (Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-problem}), the
forward (Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}) and inverse
(Sec.~\ref{sec:inv-pinn}) PINNs, the five extraction methods
M1--M5 (Sec.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods}), and the reporting convention
(Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}). M4 and M5 are the new estimators.

% --------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Schwarzschild Zerilli problem}
\label{sec:fwd-problem}

The forward problem solved by the PINN of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}
is the Zerilli equation Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} of
Sec.~\ref{sec:bg-zerilli}, with the polar potential
$V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli} at $\ell = 2$
and $M = 1$. The numerical domain is $x \in [-50,\,150]\,M$ and
$t \in [0,\,50]\,M$. The initial data are an outgoing Gaussian
pulse,
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(x,0) = A_0\,\exp\!\left[-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_0^2}\right],
  \qquad
  \partial_t \Phi(x,0) = -\partial_x \Phi(x,0),
  \label{eq:ic}
\end{equation}
with $A_0 = 1$, $x_0 = 4\,M$ and $\sigma_0 = 5\,M$. The right-hand side of the velocity
condition imposes a purely outgoing Gaussian, in contrast to the
quadratic $\partial_t \Phi \propto (x-x_0)^2 \Phi$ used by
\citet{Patel2024pinn}. We adopt the standard outgoing form, which is the conventional
initial condition for the Zerilli equation \citep{Berti2009review}. The asymptotic outgoing QNM
boundary conditions Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-bc} are not
imposed exactly at the truncated boundaries; instead we apply
first-order Sommerfeld absorbing conditions,
\begin{equation}
  (\partial_t - \partial_x)\Phi\bigl|_{x=x_{\min}} = 0, \qquad
  (\partial_t + \partial_x)\Phi\bigl|_{x=x_{\max}} = 0,
  \label{eq:bc-sommerfeld}
\end{equation}
identical to those used by \citet{Patel2024pinn}. The spatial
domain $[-50,150]\,M$ is sized so that the wavefront, propagating
at unit speed from $x_0 = 4\,M$, never reaches either boundary
within the simulated interval $t \in [0,50]\,M$. The right-going
and left-going arrival times are
\begin{equation}
  t_{R} = x_{\max} - x_0 = 146\,M, \qquad
  t_{L} = x_0 - x_{\min} = 54\,M,
  \label{eq:bc-arrival}
\end{equation}
both larger than the QNM extraction window $t \in [10,50]\,M$ at
the observer position $x_{\mathrm{obs}} = 10\,M$, so the residuals
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:bc-sommerfeld} act only as soft regularisers in a
region that is causally disconnected from the initial pulse on the
simulated interval, and therefore does not affect the extracted
modes.

The reference solution is obtained by a fourth-order Runge--Kutta
method-of-lines finite-difference (FD) integration of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} with $\Delta x = 0.2\,M$ and
$\Delta t = 0.1\,M$. This FD waveform is used throughout as the ground
truth for benchmarking PINN field accuracy and for cross-checking the
QNM extraction methods on a non-PINN signal. The Leaver reference
values $M\omega_0 = 0.3737$, $\tau_0/M = 11.241$ (fundamental) and
$M\omega_1 = 0.3467$, $\tau_1/M = 3.651$ (first overtone) of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-ref} are used for percentage-error reporting.
Figure~\ref{fig:waveform-3d} shows the full FD reference field
$\Phi_{\mathrm{FD}}(x, t)$ as a 3D surface, with the outgoing
Gaussian initial condition producing a right-moving free pulse and
a back-scattered left-moving component, the photon-sphere
barrier at $r \simeq 3M$ trapping a fraction of the energy that
then leaks out as the QNM ringdown along $x \simeq 0$--$15\,M$,
and the ringdown decay visible as the amplitude decrease in the
$t$ direction at the observer column $\xq = 10\,M$.

\subsection{Forward PINN}
\label{sec:fwd-pinn}

We solve the forward problem of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-problem} with a
fully-connected PINN
\citep{Raissi2017} implemented in DeepXDE \citep{Lu2021deepxde} with
the PyTorch backend.

The network architecture, output transform and weight initialiser
are chosen identically to \citet{Patel2024pinn} so that any
difference in extracted QNMs between the two works can be attributed
to the loss formulation, training schedule, sampling strategy or
extraction methodology rather than to the network itself.
The network has hidden layers $[80,40,20,10]$ with $\tanh$
activations and a single scalar output $\Phi(x,t)$ from input
$(x,t) \in \mathbb{R}^{2}$, giving $4{,}521$ trainable
parameters.\footnote{$2{\cdot}80 + 80 + 80{\cdot}40 + 40 +
40{\cdot}20 + 20 + 20{\cdot}10 + 10 + 10{\cdot}1 + 1 = 4521$.} The
raw scalar output $\hat y(x,t)$ is wrapped in a tanh envelope,
$\Phi_\theta(x,t) = A\tanh\bigl(\hat y(x,t)\bigr)$ with
$A = A_0 = 1$ taken as the dynamic range of the initial data,
to keep activations within
the dynamic range of $\Phi$ over the whole domain.
The architecture is summarised in Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-pinn-arch},
together with the automatic-differentiation
pipeline that produces the residual losses defined below.

The training loss combines PDE, initial-condition and boundary
residuals. Let $r_\theta(x,t) \equiv \partial_t^2\Phi_\theta -
\partial_x^2\Phi_\theta + V_\ell\,\Phi_\theta$ be the pointwise
residual of Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}. The training loss is the
standard composite of PDE, initial-condition and boundary-condition
residuals, augmented with the gradient terms of the
gradient-enhanced PINN \citep{Yu2022gpinn},
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{fwd}}
  = \lambda_{r}     \Lpde
  + \lambda_{r_x}   \mathcal{L}_{\partial_x r}
  + \lambda_{r_t}   \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t r}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{IC}} \mathcal{L}_{\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{IV}} \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{BL}} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{left}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{BR}} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{right}}.
  \label{eq:loss-fwd}
\end{equation}
The seven mean-squared component losses are
\begin{align}
  \Lpde &= \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{(x,t)\in\mathcal{X}_r}
            r_\theta(x,t)^{2},
  & \mathcal{L}_{\partial_x r} &= \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{(x,t)\in\mathcal{X}_r}
            \bigl(\partial_x r_\theta(x,t)\bigr)^{2},
  \nonumber\\
  \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t r} &= \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{(x,t)\in\mathcal{X}_r}
            \bigl(\partial_t r_\theta(x,t)\bigr)^{2},
  & \mathcal{L}_{\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}} &= \frac{1}{N_i}\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}_i}
            \bigl(\Phi_\theta(x,0)-\Phi(x,0)\bigr)^{2},
  \nonumber\\
  \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}}
     &= \frac{1}{N_i}\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}_i}
        \bigl(\partial_t\Phi_\theta(x,0)-\partial_t\Phi(x,0)\bigr)^{2},
  & \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{left}}
     &= \frac{1}{N_b}\sum_{t\in\mathcal{X}_b^{L}}
        \bigl((\partial_t-\partial_x)\Phi_\theta(x_{\min},t)\bigr)^{2},
  \nonumber\\
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{right}}
     &= \frac{1}{N_b}\sum_{t\in\mathcal{X}_b^{R}}
        \bigl((\partial_t+\partial_x)\Phi_\theta(x_{\max},t)\bigr)^{2},
  & &
  \label{eq:loss-components}
\end{align}
mirroring the formulation of \citet[Eqs.~(4)--(10)]{Patel2024pinn}.
The two gradient terms $\mathcal{L}_{\partial_x r},
\mathcal{L}_{\partial_t r}$ encourage the network to produce a
residual field that vanishes \emph{smoothly} rather than oscillating
around zero \citep{Yu2022gpinn}. The collocation set has $N_r = 32{,}000$ PDE points,
$N_i = 800$ initial-condition points and $N_b = 400$ boundary points distributed across both spatial
boundaries (so each boundary contributes $\sim 200$ points), for
$33{,}200$ space-time points in
total. \citet{Patel2024pinn} use the same $N_r$ and $N_i$ but quote
$N_b = 400$ \emph{per boundary}, giving their total of $33{,}600$;
this is the only collocation-count difference between the two
baselines. The weight vector applied throughout this work is
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\lambda}
  = (\lambda_{r},\lambda_{r_x},\lambda_{r_t},\lambda_{\mathrm{IC}},
     \lambda_{\mathrm{IV}},\lambda_{\mathrm{BL}},\lambda_{\mathrm{BR}})
  = (100,\,100,\,100,\,1,\,100,\,1,\,1).
  \label{eq:weights}
\end{equation}
The equal weight $100$ on the PDE primary residual, its two
gradient augmentations, and the velocity initial condition reflects
that the first three terms control how accurately the network
satisfies Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} throughout the bulk of the domain,
while the velocity initial condition fixes the propagation
direction of the pulse at $t = 0$ and was found by
\citet{Patel2024pinn} to be unstable to under-weighting (see
below). The unit weights on the field initial condition and on the
two Sommerfeld boundaries reflect that, by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:bc-arrival}, the wavefront does not reach either
boundary within the simulated interval, so the boundary residuals
act only as soft regularisers on a region the dynamics of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} barely populate. The choice
$\lambda_{\mathrm{IV}} = 100$ follows \citet{Patel2024pinn}, who
report empirically that down-weighting the velocity initial
condition produces a ``phase problem'' (a slow drift of the learned
waveform's phase relative to the FD reference), which the larger
weight suppresses. We observe the same behaviour and adopt the same
remedy.

Optimisation proceeds in two stages, namely $10{,}000$ iterations
of Adam \citep{Kingma2015adam} with learning rate $10^{-3}$,
followed by $30{,}000$ iterations of L-BFGS \citep{Liu1989lbfgs}.
The number of L-BFGS iterations is doubled relative to the
$15{,}000$ used by \citet{Patel2024pinn}. Empirically the field-residual loss
continues to decrease through iteration $\sim 25{,}000$ in our runs,
and the
plateau-window analysis of Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau} requires the
resulting $\Phi(\xq,t)$ to be sufficiently converged that the
plateau standard deviation is dominated by the window choice rather
than by the noise floor of the converged training loss.
We use the residual-based adaptive sampling scheme of
\citet{Lu2021deepxde} (the RAR-G variant in the taxonomy of
\citet{Wu2023adaptive}): every $1{,}000$ Adam
iterations, $10^{5}$ candidate residual points are drawn uniformly
from the spacetime domain, the PDE residual is evaluated on them in
minibatches of $5{,}000$, and the entire $N_r = 32{,}000$
collocation set is replaced by $0.3\,N_r = 9{,}600$ candidates of
largest $|r_\theta|$ together with $0.7\,N_r = 22{,}400$ candidates
drawn uniformly at random from the remainder. The greedy fraction
$0.3$ was tuned for this problem; the
uniform component prevents the collocation set from collapsing
entirely onto the wavefront at late training iterations.

All computations use double precision; we found
this necessary to avoid floating-point-induced stalls in L-BFGS for
this problem.

\subsection{Inverse PINN}
\label{sec:inv-pinn}

The forward problem of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn} computes
$\Phi(x,t)$ from a known PDE; the inverse problem reverses the
direction of inference and recovers unknown parameters of the
PDE, and of the asymptotic solution that the PDE supports, from
noisy observations of the field. Here the unknown parameters are
the black hole mass $M$, which enters the Zerilli potential
Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli} through every $M$-dependent term, and
the fundamental QNM template parameters
$(\omega,\tau,A_{\mathrm{ring}},\phi)$ that label the late-time
damped sinusoid of Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-omega} at the observer
position. The inverse PINN trains the same network of
Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn} jointly with these five scalars, treated
as learnable variables. Each scalar is appended to the optimiser's
trainable-parameter list alongside the network weights, so Adam
and L-BFGS update the network weights $\theta$ and the five
scalars $(M,\omega,\tau,A_{\mathrm{ring}},\phi)$ in the same
gradient step, with a single shared loss
\citep{Lu2021deepxde,Cornell2022pinnqnm}. The PDE residual
depends on $M$ and pushes the optimiser to values of $M$ for
which $\Phi_\theta$ satisfies the Zerilli equation; the data
residual pushes $\Phi_\theta$ to match the noisy observations;
the template residual pushes $(\omega,\tau,A_{\mathrm{ring}},\phi)$ to label the late-time behaviour of the same
$\Phi_\theta$. The shared gradient step couples all five
unknowns and the network weights, and the joint minimum is the
inverse-problem solution. Training data are noisy samples of
the true Zerilli waveform, generated by adding Gaussian noise to
the FD reference,
\begin{equation}
  \Phiobs(x_i, t_i) = \Phi_{\mathrm{FD}}(x_i, t_i) + \eta_i,
  \qquad
  \eta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_{\mathrm{obs}}^2),
  \label{eq:noisy-obs}
\end{equation}
with
$\sigma_{\mathrm{obs}} = 0.01\times\max_{x,t}|\Phi_{\mathrm{FD}}(x,t)|$
and $\Nobs = 500$ points $(x_i, t_i)$ drawn uniformly from the full
two-dimensional domain $[-50,150]\,M\times[0,50]\,M$, distinct
from the one-dimensional template grid at $x = \xq$ introduced in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring} below. The fixed observer position
$\xq = 10\,M$ is used both for the ringdown template constraint
and for all post-hoc QNM extraction.

The composite inverse loss has three blocks,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{inv}}
  = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PINN}}(M)
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{data}}\,\Ldata
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{ring}}\,\Lring(\omega,\tau,A_{\mathrm{ring}},\phi),
  \label{eq:loss-inv}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PINN}}(M)$ is the seven-term sum of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-fwd} with weights as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:weights}, the
PDE residual now depending on the learnable $M$ through the Zerilli
potential. The data term is the mean squared mismatch between the
network output and the observations,
\begin{equation*}
  \Ldata = \frac{1}{\Nobs}\sum_{i=1}^{\Nobs}
  \bigl(\Phitheta(x_i,t_i) - \Phiobs(x_i,t_i)\bigr)^{2},
\end{equation*}
with weight $\lambda_{\mathrm{data}} = 10$, set empirically so that
$\lambda_{\mathrm{data}}\Ldata$ is comparable in magnitude to the
PDE residual term at the start of training; we did not tune this
value further. The ringdown term
enforces a damped-sinusoid template at the observer position,
\begin{equation}
  \Lring = \frac{1}{\Nring}\sum_{j=1}^{\Nring}
  \Bigl(\Phitheta(\xq,t_j) - A_{\mathrm{ring}}\,e^{-t_j/\tau}\cos(\omega t_j + \phi)\Bigr)^{2},
  \label{eq:loss-ring}
\end{equation}
with the times $t_j$ on a uniform grid of $\Nring$ points on
$[\tring,\,t_{\max}]$, where $t_{\max} = 50\,M$ is the end of the
simulation domain. Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring} is implemented as a soft constraint that
evaluates the template residual on a one-dimensional grid of
$\Nring$ time samples at $x = \xq$; the residual
$\Phitheta(\xq,t_j) - \mathrm{template}(t_j)$ back-propagates into
both the network parameters $\theta$ and the
template parameters $(\omega,\tau,A_{\mathrm{ring}},\phi)$ on every
training step. Embedding a damped-sinusoid template as a soft
constraint inside the PINN loss is new. The template encodes the
prior knowledge that, in the QNM regime, $\Phi(\xq,t)$ takes the
damped-sinusoid form of Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-omega}; without it the
inverse PINN would have to discover that functional form from
noisy observations alone, and the learnable scalars
$(\omega,\tau,A_{\mathrm{ring}},\phi)$ would have no operational
definition inside the loss. Prior PINN-based black-hole
perturbation studies, including \citet{Patel2024pinn},
fit the template only after training. We therefore treat
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring} as a methodological component of this
paper and study its sensitivity to $\tring$,
$\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}}$ and $\Nring$ in
Sec.~\ref{sec:variants} below.

The architecture and the (Adam, L-BFGS) optimiser sequence are the
same as in the forward PINN; here we use $10{,}000$ Adam
iterations followed by $15{,}000$ L-BFGS iterations.
The full architecture, including the five learnable scalars
appended to the network parameters, is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:inv-pinn-arch}.

\subsubsection{Variants of the ringdown-template loss}
\label{sec:variants}

The hyperparameters $\tring$, $\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}}$ and $\Nring$
control, respectively, the start of the ringdown window, the strength
of the template constraint relative to the PDE constraint, and the
number of template evaluation points. We study the six configurations
of Table~\ref{tab:variants}, designed as a one-factor-at-a-time
ablation followed by a combined run and a structural extension.
Variants A, B, C each modify exactly one hyperparameter relative to
the baseline; variant D combines all three; variant E adds the first
overtone to the ringdown template as a second damped sinusoid with
its own learnable $(\omega_1,\tau_1,A_1,\phi_1)$, and the original
template parameters of Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring} are relabelled
$(\omega_0,\tau_0,A_0^{\mathrm{ring}},\phi_0)$ in this variant.
The overtone is initialised at $\omega_1^{\mathrm{init}} = 0.28$
($\approx 19\%$ below the Leaver value $M\omega_1 = 0.3467$) and
$\tau_1^{\mathrm{init}} = 4.7$ ($\approx 29\%$ above
$\tau_1/M = 3.651$), matching the offset philosophy adopted for the
fundamental in Sec.~\ref{sec:inv-pinn}. The subscripts
$\omega_n,\tau_n$ used here for the two learnable template modes are
distinct from the Leaver overtone labels of Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-ref}
and from the two-mode NLS post-hoc fit of Eq.~\eqref{eq:two-mode}
below; the three notations agree numerically only at the truth.

The choice $\tring = 18\,M$ in variants A, D, E is motivated
\emph{a~posteriori} by the M5 plateau scan of
Sec.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods} on the forward waveform, which identifies
$t_0 \in [10,\,16.7]\,M$ as the lowest-bias plateau for the
fundamental. The value $18\,M$ sits at the upper edge, where overtone
contamination is negligible
($e^{-18/\tau_1} = e^{-18/3.651} \approx 7\times 10^{-3}$) while
the fundamental still retains
$e^{-18/\tau_0} = e^{-18/11.241} \approx 0.20$ of its initial
amplitude. The choice $\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}} = 100$ in variants B,
D, E places the template constraint at the same weight as the PDE
primary residual $\lambda_r = 100$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:weights}. With the default $\lambda_{\mathrm{ring}} = 1$
the template is two orders of magnitude weaker than $\lambda_r$, and
the QNM parameters drift to whatever configuration minimises the
residual under the much weaker template penalty.

The ringdown loss term Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring} contains an
$A_{\mathrm{ring}}$--$\tau$ degeneracy that follows directly from
the shape of the template inside the fit window. Expand the
envelope at the early edge of the window,
$A_{\mathrm{ring}} e^{-t/\tau} = A_{\mathrm{ring}}\bigl(1 - t/\tau + \mathcal{O}(t^2/\tau^2)\bigr)$,
so that to leading order the envelope enters the residual only
through the two combinations $A_{\mathrm{ring}}$ and
$A_{\mathrm{ring}}/\tau$. Pairs $(A_{\mathrm{ring}},\tau)$ that
share the same $A_{\mathrm{ring}}$ at the start of the window and
the same ratio $A_{\mathrm{ring}}/\tau$ are therefore
indistinguishable to first order in $t/\tau$; equivalently, a
smaller $\tau$ paired with a larger $A_{\mathrm{ring}}$ produces
a fit of comparable residual at the early edge of the template
window. At late times the envelope is exponentially suppressed
and contributes negligibly to $\Lring$, so the second-order
curvature term that would lift the degeneracy is too weak to
constrain the optimiser. The optimiser drifts down this
degenerate direction during L-BFGS,
so the \emph{learned} $\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$ recorded in the
parameter history can differ substantially from the decay rate
actually present in the converged $\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$. A direct
empirical diagnostic of this effect is that M2 evaluated on the
variant's own training-template window $[\tring,\,50]\,M$
recovers the (biased) learned $\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$, whereas
M2 evaluated on the wider window $[10,\,50]\,M$ recovers a value
much closer to the Leaver value. The reporting convention of
Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting} therefore quotes M2 over the wide window
as the primary $\tau$ across all variants. The narrower-window
value is reported separately as a diagnostic in
Sec.~\ref{sec:results} to characterise the degeneracy.

\subsection{Curriculum PINN}
\label{sec:cur-pinn}

The forward PINN of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn} trains a single
network on the full temporal domain $t \in [0,50]\,M$. The
pointwise error of the converged baseline grows monotonically
with $t$: the late-time band $t \in [37.5,\,50]\,M$ contributes
$\sim 41\%$ of the total RMSD against the FD reference, despite
spanning only a quarter of the domain. This motivates a
curriculum-learning strategy
\citep{Bengio2009curriculum,Krishnapriyan2021failure} in which
the temporal domain is split into $K$ contiguous windows and a
separate PINN is trained sequentially on each window, with the
trained $k$-th PINN providing the initial conditions for the
$(k{+}1)$-th. Smaller temporal sub-domains carry less of the
late-time decay range and therefore an easier optimisation
landscape; the price is that the initial conditions of every
window after the first are themselves PINN outputs, and so
inherit the residual error of the previous window.

\paragraph{Hard partition.} The general $K$-window curriculum
splits the temporal domain at $K-1$ split points
$t_{\min} < t_{1} < \dots < t_{K-1} < t_{\max}$ into $K$ disjoint
windows
\begin{equation}
  W_{k} = [t_{k-1},\,t_{k}],
  \qquad
  t_{0} = t_{\min},\;\; t_{K} = t_{\max}.
  \label{eq:cur-split}
\end{equation}
On each window we train an independent PINN
$\Phi_{\theta_{k}}$ with the same architecture as
Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}, the loss Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-fwd},
weights Eq.~\eqref{eq:weights}, and optimiser schedule (Adam
$10{,}000$ + L-BFGS $30{,}000$). Window $W_{1}$ uses the analytic
Gaussian-pulse initial conditions of Eq.~\eqref{eq:ic};
for $k \ge 2$ the initial conditions at $t = t_{k-1}$ are
extracted from the trained $\Phi_{\theta_{k-1}}$ at the matching
time slice,
\begin{equation}
  \Phi^{\mathrm{IC}}_{k}(x) = \Phi_{\theta_{k-1}}(x,\,t_{k-1}),
  \qquad
  \partial_{t}\Phi^{\mathrm{IC}}_{k}(x)
   = \partial_{t}\Phi_{\theta_{k-1}}(x,\,t_{k-1}),
  \label{eq:cur-ic}
\end{equation}
with the velocity field $\partial_{t}\Phi_{\theta_{k-1}}$
computed by automatic differentiation on the trained network and
both fields then evaluated by cubic-spline interpolation at the
collocation points of $W_{k}$. The IC residuals
$\mathcal{L}_{\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}},
\mathcal{L}_{\partial_{t}\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}}$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-components} are then evaluated against
Eq.~\eqref{eq:cur-ic} rather than against the analytic IC. The
greedy adaptive resampler of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn} is restarted
fresh on each window so that $0.3 N_{r}$ collocation points are
concentrated on the window's own residual field. The final
stitched field is
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\mathrm{cur}}(x,t)
  = \Phi_{\theta_{k}}(x,t),
  \qquad t \in W_{k},
  \label{eq:cur-stitch}
\end{equation}
with the splits $\{t_{k}\}_{k=1}^{K-1}$ being the only points at
which two networks could disagree; we adopt the convention that
$\Phi_{\theta_{k+1}}$ owns the splice time exactly.

\paragraph{Soft-overlap partition.} The hard partition of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:cur-stitch} produces a discontinuity in the stitched
field whenever
$\Phi_{\theta_{k}}(x,t_{k}) \ne \Phi_{\theta_{k+1}}(x,t_{k})$,
which occurs generically because $\Phi_{\theta_{k+1}}$ is trained
against an interpolated rather than exact initial condition. We
also implement a soft-overlap variant in which two consecutive
windows share an overlap band of width $\Delta_{\mathrm{ov}}$
centred on $t_{\mathrm{split}}$,
\begin{equation}
  W_{1}^{\mathrm{ov}}
   = [t_{\min},\,t_{\mathrm{split}} + \Delta_{\mathrm{ov}}/2],
  \qquad
  W_{2}^{\mathrm{ov}}
   = [t_{\mathrm{split}} - \Delta_{\mathrm{ov}}/2,\,t_{\max}],
  \label{eq:cur-soft}
\end{equation}
with two additional ingredients on top of Eq.~\eqref{eq:cur-ic}.
First, $\Phi_{\theta_{2}}$ receives a fixed
\emph{continuity-anchor} loss term on a uniform grid of
$n_{x} \times n_{t}$ points across the overlap rectangle
$[x_{\min},x_{\max}] \times
[t_{\mathrm{split}} - \Delta_{\mathrm{ov}}/2,\,
 t_{\mathrm{split}} + \Delta_{\mathrm{ov}}/2]$,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cont}}
  = \frac{\lambda_{\mathrm{cont}}}{n_{x} n_{t}}
    \sum_{i=1}^{n_{x}}\sum_{j=1}^{n_{t}}
    \bigl(\Phi_{\theta_{2}}(x_{i},t_{j})
        - \Phi_{\theta_{1}}(x_{i},t_{j})\bigr)^{2},
  \label{eq:cur-anchor}
\end{equation}
that pulls $\Phi_{\theta_{2}}$ towards the (already-trained)
$\Phi_{\theta_{1}}$ across the entire overlap rather than at the
splice time alone. Second, the final field on the overlap is a
smoothstep-blended average of the two networks,
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\mathrm{cur}}^{\mathrm{ov}}(x,t)
  = \bigl(1 - s(\xi)\bigr)\,\Phi_{\theta_{1}}(x,t)
   + s(\xi)\,\Phi_{\theta_{2}}(x,t),
  \quad
  s(\xi) = \xi^{2}(3 - 2\xi),
  \quad
  \xi = \frac{t - t_{\mathrm{ov}}^{-}}{\Delta_{\mathrm{ov}}},
  \label{eq:cur-blend}
\end{equation}
with $t_{\mathrm{ov}}^{-} = t_{\mathrm{split}} -
\Delta_{\mathrm{ov}}/2$, on the band
$t \in W_{1}^{\mathrm{ov}} \cap W_{2}^{\mathrm{ov}}$. The
smoothstep $s$ is $C^{1}$ and satisfies $s(0) = 0$, $s(1) = 1$,
$s'(0) = s'(1) = 0$, so the blended field is continuously
differentiable across the overlap edges. Outside the overlap the
stitching reduces to Eq.~\eqref{eq:cur-stitch}.

$\Delta_{\mathrm{ov}} = 10\,M$, $n_{x} \times n_{t} = 201\times 11$
continuity anchors and $\lambda_{\mathrm{cont}} = 10$. All three
variants share the per-window architecture of
Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-pinn}, the loss Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-fwd}, the
weights Eq.~\eqref{eq:weights}, and the (Adam + L-BFGS) optimiser
schedule applied independently per window (so the total training
budget scales linearly with $K$).

\subsection{QNM extraction methods}
\label{sec:qnm-methods}

Once a forward or inverse PINN has been trained, the QNM parameters
$(\omega,\tau)$ are extracted post-hoc from the time series
$\Phi(\xq,t)$ at $\xq = 10\,M$. We apply five extraction methods, the
first three of which mirror standard numerical-relativity practice
and the last two of which are the plateau-based methods that
constitute our methodological contribution.

The two plateau-based methods rely on the following operational
definition, formalised and motivated in
Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau} below.

\begin{definition}[fit-window plateau]
\label{def:plateau}
A fit window $[t_0,\,\tend]$ lies inside the QNM regime if and only
if shifting either edge by a small amount produces no significant
change in the recovered $(\omega,\tau)$. Operationally, in M4 and
M5 the plateau is the contiguous block of fixed size
$w = \lfloor\tfrac12 N\rfloor$ along each scan axis that minimises
the combined relative population standard deviation
$\mathrm{std}(\omega)/|\bar\omega|+\mathrm{std}(\tau)/|\bar\tau|$ of
the per-window fits inside the block (population standard deviation,
divisor $N$).
\end{definition}

Method 1 (M1) is FFT and log-line through local maxima.
The frequency $\omega$ is estimated from the location of the largest
peak of a zero-padded, Hann-windowed FFT of $\Phi(\xq,t)$ over the
fit window, with parabolic interpolation around the peak bin. The
damping time $\tau$ is estimated from the slope of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:logline} applied to the local maxima
$\{(t_k, |\Phi(\xq, t_k)|)\}$ of $\Phi$ in the same window; in
practice the slope $-1/\tau$ is obtained by an ordinary
least-squares straight-line fit to $\log|\Phi(\xq, t_k)|$ versus
$t_k$. This is the procedure adopted by
\citet{Patel2024pinn}.

Method 2 (M2) is direct nonlinear least squares.
A single damped sinusoid is fitted to $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on the chosen
window,
\[
  \Phi_{\mathrm{model}}(t) = A\,e^{-t/\tau}\cos(\omega t + \phi),
\]
using a bound-constrained Levenberg--Marquardt solver. M2 is a
four-parameter fit and is the most sensitive of the five methods to
overtone contamination at the early edge of the window.

Method 3 (M3) is ESPRIT subspace decomposition.
ESPRIT \citep{Roy1989esprit} applied with model order $K = 6$ to
the Hilbert-analytic signal is ill-conditioned at the SNR of the
PINN-derived $\Phi(\xq,t)$ in our setup; M3 is reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:esprit} but is not used in the quoted numbers.

Method 4 (M4) is two-mode NLS with start-time plateau scan.
A two-mode template,
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\mathrm{model}}(t) =
  A_0\,e^{-t/\tau_0}\cos(\omega_0 t + \phi_0)
  + A_1\,e^{-t/\tau_1}\cos(\omega_1 t + \phi_1),
  \label{eq:two-mode}
\end{equation}
is fitted by nonlinear least squares over a window
$[t_0,\,t_{\max}]$, with the mode of longer $\tau$ identified as the
fundamental. The fit is repeated for $t_0$ on a uniform grid of $16$
values in $[10,\,25]\,M$ (step $1\,M$), at fixed $\tend = 50\,M$.
We then identify the contiguous block of
eight consecutive start times of lowest combined relative scatter in
$(\omega_0,\tau_0)$ and report the plateau mean and the plateau
population standard deviation
as the M4 estimate of $(\omega,\tau)$ and its
fit-window systematic uncertainty $(\sigma_\omega,\sigma_\tau)$. The
block width $w = 8$ is fixed by the convention
$w = \lfloor\tfrac12 N_{t_0}\rfloor$,
which trades scan
resolution against block statistics.
The procedure is summarised in Algorithm~\ref{alg:m4}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{M4 --- 1-D plateau scan for $(\omega_0,\tau_0)$.}
\label{alg:m4}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} time series $\Phi(\xq,t)$,
       grid $\{t_0^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{16} \subset [10,25]\,M$,
       fixed $\tend = 50\,M$, plateau width $w = 8$.
\FOR{$k = 1,\dots,16$}
  \STATE Fit Eq.~\eqref{eq:two-mode} to $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on $[t_0^{(k)},\tend]$
         by NLS.
  \STATE Identify the longer-$\tau$ component as the fundamental,
         giving $(\omega_0^{(k)},\tau_0^{(k)})$.
\ENDFOR
\STATE Find the contiguous index range $k^* : k^*{+}w{-}1$ minimising
       the combined relative standard deviation
       $\mathrm{std}(\omega_0)/\bar\omega_0 + \mathrm{std}(\tau_0)/\bar\tau_0$.
\STATE \textbf{Return} plateau mean
       $(\bar\omega_0,\bar\tau_0)$ and population standard deviation
       $(\sigma_{\omega_0},\sigma_{\tau_0})$ over the plateau block.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Method 5 (M5) is two-mode NLS with two-dimensional plateau scan.
M5 generalises M4 by sweeping both edges of the fit window
$[t_0,\,\tend]$. We use a $10\times 6$ rectangular grid with
$t_0 \in [10,\,25]\,M$ in steps of $5/3\,M$ and
$\tend \in [30,\,50]\,M$ in steps of $4\,M$.
The plateau is the contiguous $5\times 3$ rectangle of smallest
combined relative scatter, fixed by the same
$\lfloor\tfrac12 N\rfloor$ rule applied independently along the two
axes ($\lfloor10/2\rfloor=5$ in $t_0$, $\lfloor 6/2\rfloor=3$ in
$\tend$). The plateau mean and plateau standard deviation are
reported as for M4. To our knowledge M5 is the first
systematic two-dimensional plateau analysis applied to PINN-derived
QNM extraction. A schematic of the two scans, overlaid on a
representative ringdown waveform, is given in
Fig.~\ref{fig:windows}.

\subsection{Plateau-based fit-window selection}
\label{sec:plateau}

The damped-sinusoid template
$A\,e^{-t/\tau}\cos(\omega t + \phi)$ is the correct model for
$\Phi(\xq,t)$ only inside the QNM regime, that is, after the prompt
response of the initial pulse has passed and before the late-time
power-law tail becomes comparable to the exponential. Fitting the template to
data outside this regime is \emph{model misspecification}, not a
noise problem, and the resulting parameter biases do not vanish with
more data. The fit window is therefore a hyperparameter of the
extraction, and a robust $(\omega,\tau)$ measurement requires a
reproducible criterion for the window. Definition~\ref{def:plateau}
captures the operational criterion adopted in M4 and M5; inside the
plateau the recovered $(\omega,\tau)$ are stationary in the window
edges, and their variation across the plateau provides an estimate
of the fit-window systematic uncertainty.

For the Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$ fundamental at our parameters the
four relevant timescales are summarised by
\begin{equation}
  t_{\mathrm{burst}} \sim 5\,M, \qquad
  \tau_1 = 3.651\,M, \qquad
  \tau_0 = 11.241\,M, \qquad
  t_{\mathrm{tail}} \gg 50\,M,
  \label{eq:timescales}
\end{equation}
where $t_{\mathrm{burst}}$ is the prompt-burst duration set by the
initial condition Eq.~\eqref{eq:ic}, $\tau_1$ and $\tau_0$ are the
overtone and fundamental decay times of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-ref}, and $t_{\mathrm{tail}}$ is the onset of the
power-law Price tail $\propto t^{-(2\ell+3)}$
\citep{Berti2009review}. The Price-tail amplitude depends on the
initial data and observer position, but for our setup the QNM
remains the dominant component of $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on the entire
simulation interval $[0,50]\,M$, as confirmed by direct inspection
of the FD reference. The right edge of any fit window inside this
interval is therefore well within the QNM regime, which is borne
out empirically by the M5 plateau rectangles extending in $\tend$
all the way to the domain end (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
The plateau procedure differs from the standard practice of quoting a
single number from a single fit window, including that of
\citet{Patel2024pinn}, who do not specify a numerical $t_0$ or
$\tend$ and report no fit-window sensitivity. The closest precedents
in the gravitational-wave ringdown literature are the start-time
stability scans of \citet{Buonanno2007ringdown} and the
$f_{220}$-vs-start-time consistency check used in the
GW150914 ringdown analysis \citep{LIGO2016ringdown}, both of which
sweep $t_0$ at fixed $\tend$, and the Bayesian start-time
marginalisation of \citet{Isi2021priors}, which integrates over the
posterior of $t_0$ instead of selecting a window. The present
approach extends these along two axes. First, we sweep both edges
of the window simultaneously rather than $t_0$ alone (M5,
Sec.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods}). Second, we quote the spread of the
recovered $(\omega,\tau)$ across the empirically identified
plateau as a fit-window systematic uncertainty for $\omega$, in lieu
of the nonlinear-fit covariance which our M2 implementation
discards (see Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}).

\subsection{Reporting convention}
\label{sec:reporting}

We quote one $(\omega,\tau)$ per experiment, with $\omega$ from
the M4 plateau mean and $\tau$ from M2 on the wide window
$[10,\,50]\,M$. The
$\sigma_\omega$ quoted alongside $\omega$ is the M4 plateau standard
deviation (Algorithm~\ref{alg:m4}, line~7), an estimate of the
fit-window systematic uncertainty in the sense of
Def.~\ref{def:plateau}. M2 over a single fixed window does not
produce an uncertainty, so the dispersion in $\tau$ across variants
is reported only through the per-window per-method tables of
Appendix~\ref{app:per-window} rather than as a single $\sigma_\tau$
on the primary value.

The lower edge $t = 10\,M$ matches the lower edge $\tring$ of
the baseline ringdown-template window in Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring}
and is used unchanged across all extraction variants.
Empirically the
dominant peak of $|\Phi(\xq,t)|$ on the FD reference falls at
$t \approx 10.8\,M$ (with subsequent local maxima at
$t \approx 18.3, 26.7, 35.2, 43.6\,M$),
so the lower edge $t = 10\,M$ is placed just before the first
ringdown peak. The choice $t = 10\,M$ rather than the empirical peak
location $t \approx 10.8\,M$ is conventional and is preserved here
for exact comparability with the inverse training window.

This convention follows established practice in numerical-relativity
QNM extraction. Quoting different estimators for $\omega$ and
$\tau$ is also done by \citet{Berti2007ringdown}, who use FFT
for $\omega$ and an amplitude-envelope fit for $\tau$, and by
\citet{Patel2024pinn}, who use FFT for $\omega$ and a log-line
through local maxima for $\tau$. The motivation in all cases is
that $\omega$ is best estimated from phase information aggregated
across many cycles, and the M4 plateau is the lowest-variance such
estimator in our setup with $\sigma_\omega \sim 10^{-5}$ for the
converged variants, whereas $\tau$ is best estimated from amplitude
information over the longest available decay range, motivating a
wider M2 window.

The primary $\tau$ is taken from the post-hoc M2 wide-window fit
on $\Phi_\theta(\xq,t)$ rather than from the learned scalar
$\tau_{\mathrm{learned}}$, because of the
$A_{\mathrm{ring}}$--$\tau$ degeneracy of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss-ring} introduced in Sec.~\ref{sec:inv-pinn}
and quantified in Sec.~\ref{sec:res-inv}.

For each method and each variant the full per-window per-method
results are tabulated in Appendix~\ref{app:per-window} and the
corresponding plateau plots and 2-D heatmaps are shown in
Appendix~\ref{app:plots}. The summary table in
Sec.~\ref{sec:results} collects only the values selected by the
convention above.
