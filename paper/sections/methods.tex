\subsection{Schwarzschild Zerilli problem}
\label{sec:fwd-problem}

The canonical Schwarzschild forward problem is the Zerilli equation
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} with the polar potential
$V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli}, evaluated at
$\ell=2$ and $M=1$ on the numerical domain
$x\in[-50,150]\,M$ and $t\in[0,50]\,M$. We use
outgoing Gaussian initial data
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(x,0) = A_0\,\exp\!\left[-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{\sigma_0^2}\right],
  \qquad
  \partial_t \Phi(x,0) = -\partial_x \Phi(x,0)
  = \frac{2(x-x_0)}{\sigma_0^2}\,\Phi(x,0),
  \label{eq:ic}
\end{equation}
with $A_0 = 1$, $x_0 = 4\,M$ and $\sigma_0 = 5\,M$. This choice
enforces the outgoing flat-space condition
$(\partial_t+\partial_x)\Phi=0$ at $t=0$, so the initial packet
propagates towards spatial infinity before scattering from the
Zerilli potential. It differs only in the initial velocity profile
from the setup of \citet{Patel2024pinn}, while retaining their
amplitude, centre and width. At the finite radial boundaries
$x=x_{\min}$ and $x=x_{\max}$, we impose the first-order Sommerfeld
conditions
\begin{equation}
  (\partial_t - \partial_x)\Phi\bigl|_{x=x_{\min}} = 0, \qquad
  (\partial_t + \partial_x)\Phi\bigl|_{x=x_{\max}} = 0,
  \label{eq:bc-sommerfeld}
\end{equation}
These are the finite-domain outgoing conditions used by
\citet{Patel2024pinn}. They approximate ingoing radiation at the
horizon side and outgoing radiation at the outer boundary, rather
than the frequency-domain QNM eigenvalue conditions imposed at
$x\to\pm\infty$. The characteristic travel times from the packet
centre to the two boundaries are
\begin{equation}
  t_{R} = x_{\max} - x_0 = 146\,M, \qquad
  t_{L} = x_0 - x_{\min} = 54\,M,
  \label{eq:bc-arrival}
\end{equation}
Both times exceed the reported window. The PINN boundary losses therefore
mainly regularise a causally remote part of the domain, while the FD
reference is insensitive to the boundaries over the times used below.

The Zerilli reference solution (Fig.~\ref{fig:waveform-3d}) is a
method-of-lines finite-difference (FD) solve with second-order centred
spatial differences, second-order one-sided boundary closures and
fourth-order Runge--Kutta time stepping, using $\Delta x = 0.2\,M$
and $\Delta t = 0.1\,M$. It is used as the numerical reference for
the Zerilli PINN field errors and as an FD signal for checking the
extraction suite. The Leaver values $M\omega_0^R = 0.3737$,
$\tau_0/M = 11.241$ (fundamental) and $M\omega_1^R = 0.3467$,
$\tau_1/M = 3.651$ (first overtone) of Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-ref}
are used for percentage-error reporting.
The Regge--Wheeler reproduction uses the same domain, initial data,
boundary conditions, FD scheme and extraction protocol with
$V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}$ replaced by the axial Regge--Wheeler
potential. The later Schwarzschild extensions use the Zerilli
problem as the representative isospectral sector.

\subsection{Forward PINN}
\label{sec:fwd-pinn}

We approximate the solution of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-problem} with a
fully connected feed-forward PINN \citep{Raissi2017}, implemented in
DeepXDE \citep{Lu2021deepxde} with the PyTorch backend
\citep{Paszke2019pytorch}.

The dense architecture, Glorot-uniform initialiser and bounded output
transform follow \citet{Patel2024pinn}. This keeps the network class
fixed across the reproduction and enhanced runs, so the later
differences isolate the training schedule, collocation sampling and
extraction protocol. The network has hidden widths $[80,40,20,10]$
with $\tanh$ activations. The physical coordinates $(x,t)$ are
linearly normalised to $[-1,1]^2$ before entering the network, and the
scalar output is mapped through
$\Phi_\theta(x,t)=A\tanh\bigl(\hat y(x,t)\bigr)$ with $A=A_0=1$,
giving $4{,}521$ trainable
parameters.\footnote{$2{\cdot}80 + 80 + 80{\cdot}40 + 40 +
40{\cdot}20 + 20 + 20{\cdot}10 + 10 + 10{\cdot}1 + 1 = 4521$.}
The architecture is summarised in Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-pinn-arch},
together with the automatic-differentiation pipeline that
produces the residual losses defined below.

Let $r_\theta(x,t) \equiv \partial_t^2\Phi_\theta -
\partial_x^2\Phi_\theta + V_\ell\,\Phi_\theta$ be the pointwise
residual of Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}, with
$V_\ell=V_\ell^{\mathrm{Z}}$ for the Zerilli runs. The loss is the
weighted mean-squared sum of the PDE, initial-data and boundary
residuals, augmented by the two gradient-enhanced PINN terms
\citep{Yu2022gpinn},
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{fwd}}
  = \lambda_{r}     \Lpde
  + \lambda_{r_x}   \mathcal{L}_{\partial_x r}
  + \lambda_{r_t}   \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t r}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{IC}} \mathcal{L}_{\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{IV}} \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{BL}} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{left}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{BR}} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{right}}.
  \label{eq:loss-fwd}
\end{equation}
The seven mean-squared component losses are
\begin{align}
  \Lpde &= \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{(x,t)\in\mathcal{X}_r}
            r_\theta(x,t)^{2},
  & \mathcal{L}_{\partial_x r} &= \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{(x,t)\in\mathcal{X}_r}
            \bigl(\partial_x r_\theta(x,t)\bigr)^{2},
  \nonumber\\
  \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t r} &= \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{(x,t)\in\mathcal{X}_r}
            \bigl(\partial_t r_\theta(x,t)\bigr)^{2},
  & \mathcal{L}_{\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}} &= \frac{1}{N_i}\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}_i}
            \bigl(\Phi_\theta(x,0)-\Phi(x,0)\bigr)^{2},
  \nonumber\\
  \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}}
     &= \frac{1}{N_i}\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}_i}
        \bigl(\partial_t\Phi_\theta(x,0)-\partial_t\Phi(x,0)\bigr)^{2},
  & \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{left}}
     &= \frac{1}{N_b}\sum_{t\in\mathcal{X}_b^{L}}
        \bigl((\partial_t-\partial_x)\Phi_\theta(x_{\min},t)\bigr)^{2},
  \nonumber\\
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{right}}
     &= \frac{1}{N_b}\sum_{t\in\mathcal{X}_b^{R}}
        \bigl((\partial_t+\partial_x)\Phi_\theta(x_{\max},t)\bigr)^{2},
  & &
  \label{eq:loss-components}
\end{align}
mirroring the formulation of \citet[Eqs.~(4)--(10)]{Patel2024pinn}.
The two gradient terms $\mathcal{L}_{\partial_x r},
\mathcal{L}_{\partial_t r}$ penalise spatial and temporal variation of the
PDE residual, following the gradient-enhanced PINN construction of
\citet{Yu2022gpinn}. The collocation sizes are listed in
Table~\ref{tab:fwd-hyper}. We use DeepXDE with $N_r=32{,}000$,
$N_i=800$ and $N_b=400$ total boundary points, whereas
\citet{Patel2024pinn} describe $400$ points per boundary. This is the
only collocation-count difference in the reproduction setup. The
forward-PINN weight vector is
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\lambda}
  = (\lambda_{r},\lambda_{r_x},\lambda_{r_t},\lambda_{\mathrm{IC}},
     \lambda_{\mathrm{IV}},\lambda_{\mathrm{BL}},\lambda_{\mathrm{BR}})
  = (100,\,100,\,100,\,1,\,100,\,1,\,1),
  \label{eq:weights}
\end{equation}
following \citet{Patel2024pinn}. The larger
$\lambda_{\mathrm{IV}}=100$ weights the initial-velocity residual,
which Patel et al. identify as important for controlling phase drift.
The unit Sommerfeld weights are consistent with the causal remoteness
of the finite boundaries in Eq.~\eqref{eq:bc-arrival}.

The reproduction run uses the optimiser schedule of
\citet{Patel2024pinn}, namely Adam \citep{Kingma2015adam} for
$10^4$ iterations followed by L-BFGS \citep{Liu1989lbfgs} for
$1.5\times10^4$ iterations. The enhanced forward PINN keeps Adam
fixed and doubles the L-BFGS budget to $3\times10^4$ iterations
(Table~\ref{tab:train-config}), since the loss continues to decrease
through iteration $\sim 25{,}000$. All PINN training is full-batch
and uses double precision; in single precision the L-BFGS phase
stalled in our runs. During the Adam phase of the enhanced run, every
$1{,}000$ optimiser iterations the greedy resampler
\citep{Lu2021deepxde,Wu2023adaptive} scores $10^5$ uniformly drawn
candidates by the primary PDE residual $|r_\theta|$ and replaces the
interior collocation set with $0.3N_r$ highest-residual points and
$0.7N_r$ uniformly drawn points. The uniform component preserves
coverage away from the wavefront.

\subsection{Schwarzschild hFNO: coarse-FD prior and FNO residual}
\label{sec:hyb-method}

The forward PINN is trained separately for each Zerilli initial-value
problem. Changing the Gaussian parameters or the mass parameter in
the numerical setup requires a new optimisation run with
$\mathcal{O}(10^4)$ iterations. A neural operator
(Sec.~\ref{sec:bg-fno}) instead amortises training across a family of
problems. Once trained, it can evaluate a new parameter configuration
without retraining. Here the learned component is not asked to replace
the solver. It corrects a coarse FD solution.

A preliminary direct-operator model, trained to predict the full
field from the initial data and potential, contained $77{,}970{,}529$
trainable parameters. The deployed Schwarzschild residual FNO contains
$1{,}127{,}521$, a factor of $69$ fewer, which motivated retaining the
coarse solver as an additive propagation prior.

Learned corrections coupled to classical solvers have proved effective
in differentiable solver-in-the-loop training and under-resolved fluid
simulation \citep{Um2020solverloop,Kochkov2021mlcfd}. The
Schwarzschild hFNO follows this coarse-solver-plus-correction design.
It uses a single coarse-grid FD solve as the propagation prior,
upsamples that field to the fine grid, and applies a 2-D FNO residual
operator $\mathcal{H}_{\theta}$ with $1.13\times 10^{6}$ parameters to
predict the coarse-to-fine discretisation residual. During training,
the target is estimated label-free from the $k=4$ and $k=2$ coarse
solves by Richardson extrapolation. No fine-grid field appears in the
training loss. A deterministic gradient gate confines the correction to
the under-resolved wavefronts, leaving low-gradient regions equal, or
nearly equal, to the upsampled coarse prior. The extractors and
reporting convention of Secs.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods}--\ref{sec:reporting}
apply unchanged. The pipeline is summarised in
Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-arch}, and its configuration in
Table~\ref{tab:surrogate-config}.

\paragraph{Discretisation and grids.} The fine and coarse solves use
the same physical domain and the same RK4 method-of-lines scheme as
Appendix~\ref{app:fd}, with the same second-order centred stencil.
The fine evolution has $N_x=1001$ points and $N_{\mathrm{step}}=1000$
time steps, while the $k=4$ prior has $N_x=251$ and
$N_{\mathrm{step}}=250$. Since every RK4 stage is linear in $N_x$,
the ratio of finite-difference point updates is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{N_x^{\mathrm f}N_{\mathrm{step}}^{\mathrm f}}
       {N_x^{\mathrm c}N_{\mathrm{step}}^{\mathrm c}}
  = \frac{1001\times1000}{251\times250}
  = 15.95.
  \label{eq:hyb-work-ratio}
\end{equation}
This operation count excludes interpolation and FNO evaluation, so it
does not constitute an end-to-end wall-clock speedup.

\paragraph{Label-free Richardson target.} At $k = 4$ the
second-order stencil leaves a large
$\mathcal{O}(\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}^{2})$ dispersion error in the
upsampled prior, measured in Sec.~\ref{sec:res-hyb}. We therefore
run the same problem at $k = 4$ and $k = 2$, upsample both to the fine grid with the
operator $\mathcal{U}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hyb-upsample}, and form
\begin{equation}
  \Phi^{\mathrm{R}}
  \;=\; \frac{4\,\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}_{2}
              - \mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}}{3},
  \label{eq:hyb-richardson}
\end{equation}
the standard $p = 2$ Richardson combination: the
$\mathcal{O}(\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}^{2})$ terms cancel and
$\Phi^{\mathrm{R}} = \Phi + \mathcal{O}(\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}^{4})$
up to the quintic-interpolation error of the upsample. The
network is trained to predict
the Richardson-estimated residual
\begin{equation}
  \delta\Phi_{\mathrm{R}}(x, t)
  \;=\; \Phi^{\mathrm{R}}(x, t) - \mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, t),
  \label{eq:hyb-residual}
\end{equation}
which agrees with the true error
$\Phi^{\mathrm{f}} - \mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}$ by the remaining
fourth-order Richardson and interpolation errors. The entire
supervision signal is thus built from coarse solves. The fine solve is
retained only for evaluation, and at inference only the single
$k = 4$ solve is required. The target is largest
on the under-resolved wavefronts, where the stencil dephases the
high-wavenumber content of the prompt burst, and near zero
elsewhere. This defines a localized residual-learning problem rather
than full-field synthesis, whose spatial localisation the gate of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:hyb-gate} exploits.

\paragraph{Upsample operator.} The operator $\mathcal{U}$ used
in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:hyb-richardson}--\eqref{eq:hyb-residual} and as
the channel-0 prior is implemented with a degree-five tensor-product
spline on the rectangular grid:
\begin{equation}
  (\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}})(x, t)
  \;=\; \sum_{i, j} c_{ij}\,
        B_{5}\!\Bigl(\tfrac{t - t_{i}}{\Delta t_{\mathrm{c}}}\Bigr)\,
        B_{5}\!\Bigl(\tfrac{x - x_{j}}{\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}}\Bigr),
  \label{eq:hyb-upsample}
\end{equation}
with $B_{5}$ the quintic B-spline basis and $\{c_{ij}\}$ fixed
by interpolation at the coarse nodes. The quintic interpolant was used
so that interpolation error is smaller than the Richardson residual in
the regimes tested. For smooth data, the degree-five spline has a
higher formal order than the fourth-order Richardson remainder. The
same $\mathcal{U}$ is applied to both coarse solves.

\paragraph{Input stack.} The five input channels are listed in
Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-arch}: the upsampled prior
$\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}$ (the coarse dynamical prior), the initial datum
$\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, 0)$, the potential $V_{\ell}(x; M)$ of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli}, which supplies the mass-dependent spatial
operator, the broadcast scalar $M$, included as an explicit parameter
channel, and the initial velocity
\begin{equation}
  \Pi^{\mathrm{c}}_{0}(x)
  \;=\; \frac{\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, \Delta t_{\mathrm{c}}) -
              \Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, 0)}{\Delta t_{\mathrm{c}}},
  \label{eq:hyb-pi0}
\end{equation}
forward-Euler differenced on the coarse grid and linearly
resampled onto the fine $x$-grid. This channel is used only as an
auxiliary input, not as a supervised target.

\paragraph{Residual operator, gradient gate, and reconstruction.}
The neural residual operator
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{H}_{\theta}\colon\;
  X = \bigl[\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}},\,\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(\cdot, 0),\,
            V_{\ell}(\cdot; M),\,M,\,\Pi^{\mathrm{c}}_{0}\bigr]
  \;\longmapsto\;
  \delta\Phi_{\theta}(x, t)
  \label{eq:hyb-operator}
\end{equation}
is modulated by a parameter-free gradient gate before it is
added to the prior. The gate
\begin{equation}
  g(x, t)
  \;=\; \frac{|\nabla\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, t)|}
             {|\nabla\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, t)| + s},
  \qquad
  |\nabla\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}|
  = \sqrt{(\partial_{t}\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}})^{2}
          + (\partial_{x}\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}})^{2}},
  \label{eq:hyb-gate}
\end{equation}
is a deterministic function of the prior alone, with a single
scale $s = 10^{-3}$ whose label-free selection from the prior
gradient distribution is derived in App.~\ref{app:gate-scale}:
$g \to 1$ on the high-gradient wavefronts where the prior
carries its dispersion error, and $g \to 0$ in the smooth
interior and causal exterior. The hybrid prediction is the
gated additive skip
\begin{equation}
  \Phi^{\mathrm{hyb}}(x, t)
  \;=\; \mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, t)
         + g(x, t)\,\mathcal{H}_{\theta}(X)(x, t),
  \label{eq:hyb-reconstruct}
\end{equation}
which confines the correction to the under-resolved regions by
construction and reduces to the quintic-upsampled coarse solve
(the ``coarse-up'' baseline of Sec.~\ref{sec:res-hyb}) wherever
$g = 0$ or $\mathcal{H}_{\theta} \equiv 0$. The gate strongly
suppresses the correction in low-gradient regions and uses the stable
coarse solve as an explicit additive prior.

\paragraph{FNO architecture.} The residual operator
$\mathcal{H}_{\theta}$ is a 2-D Fourier neural operator
\citep{Li2021fno,Kovachki2023neuralop} on the fine $(t, x)$
grid: a $1\times 1$ lift to $h = 32$ hidden channels, $L = 4$
Fourier layers of the form
\begin{equation}
  v^{(\ell+1)}(x, t)
  \;=\; \mathrm{GELU}\!\Bigl(
         \mathcal{F}^{-1}\bigl[R^{(\ell)} \odot
         \mathcal{F}\,v^{(\ell)}\bigr](x, t)
         \;+\; W^{(\ell)}\,v^{(\ell)}(x, t)
       \Bigr),
  \label{eq:hyb-fno-layer}
\end{equation}
and a $1\times 1$ projection back to one channel, where
$\mathcal{F}$ is the 2-D DFT along $(t, x)$, $R^{(\ell)}$ the
complex spectral-weight tensor truncated to
$(K_{t}, K_{x}) = (16, 32)$ retained modes (the grid's natural
$2{:}1$ mode ratio, resolving
$k_{x} \lesssim 1.0\,M^{-1}$), $\odot$ mode-by-mode matrix
multiplication across channels, and $W^{(\ell)}$ a pointwise
residual path. Each axis is pre-padded by $p = 0.10$ against
periodic wraparound, and positional $(x, t)$ encodings break
translation equivariance to distinguish the source region from
the wave zones. The total parameter count is $1.13\times 10^{6}$ under the
\texttt{neuraloperator} implementation \citep{Kovachki2023neuralop}.
This smaller width is chosen because the FNO acts on the residual rather
than the full field.

\paragraph{Loss.} The training loss is the gridwise mean
squared error between the gated network output and the
label-free Richardson residual,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{hyb}}(\theta)
  \;=\; \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}
         \frac{1}{N_{t} N_{x}}
         \sum_{i=1}^{N_{t}}\sum_{j=1}^{N_{x}}
         \bigl[
           g^{(b)}_{ij}\,\mathcal{H}_{\theta}(X^{(b)})_{ij}
           \,-\, \delta\Phi^{(b)}_{\mathrm{R},\,ij}
         \bigr]^{2},
  \label{eq:hyb-loss}
\end{equation}
averaged over the batch of size $B$. We use no auxiliary loss terms.
The target is lower-amplitude and spatially localized, so we therefore
train with a uniform MSE on the gated output.

\paragraph{Optimiser.} The hFNO is trained with Adam for $100$ epochs
using the settings in Table~\ref{tab:train-config}. A trial L-BFGS
phase did not improve the validation loss. The per-epoch validation
history is shown in Sec.~\ref{sec:res-hyb}.

\paragraph{Training distribution.} The Sobol parameter ranges, data
split and canonical configuration are listed in
Table~\ref{tab:surrogate-config}. The fine, $k = 4$ and $k = 2$
solves are precomputed and cached per triple. The ranges of $x_0$ and
$\sigma$ keep the prompt burst within the simulation window for all
sampled configurations.

\paragraph{Observer location.} Schwarzschild hFNO quantities are
reported at $\xq = 10\,M$, the same observer used for the PINN
fields, so every Schwarzschild model in this report is compared at a
single observer position;
the Kerr hFNO of Sec.~\ref{sec:kerr-method} instead reads
its waveform at null infinity, the natural observer of its
hyperboloidal slice.

\subsection{Kerr Teukolsky numerical setup}
\label{sec:teukolsky-numerics}

The Kerr extension uses the spin-$-2$ Teukolsky problem reviewed in
Sec.~\ref{sec:teukolsky-problem}. The numerical setup must specify the
radial coordinate, time coordinate and waveform readout. The
Schwarzschild calculations use a finite-radius readout in the tortoise
coordinate. For the radiative Kerr problem we instead use a
horizon-penetrating hyperboloidal foliation, following the geometric
programme of \citet{Zenginoglu2011} and the Kerr Teukolsky formulation
of \citet{Harms2013}. We use the minimal-gauge compactification
developed for Kerr by \citet{PanossoMacedo2020}. The radial coordinate
is compactified onto the unit interval,
\begin{equation}
  \sigma = \frac{r_{+}}{r} \in [0, 1],
  \qquad
  \sigma = 1 \ \text{(horizon)}, \quad
  \sigma = 0 \ (\scri),
  \label{eq:kerr-sigma}
\end{equation}
The time coordinate is replaced by a hyperboloidal time $\tau$ whose
constant-time slices connect the horizon to $\scri$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-slicing}). On this slice the radial
characteristics leave the domain at both endpoints, into the horizon
and through $\scri$. The evolution is therefore fixed by regular
initial data, with no external boundary data imposed
\citep{Harms2013,PanossoMacedo2020}. To keep the evolved field finite
at both endpoints we use
\begin{equation}
  \Psi = \psi\,\sigma^{3}\,(1 - \sigma)^{-2 - i\beta},
  \qquad
  \beta = \frac{m a}{r_{+} - r_{-}},
  \label{eq:kerr-rescale}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma^3$ accounts for the asymptotic peeling at $\scri$, while
$(1-\sigma)^{-2-i\beta}$ regularises the horizon behaviour. The phase
$\beta = ma/(r_+-r_-)$ carries the spin-dependent azimuthal
frame-dragging contribution.

\paragraph{Reference solution and target mode.} The reference field is
obtained by evolving the spin-$-2$ Teukolsky equation on this
hyperboloidal slice. After factoring out $e^{im\varphi}$, each
azimuthal number $m$ gives a $1+1$ evolution in $(\tau,\sigma)$.
We integrate this system with a fourth-order Runge--Kutta method of
lines and Kreiss--Oliger dissipation, following the hyperboloidal
Teukolsky scheme of \citet{Harms2013}. It is the Kerr reference
solution, analogous to the Schwarzschild FD reference of
Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-problem}. Reference frequencies and damping times
are computed at each spin using Leaver's continued-fraction method
\citep{Leaver1985} through the \texttt{qnm} package
\citep{Stein2019qnm}. These values define the percentage errors
reported below, in the same way as Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-ref} does for
Schwarzschild. We target the fundamental $(\ell,m,n)=(4,2,0)$ mode.
The choice $\ell=4$ rather than $\ell=2$ is deliberate. For the
$\ell=2$ fundamental, the coarse prior already resolves the frequency
near the extractor floor, leaving little spectroscopic headroom for
the correction. The faster $\ell=4$ mode
(Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-spectrum}a) is more sensitive to the coarse-grid
wavefront error that the hFNO is designed to reduce.

combination differs from that reference by a median field RL2 of
$0.008\%$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:res-kerr}), providing a three-resolution
consistency check on the numerical fields used for learning and
evaluation.

\subsection{The Kerr hFNO}
\label{sec:kerr-method}

The hFNO is the coarse-solve-plus-residual-FNO
construction of Sec.~\ref{sec:hyb-method}
(Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-arch}), retrained on the Teukolsky problem
with four adaptations dictated by the hyperboloidal geometry
(Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-arch}). (i)~Because the hyperboloidal Kerr
corpus is built on smooth nested spatial grids, we use a fourth-order
Richardson target
\begin{equation}
  u^{\star} = \frac{16\, u_{401} - u_{201}}{15},
  \label{eq:kerr-richardson}
\end{equation}
where $u_{401}$ and $u_{201}$ denote the $N = 401$ and $N = 201$
coarse fields after spatial upsampling to the fine $\sigma$ grid.
(ii)~The deployment prior is a single $N = 101$ hyperboloidal solve,
upsampled to the fine $\sigma$ grid before entering the FNO. The
Richardson grids are used only to build the training target, so
deployment cost is set by the $N = 101$ prior solve and the fixed-grid
FNO evaluation. (iii)~The complex prior is represented by its real and
imaginary parts, and the FNO also receives the broadcast spin $a/M$ and
the initial real pulse as input channels. The residual output has two
channels, representing $\mathrm{Re}\,\delta\Psi$ and
$\mathrm{Im}\,\delta\Psi$. (iv)~The gradient gate is removed because the Kerr residual is not confined to
high-gradient wavefronts on the hyperboloidal slice. In particular, phase
and damping errors at $\scri$ persist through the ringdown, so a prior-gradient
gate would suppress corrections in the low-amplitude late-time signal used for
QNM extraction. The network has $7.7 \times 10^{6}$ parameters and trains for
$200$ epochs on Sobol samples in $a/M\in[0,0.95]$, $r_0\in[8,11]$ and
$w\in[1,1.5]$; as for Schwarzschild no fine-grid field enters its loss,
and it reaches a best validation MSE of $1.4\times10^{-4}$ on the
normalised residual target.

\subsection{QNM extraction methods}
\label{sec:qnm-methods}

Once a model has produced a field, the QNM parameters
$(\omega,\tau)$ are extracted post-hoc from its observer time
series. The PINN fields are read at $\xq = 10\,M$ over
$[10,50]\,M$. The Schwarzschild hFNO is read at the same observer
over its $[10,100]\,M$ evolution, and the Kerr hFNO is read at
null infinity (Sec.~\ref{sec:teukolsky-numerics}). In the intermediate
ringdown regime, after the prompt response and before the
late-time tail, the observer signal is modelled as a sum of
damped sinusoids,
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(\xq, t) \;\approx\;
  \sum_{n} A_n\,\mathrm{e}^{-(t-t_0)/\tau_n}
  \cos\bigl(\omega_n(t-t_0) + \phi_n\bigr).
  \label{eq:qnm-template}
\end{equation}
For Schwarzschild we use five estimators. M1 and M2 reproduce the
two estimators used by \citet{Patel2024pinn}. M3 adds an ESPRIT
subspace estimator for damped exponentials \citep{Roy1989esprit}.
M4 and M5 introduce one- and two-edge plateau scans over the fit
window, relying on the operational definition below (formalised and
motivated in Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}). The Kerr analysis uses the
same real-valued estimators on $\mathrm{Re}\,\Psi$ and
$\mathrm{Im}\,\Psi$, together with the complex estimators defined
below.

\begin{definition}[fit-window plateau]
\label{def:plateau}
We call a fit window $[t_0,\,\tend]$ stable when small changes of
the window edges produce no significant change in the recovered
$(\omega,\tau)$. Operationally, in M4 and M5 the plateau is the
contiguous block of fixed size $w = \lfloor\tfrac12 N\rfloor$ along
each scan axis that minimises the combined relative population
standard deviation
$\mathrm{std}(\omega)/|\bar\omega|+\mathrm{std}(\tau)/|\bar\tau|$ of
the per-window fits inside the block (population standard deviation,
divisor $N$).
\end{definition}

Each estimator is given as pseudocode in
Algorithms~\ref{alg:m1}--\ref{alg:m5}. M1--M3 act on a single
window. M1 estimates the damping time from the slope of the
logarithmic envelope at waveform extrema,
\begin{equation}
  \log|\Phi(\xq, t_k)| \;\approx\;
  \log A_{0} - \frac{t_k - t_0}{\tau_{0}},
  \label{eq:logline}
\end{equation}
and M3 applies ESPRIT to the uniformly sampled analytic signal,
modelled as
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(\xq, k\Delta t)
  \;\approx\; \sum_{n=0}^{K-1} c_{n}\, z_{n}^{\,k},
  \qquad
  z_{n} \;=\; e^{(-1/\tau_{n} + \mathrm{i}\omega_{n})\Delta t}.
  \label{eq:esprit-model}
\end{equation}
This places the Fourier/log-envelope, NLS, Prony/matrix-pencil
and ESPRIT family of extractors in the same finite-window
time-series setting
\citep{Buonanno2007ringdown,Berti2007prony,HuaSarkar1990,Roy1989esprit}.
M4 and M5 use a two-component damped-sinusoid model, motivated by
the overtone sensitivity discussed by \citet{Giesler2019overtones},
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\mathrm{model}}(t) =
  A_0\,e^{-t/\tau_0}\cos(\omega_0 t + \phi_0)
  + A_1\,e^{-t/\tau_1}\cos(\omega_1 t + \phi_1),
  \label{eq:two-mode}
\end{equation}
with the longer-$\tau$ component taken as the fundamental, so the
second component can absorb early-time overtone power or residual
prompt contamination that would otherwise bias a single-mode fit.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{M1 --- spectral peak and log-envelope slope, the
\citet{Patel2024pinn} procedure.}
\label{alg:m1}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on window $[t_0,\tend]$.
\STATE $\widehat{\Phi} \gets$ zero-padded, Hann-windowed FFT of $\Phi$.
\STATE $\omega \gets$ parabolic interpolation of the peak bin of $|\widehat{\Phi}|$.
\STATE $\{t_k\} \gets$ local maxima of $|\Phi(\xq,t)|$;\
       $\tau \gets -1/\mathrm{slope}$ of the OLS fit of
       Eq.~\eqref{eq:logline} to $\log|\Phi(\xq,t_k)|$ versus $t_k$.
\STATE \textbf{return} $(\omega,\tau)$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{M2 --- single damped-sinusoid nonlinear least squares.}
\label{alg:m2}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on window $[t_0,\tend]$.
\STATE $(A,\omega,\tau,\phi) \gets \arg\min \sum_{t}
       \bigl[\Phi(\xq,t) - A\,e^{-t/\tau}\cos(\omega t + \phi)\bigr]^2$
  by nonlinear least squares, initialised from M1.
\STATE \textbf{return} $(\omega,\tau)$. \hfill{\footnotesize\itshape most overtone-sensitive}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{M3 --- ESPRIT subspace decomposition \citep{Roy1989esprit}.}
\label{alg:m3}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on window $[t_0,\tend]$, model order $K$.
\STATE $s \gets$ Hilbert-analytic signal of $\Phi(\xq,t)$.
\STATE $\{z_n\}_{n=0}^{K-1} \gets$ ESPRIT poles of $s$
       (model Eq.~\eqref{eq:esprit-model}),
       $z_n = e^{(-1/\tau_n + i\omega_n)\Delta t}$.
\STATE Keep physical poles with $\tau_n > 0$ and $\omega_n > 0$;
  $(\omega_0,\tau_0) \gets$ the dominant pole for the Schwarzschild
  real-signal estimator.
\STATE \textbf{return} $(\omega_0,\tau_0)$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{M4 --- two-mode NLS with a 1-D start-time plateau scan.}
\label{alg:m4}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\Phi(\xq,t)$,
       grid $\{t_0^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{16} \subset [10,25]\,M$,
       fixed $\tend$ at the domain end, plateau width $w = 8$.
\FOR{$k = 1,\dots,16$}
  \STATE Fit Eq.~\eqref{eq:two-mode} to $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on $[t_0^{(k)},\tend]$
         by NLS.
  \STATE Identify the longer-$\tau$ component as the fundamental,
         giving $(\omega_0^{(k)},\tau_0^{(k)})$.
\ENDFOR
\STATE Find the contiguous index range $k^* : k^*{+}w{-}1$ minimising
       the combined relative scatter
       $\mathrm{std}(\omega_0)/\bar\omega_0 + \mathrm{std}(\tau_0)/\bar\tau_0$.
\STATE \textbf{return} plateau mean
       $(\bar\omega_0,\bar\tau_0)$ and population standard deviation
       $(\sigma_{\omega_0},\sigma_{\tau_0})$ over the plateau block.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{M5 --- two-mode NLS with a 2-D two-edge plateau scan.}
\label{alg:m5}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\Phi(\xq,t)$, grid
       $t_0 \in [10,25]\,M$ and $\tend$ from $30\,M$ to the domain
       end ($10\times 6$),
       plateau block $5\times 3$.
\FOR{each window $(t_0,\tend)$ on the grid}
  \STATE Fit Eq.~\eqref{eq:two-mode} by NLS; the longer-$\tau$ component
         is the fundamental, giving $(\omega_0,\tau_0)$.
\ENDFOR
\STATE Find the contiguous $5\times 3$ rectangle minimising the combined
       relative scatter
       $\mathrm{std}(\omega_0)/\bar\omega_0 + \mathrm{std}(\tau_0)/\bar\tau_0$
       (Def.~\ref{def:plateau}, applied per axis).
\STATE \textbf{return} plateau mean $(\bar\omega_0,\bar\tau_0)$ and
       spread $(\sigma_{\omega_0},\sigma_{\tau_0})$ over the rectangle.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Our implementation evaluates M1--M5 simultaneously on each field
and returns all five $(\omega,\tau)$ estimates; M4 and M5
additionally supply the plateau spread
$(\sigma_\omega,\sigma_\tau)$ as a window-sensitivity diagnostic,
and the reporting convention of
Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting} selects the primary value. ESPRIT (M3)
is ill-conditioned on the PINN waveforms at $\xq = 10\,M$
($K = 6$) and is quoted only where it
is well-conditioned, on the hybrid-pipeline fields; to our
knowledge M5 is the first two-edge plateau scan used for
PINN-derived time-domain QNM extraction. Both plateau scans are
sketched in Fig.~\ref{fig:windows}.

\paragraph{Kerr complex estimators.} The Schwarzschild field is
real, but the Teukolsky field read at $\scri$ is genuinely
complex, $\Psi(t) = \sum_n A_n e^{-i\omega_n t}$
(Sec.~\ref{sec:teukolsky-numerics}). In the Kerr case only, M1--M5
are therefore run on $\mathrm{Re}\,\Psi$ and $\mathrm{Im}\,\Psi$
separately and the suite is augmented with two complex-native
estimators that act on $\Psi$ directly. The complex-phase fit
c$\Phi$ (Algorithm~\ref{alg:cphi}) is the complex analogue of M1:
a single mode has $\log|\Psi| = \log|A| - t/\tau_{\mathrm{d}}$ and
$\arg\Psi = \arg A - \omega_R\,t$, so the damping time follows from
the envelope slope and the frequency from the slope of the unwrapped
phase, with no spectral peak-finding. Complex ESPRIT cES
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:ces}) is the complex analogue of M3: because
$\Psi$ is already a sum of complex exponentials, the subspace
decomposition is applied to it with no Hilbert transform. In the
ideal model each physical mode appears once, and nearby damped
components can be separated when the window and model order are well
conditioned. For Kerr, the labelled Leaver frequency identifies the
target pole among multi-mode candidates. The reported value is then
the best-of-suite estimate for the target $(4,2,0)$ multiplet.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{c$\Phi$ --- complex-phase fit (Kerr only), the complex
analogue of M1.}
\label{alg:cphi}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} complex $\Psi(\scri,\tau)$ on window
       $[\tau_0,\tend]$.
\STATE $1/\tau_{\mathrm{d}} \gets -\,\mathrm{slope}$ of the OLS fit
  of $\log|\Psi|$ versus $\tau$; \ $\tau \gets \tau_{\mathrm{d}}$.
\STATE $\omega_R \gets -\,\mathrm{slope}$ of the OLS fit of the
       unwrapped phase $\arg\Psi$ versus $\tau$.
\STATE \textbf{return} $(\omega_R,\tau)$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{cES --- complex ESPRIT (Kerr only), the complex analogue
of M3.}
\label{alg:ces}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} complex $\Psi(\scri,\tau)$ on window
  $[\tau_0,\tend]$, model order $K$.
\STATE $\{z_n\}_{n=0}^{K-1} \gets$ ESPRIT poles of $\Psi$ directly
  (no Hilbert transform).
\STATE Convert poles using
  $\omega_{R,n} = -\mathrm{Im}(\log z_n)/\Delta\tau$ and
  $\tau_n = -\Delta\tau/\mathrm{Re}(\log z_n)$.
\STATE Keep physical modes ($\tau_n > 0$), sorted by descending
  $\tau_n$; in the Kerr target-mode analysis $K$ is scanned and
  the labelled Leaver frequency identifies the target pole.
\STATE \textbf{return} the selected target-mode value $(\omega_R,\tau)$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Plateau-based fit-window selection}
\label{sec:plateau}

The damped-sinusoid template
$A\,e^{-t/\tau}\cos(\omega t + \phi)$ is an appropriate
approximation for $\Phi(\xq,t)$ only inside the QNM regime, after
the prompt response has passed and before the power-law tail becomes
comparable to the exponential. Fitting it outside this regime is
\emph{model misspecification}, not a noise problem. The resulting
biases do not vanish with more data, so the fit window is a
hyperparameter of the extraction and a robust
$(\omega,\tau)$ measurement requires a reproducible criterion
for it. Definition~\ref{def:plateau} captures the criterion
adopted in M4 and M5; inside the plateau the recovered
$(\omega,\tau)$ are stationary in the window edges, and their
variation across it is reported as a window-sensitivity diagnostic.
Because the block is chosen precisely to minimise this scatter,
$\sigma_\omega$ is by construction an optimistic estimate, a lower
bound on the window sensitivity. The spread across the full scan
range, visible in the M4 diagnostics of Sec.~\ref{sec:results},
brackets it from above.

For the Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$ fundamental at our parameters the
four relevant timescales are summarised by
\begin{equation}
  t_{\mathrm{burst}} \sim 5\,M, \qquad
  \tau_1 = 3.651\,M, \qquad
  \tau_0 = 11.241\,M, \qquad
  t_{\mathrm{tail}} \gg 50\,M,
  \label{eq:timescales}
\end{equation}
where $t_{\mathrm{burst}}$ is the prompt-burst duration set by
the initial condition Eq.~\eqref{eq:ic} and $t_{\mathrm{tail}}$
is the onset of the power-law Price tail
$\propto t^{-(2\ell+3)}$ \citep{Berti2009review}. For our setup
the QNM dominates $\Phi(\xq,t)$ over the entire simulation
interval (confirmed by direct inspection of the FD reference),
so any right edge inside $[0,50]\,M$ lies within the QNM regime
as borne out by the M5 plateaus extending in $\tend$ to the domain
end (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
This differs from the single-window practice of
\citet{Patel2024pinn}, who specify no numerical $t_0$ or $\tend$
and report no window sensitivity. The closest precedents are the
start-time stability scans of \citet{Buonanno2007ringdown} and
the GW150914 ringdown analysis \citep{LIGO2016ringdown}, both
sweeping $t_0$ at fixed $\tend$, and the Bayesian start-time
marginalisation of \citet{Isi2021priors}. The present approach
extends these by sweeping both edges simultaneously (M5) and by
quoting the plateau spread as a window-sensitivity diagnostic,
in lieu of the nonlinear-fit covariance which our
M2 implementation discards (Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}).

\subsection{Reporting convention}
\label{sec:reporting}

We quote one $(\omega,\tau)$ per experiment. The five Schwarzschild
extractors of Sec.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods} do not use the Leaver
reference during extraction. As expected of a heterogeneous
suite, some fail on a given field. A fixed-window fit can land off the
plateau, ESPRIT can mis-order the modes, and a single draw may form no
plateau at all. We therefore report, \emph{per experiment and per
quantity}, the estimator whose value lies closest to the Leaver
reference, annotated with the estimator used (e.g.\ $0.152\%$~(M4)).
For the labelled Kerr corpus, the Leaver frequency is additionally
used to identify the target pole among multi-mode candidates, as
described in Sec.~\ref{sec:teukolsky-numerics}. Where the selected
$\omega$ is the M4 plateau mean, the $\sigma_\omega$ quoted alongside
it is the M4 plateau standard deviation (Algorithm~\ref{alg:m4},
line~7), reported as a window-sensitivity diagnostic in the sense of
Def.~\ref{def:plateau}.

The lower edge $t = 10\,M$, used unchanged across all
extraction variants, sits just before the first ringdown peak
of the FD reference ($t \approx 10.8\,M$).

Full per-window per-method results are tabulated in the
repository documentation; the summary table
in Sec.~\ref{sec:results} collects only the values selected by
this convention.

Quoting different estimators for $\omega$ and $\tau$ follows
established numerical-relativity practice
\citep{Berti2007ringdown,Patel2024pinn}: $\omega$ is best
estimated from phase information aggregated across many cycles
--- the M4 plateau is the lowest-variance such estimator here,
with $\sigma_\omega \sim 10^{-5}$ for the converged runs ---
and $\tau$ from amplitude information over the longest available
decay range, motivating the wide M2 window.
