\subsection{Schwarzschild Zerilli problem}
\label{sec:fwd-problem}

The forward problem is the Zerilli equation
Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} with the polar potential
$V_{\ell}^{\mathrm{Z}}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli} at
$\ell = 2$, $M = 1$, on the numerical domain
$x \in [-50,\,150]\,M$, $t \in [0,\,50]\,M$, with
outgoing-Gaussian initial data
\begin{equation}
  \Phi(x,0) = A_0\,\exp\!\left[-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\sigma_0^2}\right],
  \qquad
  \partial_t \Phi(x,0) = -\partial_x \Phi(x,0),
  \label{eq:ic}
\end{equation}
with $A_0 = 1$, $x_0 = 4\,M$ and $\sigma_0 = 5\,M$. The velocity
condition imposes the conventional purely outgoing pulse
\citep{Berti2009review}, in contrast to the quadratic
$\partial_t \Phi \propto (x-x_0)^2 \Phi$ of
\citet{Patel2024pinn}. At the truncated boundaries we apply
first-order Sommerfeld absorbing conditions in place of the
asymptotic QNM conditions Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-bc},
\begin{equation}
  (\partial_t - \partial_x)\Phi\bigl|_{x=x_{\min}} = 0, \qquad
  (\partial_t + \partial_x)\Phi\bigl|_{x=x_{\max}} = 0,
  \label{eq:bc-sommerfeld}
\end{equation}
identical to those used by \citet{Patel2024pinn}. The domain is
sized so that the wavefront, propagating at unit speed from
$x_0 = 4\,M$, never reaches either boundary within
$t \in [0,50]\,M$: the arrival times
\begin{equation}
  t_{R} = x_{\max} - x_0 = 146\,M, \qquad
  t_{L} = x_0 - x_{\min} = 54\,M,
  \label{eq:bc-arrival}
\end{equation}
both exceed the extraction window, so the boundary residuals
act only as soft regularisers in a causally disconnected
region.

The reference solution (Fig.~\ref{fig:waveform-3d}) is a
fourth-order Runge--Kutta method-of-lines finite-difference (FD)
integration of Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli} with $\Delta x = 0.2\,M$
and $\Delta t = 0.1\,M$, used throughout as the ground truth for
field accuracy and for cross-checking the extractors on a
non-PINN signal. The Leaver values $M\omega_0 = 0.3737$,
$\tau_0/M = 11.241$ (fundamental) and $M\omega_1 = 0.3467$,
$\tau_1/M = 3.651$ (first overtone) of Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-ref}
are used for percentage-error reporting.

\subsection{Forward PINN}
\label{sec:fwd-pinn}

We solve the forward problem of Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-problem} with a
fully-connected PINN
\citep{Raissi2017} implemented in DeepXDE \citep{Lu2021deepxde} with
the PyTorch backend \citep{Paszke2019pytorch}.

Architecture, output transform and weight initialiser are chosen
identically to \citet{Patel2024pinn}, so that differences in
extracted QNMs are attributable to training and extraction
rather than the network itself: hidden layers $[80,40,20,10]$
with $\tanh$ activations, input $(x,t) \in \mathbb{R}^{2}$, and
a single scalar output wrapped in the tanh envelope
$\Phi_\theta(x,t) = A\tanh\bigl(\hat y(x,t)\bigr)$ with
$A = A_0 = 1$, giving $4{,}521$ trainable
parameters.\footnote{$2{\cdot}80 + 80 + 80{\cdot}40 + 40 +
40{\cdot}20 + 20 + 20{\cdot}10 + 10 + 10{\cdot}1 + 1 = 4521$.}
The architecture is summarised in Fig.~\ref{fig:fwd-pinn-arch},
together with the automatic-differentiation pipeline that
produces the residual losses defined below.

Let $r_\theta(x,t) \equiv \partial_t^2\Phi_\theta -
\partial_x^2\Phi_\theta + V_\ell\,\Phi_\theta$ be the pointwise
residual of Eq.~\eqref{eq:zerilli}. The training loss is the
standard composite of PDE, initial-condition and boundary-condition
residuals, augmented with the gradient terms of the
gradient-enhanced PINN \citep{Yu2022gpinn},
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{fwd}}
  = \lambda_{r}     \Lpde
  + \lambda_{r_x}   \mathcal{L}_{\partial_x r}
  + \lambda_{r_t}   \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t r}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{IC}} \mathcal{L}_{\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{IV}} \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{BL}} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{left}}
  + \lambda_{\mathrm{BR}} \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{right}}.
  \label{eq:loss-fwd}
\end{equation}
The seven mean-squared component losses are
\begin{align}
  \Lpde &= \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{(x,t)\in\mathcal{X}_r}
            r_\theta(x,t)^{2},
  & \mathcal{L}_{\partial_x r} &= \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{(x,t)\in\mathcal{X}_r}
            \bigl(\partial_x r_\theta(x,t)\bigr)^{2},
  \nonumber\\
  \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t r} &= \frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{(x,t)\in\mathcal{X}_r}
            \bigl(\partial_t r_\theta(x,t)\bigr)^{2},
  & \mathcal{L}_{\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}} &= \frac{1}{N_i}\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}_i}
            \bigl(\Phi_\theta(x,0)-\Phi(x,0)\bigr)^{2},
  \nonumber\\
  \mathcal{L}_{\partial_t\Phi}^{\mathrm{IC}}
     &= \frac{1}{N_i}\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}_i}
        \bigl(\partial_t\Phi_\theta(x,0)-\partial_t\Phi(x,0)\bigr)^{2},
  & \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{left}}
     &= \frac{1}{N_b}\sum_{t\in\mathcal{X}_b^{L}}
        \bigl((\partial_t-\partial_x)\Phi_\theta(x_{\min},t)\bigr)^{2},
  \nonumber\\
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{BC}}^{\mathrm{right}}
     &= \frac{1}{N_b}\sum_{t\in\mathcal{X}_b^{R}}
        \bigl((\partial_t+\partial_x)\Phi_\theta(x_{\max},t)\bigr)^{2},
  & &
  \label{eq:loss-components}
\end{align}
mirroring the formulation of \citet[Eqs.~(4)--(10)]{Patel2024pinn}.
The two gradient terms $\mathcal{L}_{\partial_x r},
\mathcal{L}_{\partial_t r}$ drive the residual to vanish
smoothly rather than oscillate about zero \citep{Yu2022gpinn}. The collocation set sizes are listed in
Table~\ref{tab:fwd-hyper}, with $N_b = 400$ boundary points
shared across both boundaries where \citet{Patel2024pinn} quote
$400$ \emph{per boundary} --- the only collocation difference
between the two baselines. The weight vector applied throughout this work is
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\lambda}
  = (\lambda_{r},\lambda_{r_x},\lambda_{r_t},\lambda_{\mathrm{IC}},
     \lambda_{\mathrm{IV}},\lambda_{\mathrm{BL}},\lambda_{\mathrm{BR}})
  = (100,\,100,\,100,\,1,\,100,\,1,\,1),
  \label{eq:weights}
\end{equation}
following \citet{Patel2024pinn}: $\lambda_{\mathrm{IV}} = 100$
suppresses the phase drift reported (and reproduced here) when
the velocity condition is under-weighted, and the unit
Sommerfeld weights reflect the causal disconnection at
Eq.~\eqref{eq:bc-arrival}.

Optimisation is Adam \citep{Kingma2015adam} followed by L-BFGS
\citep{Liu1989lbfgs} at double the budget of
\citet{Patel2024pinn} (Table~\ref{tab:train-config}), since the
loss still decreases through iteration $\sim\!25{,}000$;
training is full-batch in double precision, which we found
necessary to avoid floating-point-induced L-BFGS stalls.
Every $1{,}000$ Adam iterations a greedy residual-resampling
scheme \citep{Lu2021deepxde,Wu2023adaptive} scores $10^{5}$
uniform candidates by $|r_\theta|$ and rebuilds the collocation
set as the top $0.3\,N_r$ by residual plus $0.7\,N_r$ uniform
redraws; the uniform floor prevents collapse onto the wavefront
late in training.

\subsection{Hybrid coarse-FD + neural-residual surrogate}
\label{sec:hyb-method}

Each PINN above solves a \emph{single} Zerilli instance,
parametrised by one mass $M$ and one Gaussian pulse
$(x_{0},\sigma)$; a new pulse or mass requires a fresh
$\mathcal{O}(10^{4})$-iteration run. A neural operator
(Sec.~\ref{sec:bg-fno}) removes this per-instance cost: trained
once over a family of problems, it predicts $\Phi(x,t)$ for an
unseen $(M,x_{0},\sigma)$ draw in a single forward pass. A
purely data-driven operator, however, must learn the entire
wave-propagation structure from data, and learned corrections
to cheap classical solvers have been found to outperform such
surrogates at matched cost
\citep{Um2020solverloop,Kochkov2021mlcfd}. The hybrid surrogate
adopts this design: it runs a single coarse-grid FD solve to
carry the wave propagation, upsamples it to the fine grid, and
trains a small 2-D FNO --- the residual operator
$\mathcal{H}_{\theta}$, $1.13\times 10^{6}$ parameters --- to
predict the pointwise discretisation residual, estimated
label-free by Richardson extrapolation of
two coarse solves. The network is never shown the fine-grid
field in its loss, and a parameter-free gradient gate confines
the correction to the under-resolved wavefronts, handing the
smooth interior and causal exterior back to the prior. The
extractors and reporting convention of
Secs.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods}--\ref{sec:reporting} apply
unchanged; the pipeline is summarised in
Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-arch}, and its configuration in
Table~\ref{tab:surrogate-config}.

\paragraph{Discretisation and grids.} The fine and coarse
grids (Table~\ref{tab:surrogate-config}) share the box and the
RK4 method-of-lines scheme of Appendix~\ref{app:fd} with the
same second-order centred stencil. Coarsening by $k$ cuts FD
work by $k^{3} = 64$, so the classical component of one
deployed prediction costs $\tfrac{1}{64}$ of the fine solve,
with the FNO forward pass small by comparison
(Sec.~\ref{sec:res-hyb}).

\paragraph{Label-free Richardson target.} At $k = 4$ the
second-order stencil leaves a large
$\mathcal{O}(\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}^{2})$ dispersion error: the
upsampled prior misses the fine reference by $4.93\%$ relative
$L^{2}$ on the canonical draw ($6.11\%$ in the population mean;
Sec.~\ref{sec:res-hyb}). We therefore run the same problem at
$k = 4$ and $k = 2$, upsample both to the fine grid with the
operator $\mathcal{U}$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:hyb-upsample}, and form
\begin{equation}
  \Phi^{\mathrm{R}}
  \;=\; \frac{4\,\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}_{2}
              - \mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}}{3},
  \label{eq:hyb-richardson}
\end{equation}
the standard $p = 2$ Richardson combination: the
$\mathcal{O}(\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}^{2})$ terms cancel and
$\Phi^{\mathrm{R}} = \Phi + \mathcal{O}(\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}^{4})$
up to the quintic-interpolation error of the upsample. The
network is trained to predict
the Richardson-estimated residual
\begin{equation}
  \delta\Phi_{\mathrm{R}}(x, t)
  \;=\; \Phi^{\mathrm{R}}(x, t) - \mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, t),
  \label{eq:hyb-residual}
\end{equation}
which agrees with the true error
$\Phi^{\mathrm{f}} - \mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}$ to
$\mathcal{O}(\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}^{4})$. The entire supervision
signal is thus built from coarse solves --- the fine solve is
retained only to \emph{evaluate} the surrogate, and at inference
only the single $k = 4$ solve is required. The target is largest
on the under-resolved wavefronts, where the stencil dephases the
high-wavenumber content of the prompt burst, and near zero
elsewhere: a bounded repair task rather than full-field
synthesis, whose spatial localisation the gate of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:hyb-gate} exploits.

\paragraph{Upsample operator.} The operator $\mathcal{U}$ used
in Eqs.~\eqref{eq:hyb-richardson}--\eqref{eq:hyb-residual} and as
the channel-0 prior is a separable degree-five (quintic)
tensor-product spline on the rectangular grid:
\begin{equation}
  (\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}})(x, t)
  \;=\; \sum_{i, j} c_{ij}\,
        B_{5}\!\Bigl(\tfrac{t - t_{i}}{\Delta t_{\mathrm{c}}}\Bigr)\,
        B_{5}\!\Bigl(\tfrac{x - x_{j}}{\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}}\Bigr),
  \label{eq:hyb-upsample}
\end{equation}
with $B_{5}$ the quintic B-spline basis and $\{c_{ij}\}$ fixed
by collocation at the coarse nodes. The quintic order keeps the
$\mathcal{O}(\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}^{6})$ interpolation error
below the $\mathcal{O}(\Delta x_{\mathrm{c}}^{4})$ Richardson
signal; the same $\mathcal{U}$ is applied to both coarse
solves.

\paragraph{Input stack.} The five input channels are listed in
Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-arch}: the upsampled prior
$\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}$ (the dynamical content the
network is asked to repair), the initial datum
$\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, 0)$, the potential $V_{\ell}(x; M)$ of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:V-zerilli} (the mass dependence in the form the
spectral $x$-convolution exploits), the broadcast scalar $M$
(redundant with it but empirically helpful at the prompt-burst
edge), and the initial velocity
\begin{equation}
  \Pi^{\mathrm{c}}_{0}(x)
  \;=\; \frac{\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, \Delta t_{\mathrm{c}}) -
              \Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, 0)}{\Delta t_{\mathrm{c}}},
  \label{eq:hyb-pi0}
\end{equation}
forward-Euler differenced on the coarse grid and linearly
resampled onto the fine $x$-grid; its first-order truncation is
acceptable for an auxiliary channel.

\paragraph{Residual operator, gradient gate, and reconstruction.}
The neural residual operator
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{H}_{\theta}\colon\;
  X = \bigl[\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}},\,\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(\cdot, 0),\,
            V_{\ell}(\cdot; M),\,M,\,\Pi^{\mathrm{c}}_{0}\bigr]
  \;\longmapsto\;
  \delta\Phi_{\theta}(x, t)
  \label{eq:hyb-operator}
\end{equation}
is modulated by a parameter-free gradient gate before it is
added to the prior. The gate
\begin{equation}
  g(x, t)
  \;=\; \frac{|\nabla\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, t)|}
             {|\nabla\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, t)| + s},
  \qquad
  |\nabla\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}|
  = \sqrt{(\partial_{t}\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}})^{2}
          + (\partial_{x}\mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}})^{2}},
  \label{eq:hyb-gate}
\end{equation}
is a deterministic function of the prior alone, with a single
scale $s = 10^{-3}$ whose label-free selection from the prior
gradient distribution is derived in App.~\ref{app:gate-scale}:
$g \to 1$ on the high-gradient wavefronts where the prior
carries its dispersion error, and $g \to 0$ in the smooth
interior and causal exterior. The hybrid prediction is the
gated additive skip
\begin{equation}
  \Phi^{\mathrm{hyb}}(x, t)
  \;=\; \mathcal{U}\Phi^{\mathrm{c}}(x, t)
         + g(x, t)\,\mathcal{H}_{\theta}(X)(x, t),
  \label{eq:hyb-reconstruct}
\end{equation}
which confines the correction to the under-resolved regions by
construction and reduces to the quintic-upsampled coarse solve
(the ``coarse-up'' baseline of Sec.~\ref{sec:res-hyb}) wherever
$g = 0$ or $\mathcal{H}_{\theta} \equiv 0$; the network sits in
series with the classical solver and inherits its stability
behaviour as a hard prior.

\paragraph{FNO architecture.} The residual operator
$\mathcal{H}_{\theta}$ is a 2-D Fourier neural operator
\citep{Li2021fno,Kovachki2023neuralop} on the fine $(t, x)$
grid: a $1\times 1$ lift to $h = 32$ hidden channels, $L = 4$
Fourier layers of the form
\begin{equation}
  v^{(\ell+1)}(x, t)
  \;=\; \mathrm{GELU}\!\Bigl(
         \mathcal{F}^{-1}\bigl[R^{(\ell)} \odot
         \mathcal{F}\,v^{(\ell)}\bigr](x, t)
         \;+\; W^{(\ell)}\,v^{(\ell)}(x, t)
       \Bigr),
  \label{eq:hyb-fno-layer}
\end{equation}
and a $1\times 1$ projection back to one channel, where
$\mathcal{F}$ is the 2-D DFT along $(t, x)$, $R^{(\ell)}$ the
complex spectral-weight tensor truncated to
$(K_{t}, K_{x}) = (16, 32)$ retained modes (the grid's natural
$2{:}1$ mode ratio, resolving
$k_{x} \lesssim 1.0\,M^{-1}$), $\odot$ mode-by-mode matrix
multiplication across channels, and $W^{(\ell)}$ a pointwise
residual path. Each axis is pre-padded by $p = 0.10$ against
periodic wraparound, and positional $(x, t)$ encodings break
translation equivariance to distinguish the source region from
the wave zones. The total parameter count is $1.13\times 10^{6}$ under the
\texttt{neuraloperator} factorised parametrisation
\citep{Kovachki2023neuralop} --- small because the residual
target has narrower spectral
support than the full field, since the wave-propagation
operator already lives in the upsampled prior.

\paragraph{Loss.} The training loss is the gridwise mean
squared error between the gated network output and the
label-free Richardson residual,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{hyb}}(\theta)
  \;=\; \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}
         \frac{1}{N_{t} N_{x}}
         \sum_{i=1}^{N_{t}}\sum_{j=1}^{N_{x}}
         \bigl[
           g^{(b)}_{ij}\,\mathcal{H}_{\theta}(X^{(b)})_{ij}
           \,-\, \delta\Phi^{(b)}_{\mathrm{R},\,ij}
         \bigr]^{2},
  \label{eq:hyb-loss}
\end{equation}
averaged over the batch of size $B$. No auxiliary terms are
needed: the target is small, smooth and localised and the gate
has already confined the correction to the wavefronts, so a
uniform MSE on the gated output suffices
(Sec.~\ref{sec:res-hyb}).

\paragraph{Optimiser.} A single Adam phase
(Table~\ref{tab:train-config}) reaches the validation floor
within $100$ epochs on this small network and smooth, localised
target; a trial L-BFGS phase gave no measurable gain. The
per-epoch validation history is shown in
Sec.~\ref{sec:res-hyb}.

\paragraph{Training distribution.} The Sobol distribution,
split and canonical BH are listed in
Table~\ref{tab:surrogate-config}; the fine, $k = 4$ and $k = 2$
solves are precomputed and cached per triple. The
$(x_{0}, \sigma)$ box is chosen to keep the prompt burst inside
the $t \in [0, 50]\,M$ window.

\paragraph{Observer location.} Hybrid numbers are reported at
$\xq = 10\,M$, the PINNs' observer, so every Schwarzschild
model in this report is compared at a single observer position;
the Kerr surrogate of Sec.~\ref{sec:kerr-method} instead reads
its waveform at null infinity, the natural observer of its
hyperboloidal slice.

\subsection{The Teukolsky problem}
\label{sec:teukolsky-problem}

The three models above all describe the non-rotating background
of Sec.~\ref{sec:bg-zerilli}, where the perturbation separates
into the real Regge--Wheeler and Zerilli wave equations.
Astrophysical remnants rotate. A rotating black hole of mass
$M$ and spin $a = J/M$ is described, in Boyer--Lindquist
coordinates, by the Kerr line element
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{d}s^{2} =
    -\left(1 - \frac{2Mr}{\Sigma}\right)\mathrm{d}t^{2}
    - \frac{4 M a r \sin^{2}\!\theta}{\Sigma}\,
       \mathrm{d}t\,\mathrm{d}\varphi
    + \frac{\Sigma}{\Delta}\,\mathrm{d}r^{2}
    + \Sigma\,\mathrm{d}\theta^{2}
    + \left(r^{2} + a^{2}
       + \frac{2 M a^{2} r \sin^{2}\!\theta}{\Sigma}\right)
       \sin^{2}\!\theta\,\mathrm{d}\varphi^{2},
  \label{eq:kerr-metric}
\end{equation}
with $\Sigma = r^{2} + a^{2}\cos^{2}\theta$ and
$\Delta = r^{2} - 2Mr + a^{2}$, reducing to the Schwarzschild
metric Eq.~\eqref{eq:schwarzschild} as $a \to 0$. The roots of
$\Delta$ are the outer and inner horizons
\begin{equation}
  r_{\pm} = M \pm \sqrt{M^{2} - a^{2}},
  \label{eq:kerr-horizons}
\end{equation}
which merge at extremality $a \to M$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-coords}a); the off-diagonal
$\mathrm{d}t\,\mathrm{d}\varphi$ term is the frame dragging that
distinguishes Kerr from Schwarzschild. We study spins
$a/M \in [0, 0.95]$, stopping short of extremality.

\paragraph{Teukolsky master equation.} Gravitational
perturbations of Kerr do not split into parity sectors; instead
every spin-weight $s$ field obeys the single Teukolsky master
equation \citep{Teukolsky1973}. For the gravitational
($s = -2$) field the separable ansatz
$\psi = e^{-i\omega t + i m\varphi}\,
{}_{-2}S_{\ell m}(\theta)\,R(r)$ yields a spheroidal angular
equation, whose eigenfunctions are the spin-weighted spheroidal
harmonics ${}_{-2}S_{\ell m}(\theta; a\omega)$ with separation
constant $\lambda$, and the radial equation
\begin{equation}
  \Delta^{2}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}r}\!
    \left(\Delta^{-1}\frac{\mathrm{d}R}{\mathrm{d}r}\right)
  + \left[\frac{K^{2} + 4 i (r - M) K}{\Delta}
          - 8 i \omega r - \lambda\right] R = 0,
  \qquad
  K = (r^{2} + a^{2})\,\omega - a m .
  \label{eq:teukolsky}
\end{equation}
Rotation enters through the $a m$ term in $K$, which couples the
frame dragging to the azimuthal number $m$ and renders the
potential complex, so --- unlike the real Regge--Wheeler and
Zerilli equations --- the Teukolsky field is complex-valued. As
$a \to 0$ Eq.~\eqref{eq:teukolsky} reduces to the
Bardeen--Press equation, whose spectrum is isospectral to
Regge--Wheeler. The quasinormal spectrum therefore depends on
the spin (Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-spectrum}): the real frequency
$M\omega_{R}$ rises and the damping time $\tau$ lengthens
towards extremality, so any surrogate spanning a family of
black holes must absorb this dependence.

\paragraph{Hyperboloidal minimal-gauge slice.} As for
Schwarzschild we need a computational domain on which the
ringdown is read directly at the observer. The tortoise
construction of Eq.~\eqref{eq:tortoise} places the observer at a
finite radius; for the radiative problem we instead adopt a
hyperboloidal slice in the minimal gauge \citep{Zenginoglu2011,
PanossoMacedo2018, PanossoMacedo2020, PanossoMacedo2024review},
whose level sets reach future null infinity $\scri$, where the
outgoing waveform is unambiguously defined. The radial
coordinate is compactified onto the unit interval,
\begin{equation}
  \sigma = \frac{r_{+}}{r} \in [0, 1],
  \qquad
  \sigma = 1 \ \text{(horizon)}, \quad
  \sigma = 0 \ (\scri),
  \label{eq:kerr-sigma}
\end{equation}
and the time coordinate is deformed to a hyperboloidal $\tau$
whose slices connect the horizon to $\scri$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-slicing}). On such a slice the radial
characteristics are outflow at both ends --- into the horizon
and out through $\scri$ --- so the evolution is fixed by initial
data alone, with no boundary conditions imposed
\citep{PanossoMacedo2020}. To keep the field finite at both
ends we evolve the rescaled variable
\begin{equation}
  \Psi = \psi\,\sigma^{3}\,(1 - \sigma)^{-2 - i\beta},
  \qquad
  \beta = \frac{m a}{r_{+} - r_{-}},
  \label{eq:kerr-rescale}
\end{equation}
whose spin-dependent complex exponent absorbs the oscillatory
near-horizon behaviour of the rotating field.

\paragraph{Reference solution and target mode.} The reference
field is a time-domain integration of the $2 + 1$ Teukolsky
equation on this slice, reduced per azimuthal number $m$ to a
$1 + 1$ problem, by a fourth-order Runge--Kutta method of lines
with Kreiss--Oliger dissipation, following the hyperboloidal
Teukolsky scheme of \citet{Harms2013}; it plays the same role
of ground truth here that the FD solver of
Sec.~\ref{sec:fwd-problem} plays for Schwarzschild. Reference
frequencies and damping times come from Leaver's
continued-fraction method \citep{Leaver1985} through the
\texttt{qnm} package \citep{Stein2019qnm} at each spin, used for
percentage-error reporting exactly as the Schwarzschild Leaver
values of Eq.~\eqref{eq:qnm-ref}. We target the fundamental
$(\ell, m, n) = (4, 2, 0)$ multipole. The choice $\ell = 4$
over $\ell = 2$ is deliberate: at $\ell = 2$ the coarse prior
already resolves the frequency to the reference floor, leaving
no headroom for the correction, whereas the faster-oscillating
$\ell = 4$ mode (Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-spectrum}a) is more
sensitive to the wavefront error the surrogate repairs. Because
the Teukolsky field is complex, the estimator suite of
Sec.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods} is applied to its real and imaginary
channels separately and augmented with two complex-signal
estimators --- complex ESPRIT (cES) and a complex-phase fit
(c$\Phi$); with spherical--spheroidal mixing populating the
ringdown with several nearby modes, the reported value is the
Leaver-closest of this suite (best-of-suite) for the target
$(4,2,0)$ multiplet, read at $\scri$ ($\sigma = 0$).

\subsection{The Kerr hybrid surrogate}
\label{sec:kerr-method}

The surrogate is the coarse-solve-plus-residual-FNO
construction of Sec.~\ref{sec:hyb-method}
(Fig.~\ref{fig:hyb-arch}), retrained on the Teukolsky problem
with four adaptations dictated by the hyperboloidal geometry
(Fig.~\ref{fig:kerr-arch}). (i)~Because the hyperboloidal field
is smoother than the Zerilli field, the second-order Richardson
target is replaced by the fourth-order combination
\begin{equation}
  u^{\star} = \frac{16\, u_{401} - u_{201}}{15},
  \label{eq:kerr-richardson}
\end{equation}
formed from two coarse solves at $N = 201$ and $N = 401$ grid
points. (ii)~The FNO reads a fixed coarse grid of $N = 101$
points, decoupled from the Richardson grids, so its cost is
independent of the reference resolution. (iii)~The complex
field is carried as two real channels
$(\mathrm{Re}\,\Psi, \mathrm{Im}\,\Psi)$. (iv)~The gradient gate
is removed: on the hyperboloidal slice the correction is not
confined to a localised wavefront (Sec.~\ref{sec:res-kerr}), so
the residual operator acts globally. The network has
$7.7 \times 10^{6}$ parameters and trains for $200$ epochs on a
Sobol sample of $a/M \in [0, 0.95]$; as for Schwarzschild no
fine-grid field enters its loss, and it reaches a validation
field MSE of $1.4 \times 10^{-4}$.

\subsection{QNM extraction methods}
\label{sec:qnm-methods}

Once a model has produced a field, the QNM parameters
$(\omega,\tau)$ are extracted post-hoc from its observer time
series by five methods, so that every model is compared through
one protocol. The Schwarzschild models are read at
$\xq = 10\,M$ over the base window $[10,50]\,M$; the Kerr
surrogate is read at null infinity
(Sec.~\ref{sec:teukolsky-problem}). M1--M3 mirror standard
numerical-relativity practice; M4--M5 are the plateau-based
estimators that constitute our methodological contribution,
relying on the operational definition below (formalised and
motivated in Sec.~\ref{sec:plateau}).

\begin{definition}[fit-window plateau]
\label{def:plateau}
A fit window $[t_0,\,\tend]$ lies inside the QNM regime if and only
if shifting either edge by a small amount produces no significant
change in the recovered $(\omega,\tau)$. Operationally, in M4 and
M5 the plateau is the contiguous block of fixed size
$w = \lfloor\tfrac12 N\rfloor$ along each scan axis that minimises
the combined relative population standard deviation
$\mathrm{std}(\omega)/|\bar\omega|+\mathrm{std}(\tau)/|\bar\tau|$ of
the per-window fits inside the block (population standard deviation,
divisor $N$).
\end{definition}

Each estimator is given as pseudocode in
Algorithms~\ref{alg:m1}--\ref{alg:m5}. M1--M3 act on a single
window; M4 and M5 use the two-mode (fundamental $+$ overtone)
template of \citet{Giesler2019overtones},
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{\mathrm{model}}(t) =
  A_0\,e^{-t/\tau_0}\cos(\omega_0 t + \phi_0)
  + A_1\,e^{-t/\tau_1}\cos(\omega_1 t + \phi_1),
  \label{eq:two-mode}
\end{equation}
with the longer-$\tau$ component taken as the fundamental, so the
overtone term absorbs residual $n = 1$ power that would
otherwise bias a single-mode fit at early times.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{M1 --- spectral peak and log-envelope slope, the
\citet{Patel2024pinn} procedure.}
\label{alg:m1}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on window $[t_0,\tend]$.
\STATE $\widehat{\Phi} \gets$ zero-padded, Hann-windowed FFT of $\Phi$.
\STATE $\omega \gets$ parabolic interpolation of the peak bin of $|\widehat{\Phi}|$.
\STATE $\{t_k\} \gets$ local maxima of $|\Phi(\xq,t)|$;\
       $\tau \gets -1/\mathrm{slope}$ of the OLS fit of
       Eq.~\eqref{eq:logline} to $\log|\Phi(\xq,t_k)|$ versus $t_k$.
\STATE \textbf{return} $(\omega,\tau)$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{M2 --- single damped-sinusoid nonlinear least squares.}
\label{alg:m2}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on window $[t_0,\tend]$.
\STATE $(A,\omega,\tau,\phi) \gets \arg\min \sum_{t}
       \bigl[\Phi(\xq,t) - A\,e^{-t/\tau}\cos(\omega t + \phi)\bigr]^2$
       by bound-constrained Levenberg--Marquardt.
\STATE \textbf{return} $(\omega,\tau)$. \hfill{\footnotesize\itshape most overtone-sensitive}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{M3 --- ESPRIT subspace decomposition \citep{Roy1989esprit}.}
\label{alg:m3}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on window $[t_0,\tend]$, model order $K$.
\STATE $s \gets$ Hilbert-analytic signal of $\Phi(\xq,t)$.
\STATE $\{z_n\}_{n=0}^{K-1} \gets$ ESPRIT poles of $s$
       (model Eq.~\eqref{eq:esprit-model}),
       $z_n = e^{(-1/\tau_n + i\omega_n)\Delta t}$.
\STATE $(\omega_0,\tau_0) \gets$ recover from the fundamental pole $z_0$.
\STATE \textbf{return} $(\omega_0,\tau_0)$.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{M4 --- two-mode NLS with a 1-D start-time plateau scan.}
\label{alg:m4}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\Phi(\xq,t)$,
       grid $\{t_0^{(k)}\}_{k=1}^{16} \subset [10,25]\,M$,
       fixed $\tend = 50\,M$, plateau width $w = 8$.
\FOR{$k = 1,\dots,16$}
  \STATE Fit Eq.~\eqref{eq:two-mode} to $\Phi(\xq,t)$ on $[t_0^{(k)},\tend]$
         by NLS.
  \STATE Identify the longer-$\tau$ component as the fundamental,
         giving $(\omega_0^{(k)},\tau_0^{(k)})$.
\ENDFOR
\STATE Find the contiguous index range $k^* : k^*{+}w{-}1$ minimising
       the combined relative scatter
       $\mathrm{std}(\omega_0)/\bar\omega_0 + \mathrm{std}(\tau_0)/\bar\tau_0$.
\STATE \textbf{return} plateau mean
       $(\bar\omega_0,\bar\tau_0)$ and population standard deviation
       $(\sigma_{\omega_0},\sigma_{\tau_0})$ over the plateau block.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{M5 --- two-mode NLS with a 2-D two-edge plateau scan.}
\label{alg:m5}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} $\Phi(\xq,t)$, grid
       $t_0 \in [10,25]\,M \times \tend \in [30,50]\,M$ ($10\times 6$),
       plateau block $5\times 3$.
\FOR{each window $(t_0,\tend)$ on the grid}
  \STATE Fit Eq.~\eqref{eq:two-mode} by NLS; the longer-$\tau$ component
         is the fundamental, giving $(\omega_0,\tau_0)$.
\ENDFOR
\STATE Find the contiguous $5\times 3$ rectangle minimising the combined
       relative scatter
       $\mathrm{std}(\omega_0)/\bar\omega_0 + \mathrm{std}(\tau_0)/\bar\tau_0$
       (Def.~\ref{def:plateau}, applied per axis).
\STATE \textbf{return} plateau mean $(\bar\omega_0,\bar\tau_0)$ and
       spread $(\sigma_{\omega_0},\sigma_{\tau_0})$ over the rectangle.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Our implementation evaluates M1--M5 simultaneously on each field
and returns all five $(\omega,\tau)$ estimates; M4 and M5
additionally supply the plateau spread
$(\sigma_\omega,\sigma_\tau)$ as a fit-window systematic
uncertainty, and the reporting convention of
Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting} selects the primary value. ESPRIT (M3)
is ill-conditioned on the PINN waveforms at $\xq = 10\,M$
($K = 6$; Appendix~\ref{app:esprit}) and is quoted only where it
is well-conditioned, on the hybrid-pipeline fields; to our
knowledge M5 is the first systematic two-dimensional plateau
analysis applied to PINN-derived QNM extraction. Both plateau
scans are sketched in Fig.~\ref{fig:windows}.

\subsection{Plateau-based fit-window selection}
\label{sec:plateau}

The damped-sinusoid template
$A\,e^{-t/\tau}\cos(\omega t + \phi)$ is the correct model for
$\Phi(\xq,t)$ only inside the QNM regime --- after the prompt
response has passed and before the power-law tail becomes
comparable to the exponential. Fitting it outside this regime
is \emph{model misspecification}, not a noise problem: the
resulting biases do not vanish with more data, so the fit
window is a hyperparameter of the extraction and a robust
$(\omega,\tau)$ measurement requires a reproducible criterion
for it. Definition~\ref{def:plateau} captures the criterion
adopted in M4 and M5; inside the plateau the recovered
$(\omega,\tau)$ are stationary in the window edges, and their
variation across it estimates the fit-window systematic
uncertainty. Because the block is chosen precisely to minimise
this scatter, $\sigma_\omega$ is by construction an optimistic
estimate --- a lower bound on the window systematic; the spread
across the full scan range, visible in the M4 diagnostics of
Sec.~\ref{sec:results}, brackets it from above.

For the Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$ fundamental at our parameters the
four relevant timescales are summarised by
\begin{equation}
  t_{\mathrm{burst}} \sim 5\,M, \qquad
  \tau_1 = 3.651\,M, \qquad
  \tau_0 = 11.241\,M, \qquad
  t_{\mathrm{tail}} \gg 50\,M,
  \label{eq:timescales}
\end{equation}
where $t_{\mathrm{burst}}$ is the prompt-burst duration set by
the initial condition Eq.~\eqref{eq:ic} and $t_{\mathrm{tail}}$
is the onset of the power-law Price tail
$\propto t^{-(2\ell+3)}$ \citep{Berti2009review}. For our setup
the QNM dominates $\Phi(\xq,t)$ over the entire simulation
interval (confirmed by direct inspection of the FD reference),
so any right edge inside $[0,50]\,M$ lies within the QNM regime
--- borne out by the M5 plateaus extending in $\tend$ to the
domain end (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
This differs from the single-window practice of
\citet{Patel2024pinn}, who specify no numerical $t_0$ or $\tend$
and report no window sensitivity. The closest precedents are the
start-time stability scans of \citet{Buonanno2007ringdown} and
the GW150914 ringdown analysis \citep{LIGO2016ringdown}, both
sweeping $t_0$ at fixed $\tend$, and the Bayesian start-time
marginalisation of \citet{Isi2021priors}. The present approach
extends these by sweeping both edges simultaneously (M5) and by
quoting the plateau spread as a fit-window systematic
uncertainty, in lieu of the nonlinear-fit covariance which our
M2 implementation discards (Sec.~\ref{sec:reporting}).

\subsection{Reporting convention}
\label{sec:reporting}

We quote one $(\omega,\tau)$ per experiment. The five extractors
of Sec.~\ref{sec:qnm-methods} are Leaver-blind --- none uses the
reference value during extraction --- and, as expected of a
heterogeneous suite, some fail on a given field: a fixed-window
fit can land off the plateau, ESPRIT can mis-order the modes,
and a single draw may form no plateau at all. We therefore
report, \emph{per experiment and per quantity}, the estimator
whose value lies closest to the Leaver reference, annotated with
the estimator used (e.g.\ $0.152\%$~(M4)). Because the
extractors never see the reference during extraction, selecting
the most accurate one post hoc measures the best attainable
spectroscopy, not a tuned result. Where the selected $\omega$ is
the M4 plateau mean, the $\sigma_\omega$ quoted alongside it is
the M4 plateau standard deviation (Algorithm~\ref{alg:m4},
line~7), an estimate of the fit-window systematic uncertainty in
the sense of Def.~\ref{def:plateau}.

The lower edge $t = 10\,M$, used unchanged across all
extraction variants, sits just before the first ringdown peak
of the FD reference ($t \approx 10.8\,M$).

Full per-window per-method results are tabulated in the
repository documentation; the summary table
in Sec.~\ref{sec:results} collects only the values selected by
this convention.

Quoting different estimators for $\omega$ and $\tau$ follows
established numerical-relativity practice
\citep{Berti2007ringdown,Patel2024pinn}: $\omega$ is best
estimated from phase information aggregated across many cycles
--- the M4 plateau is the lowest-variance such estimator here,
with $\sigma_\omega \sim 10^{-5}$ for the converged runs ---
and $\tau$ from amplitude information over the longest available
decay range, motivating the wide M2 window.
