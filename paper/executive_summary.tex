\section{Motivation \& Scientific Justification}

The ringdown of a perturbed black hole is a superposition of damped
sinusoids --- quasi-normal modes (QNMs) --- whose frequencies and
damping times are fixed entirely by the black hole's mass and spin
[1]. Measuring them from gravitational-wave data and comparing
against the predictions of general relativity is among the cleanest
strong-field tests available, and has been an observational
programme since the ringdown analysis of GW150914 [2]. The precision
of such tests rests on two computational ingredients: the accuracy
of the underlying waveform solution, and the reliability of reading
the QNM parameters off it. The second receives surprisingly little
scrutiny --- the damped-sinusoid template is valid only inside a
finite time window, and the choice of window biases the recovered
parameters in ways most analyses do not quantify.

Machine-learning solvers promise to relieve the first bottleneck.
Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) solve differential
equations by minimising the equation residual directly; the only
prior PINN treatment of the time-domain Schwarzschild problem,
however, reports a $28\%$ relative waveform error that dominates its
QNM error budget, with a fit window fixed by hand [5]. This project
benchmarks what PINNs and neural surrogates can deliver for QNM
extraction --- on the Schwarzschild $\ell = 2$ master equations,
where exact reference values are known from Leaver's
continued-fraction method [3] --- and builds an extraction protocol
that quantifies its own fit-window systematic.

\section{Methodology}

Four models are evaluated under one fixed protocol. (i) A
\emph{reproduction} PINN, architecture-matched to the prior study
[5], establishes a trustworthy baseline. (ii) An \emph{enhanced
forward} PINN doubles the quasi-Newton optimisation budget and adds
residual-greedy sampling, which concentrates training points where
the equation residual is currently largest. (iii) An \emph{inverse}
PINN embeds a ringdown template with five learnable scalars and
recovers the black-hole mass jointly with the waveform from $500$
observations at $1\%$ noise. (iv) A \emph{hybrid surrogate} runs a
coarse finite-difference solve at $1/64$ of the reference cost and
trains a small Fourier neural operator [6] ($1.1$ million
parameters) to predict its discretisation error, following the
solver-correction strand of the machine-learning literature [7, 8];
the training target is built label-free by Richardson extrapolation
of two coarse solves, and a parameter-free gate confines the
correction to the under-resolved wavefronts.

Extraction is deliberately model-agnostic: every model produces the
same observer time series (Figure~1), from which five estimators
recover the frequency and damping time $(\omega, \tau)$. The
methodological contribution is a \emph{plateau scan}
(Figure~2): the fit window is swept systematically (one edge or
both), and the contiguous block of windows over which the recovered
values are stationary supplies the estimate, its window systematic
$\sigma_\omega$, and a validity flag --- if no plateau exists, the
estimate is rejected rather than silently biased.

\section{Key Findings}

The reproduction reduces the prior study's waveform error by an
order of magnitude on both of its benchmark potentials (relative
$L^{2}$ error $1.30\%$ versus $28.06\%$, and $2.67\%$ versus
$23.59\%$). The enhanced forward PINN reaches $0.58\%$, from which
the plateau estimator recovers the fundamental frequency to
$0.029\%$ and the damping time to $2.65\%$; an error budget shows
the frequency measurement is limited by the window systematic, not
by the field solution. The inverse PINN recovers the black-hole
mass to $0.34\%$ under $1\%$ observation noise --- after isolating,
analytically and empirically, an amplitude--damping-time degeneracy
of the embedded template that would otherwise bias the damping time
by ten to twenty percent. The hybrid surrogate cuts the mean-squared
waveform error by a factor of $82$ relative to its coarse input at
$1/64$ of the reference cost, with no fine simulation anywhere in
its training loss; at the common observer it tracks the
expensive reference's frequency to $0.15\%$ across a
$100$-black-hole population, its damping-time error ($0.53\%$)
set by the network's late-ring residual rather than the plateau
estimator.

\section{Recommendation \& Next Steps}

Three follow-ups are scoped. First, the lift from Schwarzschild to
rotating (Kerr) black holes --- the astrophysically relevant case
--- for which each component here (solver, extraction protocol,
hybrid design) was chosen to carry over. Second, wrapping the
deterministic inverse estimate in a Bayesian posterior, so that
data noise and network systematics enter a single uncertainty
budget, with a Cram\'er--Rao bound establishing how close the
$0.34\%$ mass estimate sits to the information-theoretic limit.
Third, learning the inverse map directly --- from observed waveform
to source parameters --- across the hybrid's training population,
with the plateau diagnostic carrying over as a population-level
quality flag.

\section{Research Impact \& Conclusion}

The plateau estimator and its validity flag apply to any
time-domain ringdown analysis, machine-learning-based or not: they
replace a hand-tuned window choice with a reproducible procedure
that reports its own systematic uncertainty, directly relevant as
next-generation detectors push ringdown spectroscopy towards
per-mille precision [4]. The hybrid result carries a broader design
lesson for scientific computing: keeping a cheap classical solver
responsible for the physics and learning only its discretisation
error delivered reference-quality waveforms at a fraction of the
cost. Every result, figure and table in the report regenerates from
the accompanying code repository.
