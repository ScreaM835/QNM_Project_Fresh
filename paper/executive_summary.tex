\section{Motivation \& Scientific Justification}

A perturbed black hole settles by emitting a ringdown signal composed
of damped oscillations, the quasinormal modes (QNMs). For each mode of
a Kerr black hole, general relativity fixes the frequency and damping
time through the remnant mass and spin [1,2]. Measuring these tones in
gravitational-wave data tests whether the remnant is consistent with
the Kerr prediction, an observational programme opened by the
ringdown analysis of GW150914 [3]. Reliable spectroscopy requires an
accurate time-domain waveform and a fit interval in which the QNM
model is valid [4].

Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) approximate differential
equations by minimising their governing-equation, initial-data and
boundary residuals. Patel et al. [5] solve the Regge--Wheeler and
Zerilli equations in the time domain, but report relative $L^2$ field
errors of $23.59\%$ and $28.06\%$, respectively, and select one fit
window manually. We first reproduce and improve this calculation. We
then introduce separately trained Schwarzschild and Kerr hybrid
Fourier neural operators (hFNOs) that correct reduced-resolution
numerical solutions across black-hole and initial-data parameter sets.
At inference, each new configuration only requires one cheap
numerical-prior evolution, interpolation to the output grid and one
FNO forward pass, without per-configuration neural-network retraining.

\section{Methodology}

The reproduction uses Patel et al.'s network architecture, loss
weights and optimiser schedule. The enhanced PINN extends the L-BFGS
optimisation and applies residual-greedy sampling, which places
additional training points where the differential-equation residual
is largest.

The Schwarzschild hFNO combines a reduced-resolution
finite-difference solution with a learned correction. Two coarse
solutions, computed with grid factors $k=2$ and $k=4$, are combined by
Richardson extrapolation to estimate the field that would be obtained
at higher resolution [6]. The difference between this estimate and
the upsampled $k=4$ field defines the correction learned during
training. The fine finite-difference field is reserved for evaluation
and does not enter the training loss.

At inference, the reconstruction is
\begin{equation}
	\Phi^{\mathrm{hyb}}(x,t)
	= \mathcal U\Phi^{\mathrm c}(x,t)
	+ g(x,t)\,\mathcal H_\theta(X)(x,t).
	\label{eq:es-hfno}
\end{equation}
Here $\mathcal U\Phi^{\mathrm c}$ is the numerical prior obtained by
interpolating the $k=4$ field to the output grid,
$\mathcal H_\theta$ is the FNO correction [7--9], and $g$
concentrates that correction where the numerical prior varies most
rapidly. The training-only $k=2$ solve and Richardson construction are
absent at inference. Each new configuration therefore only requires one coarse
$k=4$ numerical evolution, interpolation and one FNO forward pass.
Coarsening space and time by four reduces the finite-difference
point-update count to approximately one-sixteenth of the fine
evolution, dramatically reducing computational costs at inference.

The Kerr hFNO applies the same numerical-prior-plus-correction
principle to a radial Teukolsky evolution of the $(4,2,0)$ mode on a
horizon-penetrating hyperboloidal domain [10,11]. Its correction is
learned from independently evolved lower-resolution fields and is
left ungated to preserve the low-amplitude signal at future null
infinity. The angular separation constant is fixed at the
corresponding Leaver frequency, so the recovered frequency measures
reconstruction of the prescribed radial mode rather than an
independent spectral prediction. QNMs are extracted using several
real and complex estimators over scanned fit windows, with the
complete estimator dependence reported in the main report.

Our reproduction obtains relative $L^2$ field errors of $2.61\%$ for
Zerilli and $2.67\%$ for Regge--Wheeler, compared with the published
$28.06\%$ and $23.59\%$. The enhanced PINN reaches $0.58\%$ relative
$L^2$ field error. From its observer waveform, a two-mode fit-window
scan gives a $0.029\%$ fundamental-frequency error, while a
single-mode nonlinear fit gives a $1.99\%$ damping-time error against
Leaver [12].

Across $100$ independent Schwarzschild configurations, the hFNO
reduces the mean relative $L^2$ error from $13.39\%$ for the numerical
prior to $1.85\%$, and the mean-squared field error by a factor of
$46$. The two-mode fit-window method gives a median fundamental QNM
frequency error of $0.152\%$ against Leaver. Figure~1 shows that the
hFNO substantially reduces the coherent wavefront-error bands of the
numerical prior, while structured wavefront and late-time errors
remain.

Across $128$ independent Kerr configurations spanning
$a/M\in[0,0.95]$, the hFNO achieves a median relative $L^2$ field
error of $0.78\%$ against the $N=801$ reference and a median
frequency error of $0.33\%$ against Leaver. The median damping-time
error against Leaver is $1.52\%$, consistent with the greater
sensitivity of the low-amplitude late-time signal. Complete
per-estimator results show substantial method dependence.

\section{Recommendation \& Next Steps}

The first priority is a late-time-sensitive training objective that
improves the low-amplitude Kerr ringdown. Further tests should cover
additional harmonics and initial data, parameters outside the training
ranges, and changes of numerical domain and resolution. End-to-end
timing should quantify the wall-clock reduction and training
break-even point, including numerical evolution, interpolation and
neural inference. The resulting waveform families can then be
combined with reduced-order likelihood methods to test their practical
value for Bayesian parameter estimation [13].

\section{Research Impact \& Conclusion}

The Schwarzschild and Kerr hFNOs show that a reduced-resolution
numerical evolution can retain the governing propagation, scattering
and boundary treatment while a neural operator corrects its
discretisation error across a parameter family. At inference, each
new configuration requires one cheap numerical evolution,
interpolation and one FNO forward pass, without neural-network
retraining. The resulting field errors of $1.85\%$ and $0.78\%$,
together with frequency errors of $0.152\%$ and $0.33\%$ against
Leaver, demonstrate accurate, lower-cost ringdown modelling across
the tested parameter spaces. The $1.52\%$ Kerr damping-time error
identifies the low-amplitude late-time ringdown as the principal
target for improvement.
