ABSTRACT
--------
Investigates feasibility of computing QNMs of non-rotating 4-D black holes
using PINNs. Uses an "eigenvalue solver" approach (a data-free PINN). PINN
QNMs match the established CFM and 6th-order WKB to the same number of
significant figures (deviations as low as (δω_Re, δω_Im) ≈ (<0.01%, <0.01%)).
However in run-time PINNs fall short of WKB/CFM. CONCLUSION: the method is
NOT currently recommended on overall performance grounds, but is a viable
new angle.
PACS: 04.20.-q, 07.05.Mh, 02.60.Cb.


I. INTRODUCTION
==============================================================================
NN as universal function approximators (Hornik 1989). Recently rediscovered
for SciML (Raissi 2019; Lu et al 2020; Nordström 2021). Advantage: trained
once, generalises within the problem class; natively parallelisable.
PINNs (Raissi 2019; Jin 2020) embed BCs in loss. QNMs of BHs are well-suited
because:
  - low-dimensional parameter space (BH parameters);
  - well-defined BCs ⇒ act as regularisation, stabilise the NN.
QNMs are gravitational-wave fingerprints (LIGO/Virgo: Isi 2019, Ghosh 2021).
Existing methods: WKB, AIM, CFM (Iyer-Will 1987, Leaver 1985, Cho 2012); WKB
becomes harder at higher orders. This paper: test PINNs as a NEW alternative.
Paper structure: §II QNM equations; §III established methods; §IV PINN
methodology; §V results; §VI conclusions.


II. RADIAL PERTURBATION EQUATIONS OF BLACK HOLES
==============================================================================

II.1 Asymptotically flat Schwarzschild
---------------------------------------
Klein-Gordon (eq 2.1):  ∂_μ ∂^μ Φ + m² Φ = (1/√|g|) ∂_μ (√|g| g^{μν} ∂_ν Φ)
                                          + m² Φ = 0.
Schwarzschild metric (eq 2.2):
  ds² = −f dt² + f^{−1} dr² + r²(dθ² + sin²θ dφ²),  f = 1 − 2M/r.
Massless KG ⇒ separation of variables ⇒ in tortoise coord x (Regge-Wheeler):
  d²ψ/dx² + {ω² − V(r)} ψ = 0          (eq 2.4)
  V(r) = (1 − 2M/r)[ℓ(ℓ+1)/r² + 2M/r³] (scalar, eq 2.5)
  f(r) = dr/dx = (1 − 2M/r)^{−1}        (eq 2.6)
Tortoise maps r=2M ↦ x=−∞.
QNM (non-Hermitian, complex ω) asymptotic BCs (eq 2.7):
  ψ(x) → e^{−iωx}, x → −∞;     ψ(x) → e^{+iωx}, x → +∞.
Map to ξ = 1 − 2M/r ∈ [0,1) and absorb BCs ⇒ eq 2.8:
  χ″ = λ_0(ξ) χ′ + s_0(ξ) χ
with (eqs 2.9-2.10):
  λ_0(ξ) = [4Miω(2ξ²−4ξ+1) − (1−3ξ)(1−ξ)] / [ξ(1−ξ)²]
  s_0(ξ) = [16M²ω²(ξ−2) − 8Miω(1−ξ) − ℓ(ℓ+1) + (1−s²)(1−ξ)] / [ξ(1−ξ)²]
EM perturbations (Maxwell, eqs 2.11-2.13) ⇒ same eq 2.4 with
  V(r) = ℓ(ℓ+1) f(r)/r².
Gravitational perturbations: same eq 2.4 with different V(r) (Cardoso-Lemos
2001; Dey-Chakrabarti 2019).

II.2 Schwarzschild (anti-)de Sitter
------------------------------------
Metric (eq 2.14):  ds² = −(1 − r_s/r − Λ r²/3) dt² + (…)^{−1} dr² + r² dΩ².
Same Schrödinger-like radial eq 2.4 but
  V(r) = f(r)[ℓ(ℓ+1)/r² + (1 − s²)(2M/r³ − (4 − s²)Λ/6)]   (eq 2.15)
with f(r) = 1 − 2M/r − Λ r²/3, and s = 0, 1/2, 1, 2 for scalar, Dirac, EM,
grav.

II.3 Near-extremal Schwarzschild & Reissner-Nordström-de Sitter
-----------------------------------------------------------------
RN-dS metric (eq 2.16):
  ds² = −(1 − r_s/r + r_Q²/r² − Λ r²/3) dt² + (…)^{−1} dr² + r² dΩ²,
  r_s = 2M, r_Q² = Q²/(4πε₀).
Near-extremal limit (Cardoso-Lemos 2003): r_b ~ r_c ⇒ inverted Pöschl-Teller
effective potential (eq 2.23):
  V(x) = V₀ / cosh²(κ x),
with V₀ = κ² ℓ(ℓ+1) (scalar, EM) or κ²(ℓ+2)(ℓ−1) (grav), κ = surface gravity.
ANALYTIC QNMs (Ferrari-Mashhoon 1984; Cardoso-Lemos 2003; Molina 2003), eqs
2.24-2.25:
  ψ(x) = [ξ(ξ−1)]^{iω/2κ} · ₂F₁(1+β+iω/κ, −β+iω/κ; 1+iω/κ; ξ)
  ω/κ = √(ℓ(ℓ+1) − 1/4) − i (n + 1/2),  n ∈ ℤ_{≥0}
with ξ^{−1} = 1 + exp(−2κx), β = −1/2 + (1/4 − V₀/κ²)^{1/2}.
Near-extremal RN-dS: same expressions.


III. ESTABLISHED METHODS
==============================================================================
III.1 Ferrari-Mashhoon: invert potential ⇒ bound-state problem (eq 3.1-3.4).
III.2 WKB: Schutz-Will 1985 — semi-analytic; popular for Schwarzschild.
III.3 CFM (Leaver 1985, Nollert 1993): very accurate including high overtones;
       used for Schwarzschild, Kerr, RN.
III.4 AIM: Cho et al 2012 improved iteration — comparable to Leaver CFM.


IV. PHYSICS-INFORMED NEURAL NETWORKS
==============================================================================

IV.1 Deep neural networks
--------------------------
Definition 4.1 (FNN): N₀(x)=x; N_ℓ(x)=σ(W_ℓ N_{ℓ−1}(x) + b_ℓ) for hidden;
N_L(x)=σ(W_L N_{L−1}(x) + b_L) for output. Activations: tanh, sigmoid.
Universal approximators of functions AND their derivatives (Hornik 1990,
Hornik 1991, Pinkus 1999). SGD weight update (eqs 4.1-4.2):
  w_{jk}^ℓ → w_{jk}^ℓ − (η/m) Σ_x ∂C_x/∂w_{jk}^ℓ
  b_j^ℓ    → b_j^ℓ    − (η/m) Σ_x ∂C_x/∂b_j^ℓ.
Adam used (Kingma-Ba 2017).

IV.2 Physics-informed neural networks
--------------------------------------
Two components: (1) NN architecture (FNN); (2) loss embedding the PDE +
BC/IC (mesh-less; AD-driven; advantage over FE/FD that need mesh refinement
near steep gradients).

PDE form (eq 4.3): f(x; ∂u/∂x_i; ∂²u/∂x_i ∂x_j; …; λ) = 0 on Ω.
BC (eq 4.4): B(u, x) = 0 on ∂Ω.
PINNs are unsupervised in forward + eigenvalue settings; supervised (with
dataset T_o) in inverse settings.

IV.2.1 PINN ALGORITHM:
  1. Build NN û(x; θ).
  2. Specify training set T (residual T_f, boundary T_b; for inverse +T_o).
  3. Loss (eq 4.5):
       L(θ; T) = w_f L_f(θ; T_f) + w_b L_b(θ; T_b) + w_r L_r(θ; T_r)
     with (eqs 4.6-4.7):
       L_f = (1/|T_f|) Σ ‖f(x; ∂û, ∂²û, …; λ̂)‖²₂
       L_b = (1/|T_b|) Σ ‖B(û, x)‖²₂
     and inverse extra (eq 4.8): L_r = (1/|T_r|) Σ ‖u(x) − û(x)‖²₂.
  4. (θ*, û*, λ̂*) = argmin L (eq 4.9). Adam used.
Remark 4.2: PINNs learn from their own predictions (loss includes the PDE,
not labelled data); the NN is constrained to the domain Ω.

IV.2.2 The DeepXDE package — TOY PROBLEM
-----------------------------------------
Inverted symmetric Pöschl-Teller V_PT(x) = 1/(2 cosh²(x)) (eq 4.10) plugged
into eq 2.4. Exact QNMs (Cho-Ho 2007, eqs 4.11-4.12):
  ψ_n(x) = (cosh x)^{(i+1)/2} χ_n(sinh x)
  ω_n     = ±1/2 − i (n + 1/2)
Coordinate change y = tanh(x): infinite ↦ (−1, 1). Eq 4.13:
  (1 − y²)² ψ″(y) − 2y(1 − y²) ψ′(y) + [ω² − ½(1 − y²)] ψ(y) = 0.
INVERSE PROBLEM in DeepXDE: ω is a tf.Variable, learnt with θ. Loss (eq 4.14):
  L(θ; T) = w_f L_f + w_b L_b + w_o L_o
with (eqs 4.15-4.16):
  L_f = (1/|T_f|) Σ ‖(1−y²)² ψ̂″ − 2y(1−y²) ψ̂′ + [ω̂² − (1−y²)/2] ψ̂‖²₂
  L_b = ½ Σ ‖ψ̂(y) − ψ_b(y)‖²₂  for y ∈ T_b = {−0.9, +0.9}
  L_o = (1/|T_o|) Σ ‖ψ̂(y) − ψ(y)‖²₂   from 50 equispaced labelled points.
Domain narrowed to (−0.9, 0.9) because ψ blows up at y=±1.
DeepXDE building blocks used: dde.grad.jacobian, dde.grad.hessian,
dde.geometry.Interval(-0.9, 0.9), dde.DirichletBC, tf.Variable for ω,
dde.callback. Architecture: 1 input (y), 2 outputs (Re ψ̂, Im ψ̂), 3 hidden
layers × 20 nodes, tanh activation; trained for 20 000 epochs.
Note: tanh chosen because it is smooth and infinitely differentiable; Swish
(x · sigmoid(βx), trainable β) is a recent alternative (Cheng-Zhang 2021;
Salvatori 2022).

IV.2.3 The eigenvalue solver (Jin et al. 2020)
-----------------------------------------------
Unsupervised PINN. Toy: bound-state Pöschl-Teller eq 4.17:
  −½ ψ″(x) + V(x) ψ(x) = E ψ(x),  V(x) = −λ(λ+1)/2 sech²(x).
Substitute u = tanh(x) ⇒ Legendre form (eq 4.19):
  [(1−u²) ψ′(u)]′ + λ(λ+1) ψ(u) + (2E)/(1−u²) ψ(u) = 0.
Exact: ψ = P_λ^μ(tanh x), E = −μ²/2, μ = 1, …, λ.
Loss (eqs 4.20-4.23):
  L(θ; T) = L_ODE + L_reg
  L_ODE = (1/|T|) Σ_{u∈T} [((1−u²) ψ̂′)′ + λ(λ+1) ψ̂ + 2Ê/(1−u²) ψ̂]²
  L_reg = w_f L_f + w_E L_E + w_d L_drive
  L_f = (1/|T|) Σ 1/ψ̂²       (penalise ψ̂ → 0)
  L_E = (1/|T|) Σ 1/Ê²        (penalise Ê → 0)
  L_drive = (1/|T|) Σ exp(−Ê + c)  (scan eigenvalue space; c bumped during
                                    training)
HARD CONSTRAINT for BCs: ψ̂(u) = (1 − u)(1 + u) · N(u) ⇒ vanishing at u=±1
exactly.
Architecture: 2 inputs, 1 output, 2 hidden × 50 nodes, sine activation
(empirically much faster convergence to eigenstates than sigmoid/ReLU).
Implementation: PyTorch (torch.autograd, torch.linspace, Adam).
Training: 100 points, 100 000 epochs, lr = 8e-3.
Note: L_drive flips between eigenstates randomly ⇒ used for the toy
demonstration only; OMITTED from the QNM computations (those converge to
the most-energetic n=0 mode via L_f alone).


V. RESULTS: QNM COMPUTATIONS WITH THE EIGENVALUE SOLVER
==============================================================================

V.1 Hyperparameter scan — Schwarzschild s=0, ℓ=2, n=0
------------------------------------------------------
Fixed: lr 8e-3, 2 hidden layers, sine activation.
Varied: # training points, # epochs, # nodes/layer.
Loss (eqs 5.1-5.3):
  L = L_ODE + L_f
  L_ODE = (1/|T|) Σ_{ξ∈T} [χ″ − λ_0(ξ) χ′ − s_0(ξ) χ]²
  L_f   = (1/|T|) Σ_{ξ∈T} 1/χ̂².
NN converges on n=0 because that mode has the largest |χ̂| (least-damped,
longest-lived); higher overtones are damped faster (Isi 2019).
Findings:
  • Percentage deviations are FLAT across hyperparameter choices — Re ≈
    0.009 %, Im ≈ −0.042 %, matching Leaver to 4 d.p.
  • Training time grows with #epochs and slightly with #nodes.
  • Recommendation: 100 000 epochs (vs 200 000) suffices.
A small number of "red cells" — failed convergences to n=0 — exist but rare.

V.2 Near-extremal non-rotating BH QNMs
---------------------------------------
Exact (eq 5.4): ω = √(ℓ(ℓ+1) − 1/4) − i (n + 1/2).
Optional seed-loss term (eq 5.5):
  L_seed(θ, ω̂; T) = (1/|T|) Σ [ω̂ − ω_seed]²
to push the NN to the desired ℓ-dependent QNM (seed accurate to 2 d.p.).
TABLE 1 (n=0, ℓ=1,…,3) compares Ferrari-Mashhoon exact value vs eigeNN
with no seed (% dev ~0.001-0.002) and with seed; near-extremal eigenfunctions
(fig 7) match the exact pure-outgoing-wave shape; ω vs epoch (fig 8)
converges quickly regardless of ℓ.

V.3 Asymptotically flat Schwarzschild — full results
-----------------------------------------------------
Fixed FNN: 2 hidden layers × 50 nodes, sine activation, Adam, 90 000 epochs,
lr 8e-3, 100 random training points in ξ ∈ [0,1].
Percentage deviation defined (eq 5.7):
  %dev = ||Re/Im[ω_eigeNN/WKB]| − |Re/Im[ω_Leaver]|| / |Re/Im[ω_Leaver]| × 100.

TABLE 2 — Scalar (s=0), n=0, ℓ ∈ {0,1,2}
  ℓ=0: eigeNN dev (Re, Im) = (0.10 %, −0.01 %); WKB-6 = (−0.03 %, −3.89 %).
  ℓ=1: eigeNN (0.02 %, −0.02 %);                WKB-6 (<0.01 %, 0.06 %).
  ℓ=2: eigeNN (0.01 %, −0.04 %);                WKB-6 (—, −0.04 %).

  ℓ=2: eigeNN (0.01 %,  0.03 %);  WKB-6 (—,  0.05 %).
  ℓ=3: eigeNN (—, −0.02 %);       WKB-6 (—, −0.02 %).

TABLE 4 — EM (s=1), n=0, ℓ ∈ {1,2,3}
  ℓ=1: eigeNN (−0.01 %,  0.01 %); WKB-6 (−0.04 %, 0.15 %).
  ℓ=2: eigeNN (—, —);             WKB-6 (—, 0.01 %).
  ℓ=3: eigeNN (—, 0.01 %);        WKB-6 (—, 0.02 %).

  ℓ=2: eigeNN (0.01 %, −0.05 %); WKB-6 (−0.02 %, −0.12 %).

TIMING: 90 000 epochs ≈ 12 minutes / training session; 6th-order WKB < 1
minute for many QNMs.
WITHOUT seed loss the NN fails to converge for ℓ=0,1,2 in the scalar case
(only succeeds for ℓ ≥ 3). Reissner-Nordström and (anti-)de Sitter cases
fail to converge under their setup (loss too non-convex).


VI. DISCUSSION & CONCLUSION
==============================================================================
* Two PINN flavours implemented (DeepXDE inverse + PyTorch eigenvalue
  solver). Eigenvalue solver used for QNMs.
* Near-extremal (Pöschl-Teller analytic): NN reaches up-to-6-digit accuracy.
* Asymptotically flat Schwarzschild s=0,1/2,1,2: PINN matches CFM and
  6th-order WKB to 4 d.p. (the literature limit).
* Limitation 1 (efficiency): PINNs are minutes per QNM vs seconds for
  WKB/AIM. PINN parallelism could close the gap.
* Limitation 2 (modes): only n=0 fundamental modes, because the L_f
  regulariser steers to the most-energetic eigenstate. Future: scan via
  L_drive (Jin et al. 2020).
* Limitation 3 (instability): non-convex loss ⇒ R-N-dS, full A-dS fail.
  Self-scalable activation functions (Gnanasambandam 2022) are a candidate
  cure.
* Stability/convergence theory of PINNs is still nascent (Shin 2020a/b).
* CONCLUSION (echoing Raissi 2019): PINNs are NOT a replacement for CFM/WKB
  but bring "implementation simplicity to accelerate the rate of testing
  new ideas". Worth pursuing.
