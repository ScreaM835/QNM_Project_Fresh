ABSTRACT
--------
PINNs have not, to date, succeeded on dynamical systems whose solution exhibits
multi-scale, chaotic, or turbulent behaviour. We attribute this failure to the
inability of existing PINN formulations to respect the spatio-temporal CAUSAL
structure inherent in physical evolution. We propose a simple re-formulation
of the PINN loss that explicitly accounts for physical causality during model
training. This single change yields significant accuracy improvements AND a
practical quantitative criterion for assessing convergence. State-of-the-art
results on benchmarks where existing PINN formulations fail: chaotic Lorenz
system, Kuramoto–Sivashinsky in chaotic regime, Navier–Stokes in turbulent
regime. First time PINNs have succeeded on such systems.

KEYWORDS: deep learning · PDEs · computational physics · chaotic systems


1  INTRODUCTION
==============================================================================
PINNs ([1-8]) have severe difficulty / fail on highly nonlinear, multi-scale,
or chaotic problems [9,10]. Series of extensions: adaptive optimizers
[12,13,14,15], adaptive batch sampling [16,17], novel architectures
[12,9,18,19,20], domain decomposition [21,22], new activation functions [23],
sequential learning [16,24,25] — none have cracked broadband / chaotic
problems (e.g. K-S, turbulent N-S).

KEY ARGUMENT: Physical systems possess inherent causal structure. Linear
wave with speed c respects principle of causality (info cannot propagate
faster than c) [26]; this underpins the method of characteristics [27].
Classical numerics enforce causality by SEQUENTIAL time-stepping: t is
fully resolved before t+Δt. Continuous-time PINNs ([28,29,30,21,12,13,23])
LACK this temporal dependence — and as Section 3 shows, gradient descent
implicitly biases continuous-time PINNs to first approximate solutions at
LATER times before resolving ICs, profoundly violating temporal causality.

CONTRIBUTIONS:
• Reveal an implicit bias: continuous-time PINNs can violate causality and
  thus converge to erroneous solutions.
• Propose a simple re-formulation of the PINN loss that explicitly respects
  causality.
• Provide a quantitative practical convergence criterion.
• State-of-the-art results on chaotic Lorenz, K-S in chaotic regime, and
  Navier-Stokes in turbulent regime — first PINN successes on these
  systems.

2  PHYSICS-INFORMED NEURAL NETWORKS (PINNs)
==============================================================================

Problem setup
-------------
PDEs of form (eq 2.1):
    u_t + N[u] = 0,        t ∈ [0,T], x ∈ Ω
with IC u(0,x) = g(x) (eq 2.2) and BC B[u] = 0 on ∂Ω (eq 2.3).
Network u_θ(t,x). Composite loss (eq 2.4):
    L(θ) = λ_ic L_ic(θ) + λ_bc L_bc(θ) + λ_r L_r(θ)
with usual MSE definitions for L_ic (eq 2.5), L_bc (eq 2.6), L_r (eq 2.7).

Illustrative example: 1-D Allen–Cahn (eqs 2.8-2.11)
    u_t - 0.0001 u_xx + 5u^3 - 5u = 0,  t∈[0,1], x∈[-1,1]
    u(x,0) = x^2 cos(πx);  periodic BC u(t,-1) = u(t,1), u_x(t,-1)=u_x(t,1).
PINN setup: tanh fully-connected, 4×128 hidden. Periodic BCs imposed exactly
(Fourier embedding eq 4.8 with m=10). Reduced loss (eq 2.12):
    L = λ_ic L_ic + λ_r L_r
with λ_ic=100, λ_r=1; 100×256 mesh ⇒ N_ic=256, N_r=25600. Adam, 2×10^5
iterations.
Result: relative L^2 error 49.87% — PINN gets stuck in erroneous local
minimum after a few thousand iterations (Fig 1, Fig 2 left).

PINNs CAN VIOLATE PHYSICAL CAUSALITY
------------------------------------
Discretise t into 0=t_1<...<t_N_t=T. Define temporal residual loss (eq 2.13):
    L_r(t,θ) = (1/N_x) Σ_j |∂u_θ/∂t (t,x_j) + N[u_θ](t,x_j)|^2
Then total residual loss (eqs 2.14, 2.15):
    L_r(θ) = (1/N_t) Σ_i L_r(t_i,θ)
Forward-Euler discretisation of ∂u_θ/∂t shows L(t_i,θ) ALSO depends on the
prediction at t_{i-1} (eq 2.16). Therefore a small L_r(t_i,θ) is meaningful
ONLY IF the predictions at PREVIOUS times are accurate. The original
formulation (eq 2.7) tries to minimise all L_r(t_i,θ) simultaneously,
which can drive L_r(t_i,θ) → 0 even when predictions at t_i AND at earlier
times are inaccurate. This violates temporal causality.

Empirical confirmation (Fig 2 middle): the temporal residual is large near
the IC and decays to ~0 after t=0.5 — i.e. the model has minimised the
late-time residual without correctly fitting the early-time solution.

UNDESIRABLE IMPLICIT BIAS (NTK analysis)
----------------------------------------
For each L_r(t,θ), define empirical NTK K_θ(t) ∈ R^{N_x × N_x} (eq 2.17):
    K_θ(t)_{ij} = ⟨∂R_θ/∂θ(t,x_i), ∂R_θ/∂θ(t,x_j)⟩
where R_θ(t,x) = ∂u_θ/∂t(t,x) + N[u_θ](t,x) is the PDE residual (eq 2.18).
By the NTK theory of Wang et al. [13], the eigenvalues of K_θ(t) determine
the convergence rate of L_r(t,θ).

Definition 2.1 — Temporal convergence rate (eq 2.19):
    C(t) = (1/N_t) Σ_k λ_k(t) = Trace(K_θ(t)) / N_t

Visualisation (Fig 2 right) on Allen-Cahn: C(t) is GREATER for greater t,
i.e. the PINN preferentially minimises L_r(t,θ) for LARGER t. ⇒ implicit
bias to fit late times first, violating causality.


3  CAUSAL TRAINING FOR PINNs
==============================================================================

A simple re-formulation
------------------------
Weighted residual loss (eq 3.1):
    L_r(θ) = (1/N_t) Σ_i w_i L_r(t_i,θ)
with weights (eq 3.2):
    w_i = exp(-ε Σ_{k=1}^{i-1} L_r(t_k,θ)),   i = 2,3,...,N_t.
Equivalently (eq 3.3):
    L_r(θ) = (1/N_t) Σ_i exp(-ε Σ_{k=1}^{i-1} L_r(t_k,θ)) · L_r(t_i,θ)
ε is the causality parameter; controls steepness of the weights.
Interpretation: w_i is large only if all previous residuals are already
small ⇒ L_r(t_i,θ) is not minimised until all previous L_r(t_k,θ) (k<i)
have decreased.

ALLEN-CAHN REVISITED: with ε = 100, 3×10^5 Adam iterations, same setup
otherwise:
    Standard MLP   :  rel L^2 error  1.43e-3
    Modified MLP   :  rel L^2 error  1.39e-4
The latter is ~10–100× better than every prior approach (Table 1):
    Original Raissi et al. [11]      ~ 50%
    Adaptive time sampling [16]      ~ 0.02-0.06 (order)
    Self-attention SA-PINN [14]      ~ 2e-3
    Time marching [25]               ~ 6e-4
    Causal training (MLP)            1.43e-3
    Causal training (modified MLP)   1.39e-4   ← NEW SOTA

Stopping criterion for assessing convergence
--------------------------------------------
At init all w_i = 0 except w_1 (≡1) ⇒ only L_r(t_0) is minimised.
Throughout training more weights activate; at end all → 1.
⇒ TERMINATE training when min_i w_i > δ (recommended δ = 0.99).
Both faster training AND better predictive accuracy (training longer past
this criterion can degrade accuracy via over-fitting).

Sensitivity on ε
----------------
- ε too small: latter temporal residuals not effectively minimised.
- ε too large: optimisation problem too hard; early residuals must be
  driven to extreme small values to activate latter weights.
ANNEALING strategy: increasing sequence {ε_i} of values; recommended
{10^{-2}, 10^{-1}, 10^0, 10^1, 10^2}.

Fitting the initial data
-------------------------
Treat L_ic as a special temporal residual at t=0 inside the same weighted
loss.

ALGORITHM 1 — Causal training for PINNs
---------------------------------------
Network u_θ(t,x) with exact BC enforcement. Weighted loss (eq 3.4):
    L(θ) = (1/N_t) Σ_{i=0}^{N_t} w_i L(t_i,θ)
with L(t_0,θ) = λ_ic L_ic(θ); for 1≤i≤N_t, L(t_i,θ) = L_r(t_i,θ) (eq 2.13).
All w_i initialised to 1.
For ε in {ε_1,...,ε_k} do
   For S steps of gradient descent do
      (a) Compute w_i = exp(-ε Σ_{k<i} L(t_k,θ));  use stop_gradient on w_i.
      (b) θ_{n+1} = θ_n - η ∇_θ L(θ_n)             (eq 3.6)
      If min_i w_i > δ then break.
   end for
end for
Recommended hyper-parameters:  λ_ic = 10^3,  δ = 0.99,
{ε_i} = [10^{-2}, 10^{-1}, 10^0, 10^1, 10^2].

REMARKS:
- Periodic BCs imposed exactly (Section 4); other BCs can be incorporated
  similarly via boundary loss inside the weighted scheme.
- w_i is a function of θ; use lax.stop_gradient (JAX) so gradient does NOT
  back-prop through w_i.
- Computational overhead is NEGLIGIBLE — w_i uses values already present
  in the computational graph.
- Collocation points may be re-sampled each iteration; only requirement
  is that {t_i} be NON-DECREASING.
- Algorithm 1 generalises to physics-informed DeepONet [38,39,40] and
  physics-informed neural operators [41].

Connection to existing approaches
---------------------------------
- Adaptive sampling of Wight et al. [16]: weight effect is equivalent to
  changing collocation density. Disadvantages: density designed manually
  per problem; needs many collocation points.
- Time-marching / curriculum learning [16,24,42,43]: shares the spirit of
  respecting causality. Causal training is an ENHANCEMENT to time-marching
  rather than a replacement, since causality violations may still occur
  WITHIN each time window.


4  PRACTICAL CONSIDERATIONS
==============================================================================
High-order accuracy is needed for chaotic problems. The following
extensions further enhance accuracy and efficiency, but are NOT required
for Algorithm 1 to work.

Modified multi-layer perceptrons
--------------------------------
Wang et al. [12] modified MLP. Forward pass (eqs 4.1-4.5):
    U = σ(X W_1 + b_1),  V = σ(X W_2 + b_2)
    H^(1) = σ(X W^(l) + b^(l))
    Z^(l) = σ(H^(l) W^(l) + b^(l)),  l=1,...,L-1
    H^(l+1) = (1 - Z^(l)) ⊙ U + Z^(l) ⊙ V,  l=1,...,L-1
Two encoders U,V merged into each hidden layer via point-wise multiplication.
Better than standard MLP at minimising PDE residuals and capturing sharp
gradients [12,9,38,39].

Exact periodic boundary conditions
-----------------------------------
Dong & Ni [44]. For 1-D periodic in x with period P, embed x into Fourier
features (eq 4.8):
    v(x) = (1, cos ωx, sin ωx, cos 2ωx, sin 2ωx, ..., cos mωx, sin mωx)
with ω = 2π/L; m a non-negative integer. Then u_θ(v(x)) is exactly periodic.
2-D extension: tensor product of 1-D Fourier features (eq 4.11), parameters
(m,n,ω_x,ω_y); guarantees periodicity in both coordinates.
For time-dependent problems, concatenate t with v(x): u_θ([t, v(x)]).

Taylor-mode automatic differentiation for high-order derivatives
-----------------------------------------------------------------
Conventional AD scales exponentially in cost with derivative order. Taylor-
mode AD uses a truncated Taylor polynomial via the Faà di Bruno formula
(eq 4.12). Empirical 3-5× speed-up over reverse-mode AD for K-S benchmark.
Implementation: jax.jet primitive of Bettencourt et al. [47, 35].

Parallel training
-----------------
Synchronous data-parallelism over multiple GPUs via jax.pmap. Each GPU
holds an identical copy of θ; sub-batches sampled independently per GPU;
gradients aggregated by reduce-mean. Larger batches ⇒ better convergence
& accuracy (essential for N-S).


5  RESULTS
==============================================================================
Throughout: modified MLP + tanh + Glorot-normal init. Periodic BCs as
hard constraints via Fourier features. Adam with default settings;
exponential lr decay (rate 0.9 every 5000 iterations). Time-marching:
[0,T] split into sub-domains [0,Δt],[Δt,2Δt],…; each sub-domain trained
with the previous prediction as IC. Code:
github.com/PredictiveIntelligenceLab/CausalPINNs

5.1  Lorenz system (chaotic ODE)
---------------------------------
ODEs (eqs 5.1-5.3):
    dx/dt = σ(y - x);   dy/dt = x(ρ - z) - y;   dz/dt = xy - βz
Parameters σ=3, ρ=28, β=8/3 (chaotic regime).
Domain: t ∈ [0,20], IC = (1,1,1).
Implementation (Appendix E): 40 disjoint time windows of size Δt=0.5.
5-layer fully-connected, 512 neurons/layer. Exact IC via:
    x̂_θ(t) = x_θ(t)·t + x(0)  (similarly for y, z)
N_t = 256 uniform grid in [0,Δt]. Full-batch GD.
Results: rel L^2 errors  1.139e-2 (x), 1.656e-2 (y), 7.038e-3 (z).
Stopping criterion min_i w_i > δ helps both accuracy AND cost: training
beyond it shows over-fitting (Appendix Fig 18-19).

5.2  Kuramoto–Sivashinsky equation
-----------------------------------
PDE (eq 5.4):
    u_t + α u u_x + β u_xx + γ u_xxxx = 0
periodic BC, IC u(0,x)=u_0(x).

Case I (regular): α=5, β=0.5, γ=0.005, x∈[-1,1], u_0(x)=-sin(πx), T=1.
10 time windows (Δt=0.1). 5-layer modified MLP, 256 neurons. N_t=32, N_x=64
randomly sampled per iteration. λ_ic = 10^3.
Result: rel L^2 error 3.49e-4. Error spikes ~ one order in t∈[0.4,0.6]
(rapid solution transition).
Taylor-mode AD: 3-5× speed-up on the loss-evaluation cost vs reverse-mode.

Case II (chaotic): α=100/16, β=100/16^2, γ=100/16^4, x∈[0,2π], T=0.5.
5 time windows. 10-layer modified MLP, 128 neurons. λ_ic = 10^4.
Result: rel L^2 error 2.46e-2. First PINN success on chaotic K-S.
Long-time integration (T=0.9): error grows above 10% after t=0.8 due to
chaotic accumulation. Future work: operator learning [39].

5.3  Navier–Stokes (2-D decaying turbulence in periodic box)
-------------------------------------------------------------
Velocity-vorticity formulation (eqs 5.6-5.8):
    w_t + u·∇w = (1/Re) Δw,    in [0,T] × Ω
    ∇·u = 0,                    in [0,T] × Ω
    w(0,x,y) = w_0(x,y),        in Ω
Domain Ω = [0,2π]^2;  Re = 100;  T = 1.
Network (Appendix G): 6-layer modified MLP, 128 neurons, [t,x,y] → [u_θ,v_θ];
w_θ = ∂_x v_θ - ∂_y u_θ via AD.
Residuals (eqs G.2, G.3); loss with λ_w = 1, λ_c = 10^2, λ_ic = 10^4.
N_t = 64, N_x = 512 collocation per iteration; 10 time windows of Δt = 0.1.
Validation by JAX-CFD [56] spectral solver on 1024^2 mesh, dt = 10^{-4}.

Results: rel L^2 errors over [0,T] × Ω:
    u: 3.90e-2,   v: 2.61e-2,   w: 3.53e-2
First PINN simulation of TURBULENT 2-D decaying turbulence (no training
data). Predicted kinetic-energy spectra agree with reference (Fig 13).

Parallel scaling on 8× NVIDIA A6000:
- Strong scaling: speed-up tapers beyond 4 GPUs (under-utilisation per
  device when batch fixed).
- Weak scaling: parallel efficiency > 99% as #GPUs grows with fixed
  per-GPU batch.
- Larger batch ⇒ higher accuracy (motivates data-parallelism).
Long-time (T=2): rel L^2 above 10% but qualitatively correct.


6  DISCUSSION
==============================================================================
Physical systems possess a causal structure. Continuous-time PINNs trained
by gradient descent VIOLATE this structure: NTK analysis shows an implicit
bias to first minimise PDE residuals at LATER times before fitting the IC.
Causal training reweights the residual loss at each iteration to restore
causality. This single change yields 10-100× improvements over competing
approaches and unlocks problems previously inaccessible to PINNs (chaotic
Lorenz, K-S, turbulent N-S).

Limitations / future:
- This work is forward-only. For inverse problems, observational data
  should be treated as point sources; PDE residuals should first be
  minimised at those locations and then propagated outwards.
- Different problems pose different causal structures (e.g. optimal
  control: forward state + adjoint backward). Training algorithms should
  respect the underlying information-propagation rules.
- PINNs are still much slower than traditional numerics for forward
  problems → distributed/parallel implementations [55, 21] are needed.
- A "Transformer for PDEs"-style niche architecture remains undiscovered;
  modified MLP [12] is currently the best generic backbone.
