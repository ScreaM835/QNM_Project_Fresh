ABSTRACT
--------
Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) have lately received great attention
thanks to their flexibility in tackling a wide range of forward and inverse
problems involving partial differential equations. However, despite their
noticeable empirical success, little is known about how such constrained neural
networks behave during their training via gradient descent. More importantly,
even less is known about why such models sometimes fail to train at all. In
this work, we investigate these questions through the lens of the Neural
Tangent Kernel (NTK); a kernel that captures the behavior of fully-connected
neural networks in the infinite width limit during training via gradient
descent. Specifically, we derive the NTK of PINNs and prove that, under
appropriate conditions, it converges to a deterministic kernel that stays
constant during training in the infinite-width limit. This allows us to
analyze the training dynamics of PINNs through the lens of their limiting NTK
and find a remarkable discrepancy in the convergence rate of the different
loss components contributing to the total training error. To address this
fundamental pathology, we propose a novel gradient descent algorithm that
utilizes the eigenvalues of the NTK to adaptively calibrate the convergence
rate of the total training error. Finally, we perform a series of numerical
experiments to verify the correctness of our theory and the practical
effectiveness of the proposed algorithms.

KEYWORDS: Physics-informed neural networks · Spectral bias · Multi-task
learning · Gradient descent · Scientific machine learning


1  INTRODUCTION
==============================================================================
PINNs have produced remarkable results in fluids [1,2,4,5], bio-engineering
[6,7], meta-materials [8,9,10], free boundary problems [11], Bayesian PINNs
and UQ [12-16], high-D PDEs [17,18], SDEs [19], fractional PDEs [20,21], etc.

KEY PROBLEM: PINNs with fully-connected architectures often FAIL to train
stably, especially for solutions with high frequencies or multi-scale
features [26,13,27]. Wang-Teng-Perdikaris [28] attributed this to multi-scale
gradient interactions causing stiffness in the gradient flow ODE; they
proposed an empirical learning-rate annealing scheme balancing back-prop
gradient magnitudes. But the FUNDAMENTAL REASON for failure remained unclear.

Parallel: SPECTRAL BIAS — fully-connected networks cannot learn
high-frequency functions [29,30,31,32]. NTK theory of Jacot et al. [33,34]
shows infinite-width networks are equivalent to kernel regression and lets
us analyze training convergence via NTK eigenvalue spectrum.

CONTRIBUTIONS:
• Prove fully-connected PINNs converge to GPs at infinite width for
  linear PDEs.
• Derive NTK of PINNs; prove it converges to a deterministic kernel and
  stays constant during gradient descent with infinitesimal learning rate.
• Show overall convergence rate is governed by NTK spectrum at
  initialization.
• Show PINNs suffer from BOTH spectral bias AND a discrepancy in
  convergence rate between loss components.
• Propose novel adaptive training algorithm to resolve this
  pathology.


2  INFINITELY WIDE NEURAL NETWORKS
==============================================================================
Fully-connected NN with L hidden layers under "NTK parameterization":
    f^(0)(x) = x
    g^(h)(x) = σ(W^(h-1) f^(h-1)(x) + b^(h-1))                 (2.2)
    f^(h)(x) = (1/sqrt(d_h)) W^(h) g^(h) + b^(h)                (2.1)
    f(x,θ) = f^(L)(x) = (1/sqrt(d_L)) W^(L) g^(L)(x) + b^(L)    (2.3)
W,b initialized i.i.d. N(0,1), sequential limit d_1, d_2, …, d_L → ∞.

Recursive covariance kernels (Jacot et al.):
    Σ^(0)(x,x') = x^T x' + 1
    Λ^(h)(x,x') = 2×2 covariance matrix from Σ^(h-1)
    Σ^(h)(x,x') = E_{(u,v)~N(0,Λ^(h))}[σ(u)σ(v)] + 1            (2.4)
    Σ̇^(h)(x,x') = E[σ̇(u)σ̇(v)]                                  (2.5)

NTK at training time t (definition):
    Ker_t(x,x') = ⟨∂f(x,θ(t))/∂θ, ∂f(x',θ(t))/∂θ⟩               (2.6)

In the infinite-width limit, Ker_0 → deterministic Θ^(L) (eq 2.7) defined
recursively by:
    Θ^(L)(x,x') = Σ_{h=1..L+1} ( Σ^(h-1)(x,x') ⋅ Π_{h'=h..L+1} Σ̇^(h')(x,x') )
                                                                  (2.8)
Jacot proved Ker_t → Θ^(L) for all 0 ≤ t ≤ T as width → ∞.


3  PINN SETUP & NTK THEORY
==============================================================================
PDE on bounded Ω:
    L[u](x) = f(x), x ∈ Ω                                        (3.1)
    u(x)    = g(x), x ∈ ∂Ω                                       (3.2)
(time treated as additional coord; IC = special Dirichlet BC.)

Approximate by NN u(x,θ); residual r(x,θ) := Lu(x,θ) - f(x).    (3.3)

Loss:
    L(θ) = L_b(θ) + L_r(θ)                                       (3.4)
    L_b(θ) = (1/2) Σ_i |u(x_b_i,θ) - g(x_b_i)|^2                  (3.5)
    L_r(θ) = (1/2) Σ_i |r(x_r_i,θ)|^2                              (3.6)

Continuous-time gradient flow:
    dθ/dt = -∇L(θ)                                                (3.7)

LEMMA 3.1 (NTK ODE): Under (3.7), with u(t) := {u(x_b_i,θ(t))} and
L_u(t) := {Lu(x_r_i,θ(t))}:
    [du/dt; dL_u/dt] = -[K_uu(t)  K_ur(t); K_ru(t)  K_rr(t)]
                       · [u-g; L_u - f]                            (3.8)
where
    (K_uu)_ij(t) = ⟨du(x_b_i,θ(t))/dθ, du(x_b_j,θ(t))/dθ⟩
    (K_ur)_ij(t) = ⟨du(x_b_i,θ(t))/dθ, dL_u(x_r_j,θ(t))/dθ⟩
    (K_rr)_ij(t) = ⟨dL_u(x_r_i,θ(t))/dθ, dL_u(x_r_j,θ(t))/dθ⟩
Holds for ANY differential operator and ANY architecture.
The matrix K(t) is real symmetric PSD; K = J J^T with stacked Jacobian
J = [J_u; J_r].


4  TRAINING DYNAMICS VIA NTK — main theoretical results
==============================================================================
Model problem (1D Poisson):
    u_xx(x) = f(x), x ∈ Ω; u = g on ∂Ω                            (4.1)
1-hidden-layer FCN of width N, NTK parametrization (eq 4.2-4.3):
    u(x,θ)    = (1/sqrt(N)) W^(1) σ(W^(0) x + b^(0)) + b^(1)
    u_xx(x,θ) = (1/sqrt(N)) W^(1) [σ̈(W^(0)x+b^(0)) ⊙ W^(0) ⊙ W^(0)]

THEOREM 4.1 (Joint GP for solution AND PDE residual):
For one-hidden-layer FCN at init, σ smooth with bounded σ̈,
    u(x,θ)    →_D  GP(0, Σ^(1)(x,x'))                              (4.4)
    u_xx(x,θ) →_D  GP(0, Σ_xx^(1)(x,x'))                           (4.5)
where Σ_xx^(1)(x,x') = E_{u,v~N(0,1)}[u^4 σ̈(ux+v) σ̈(ux'+v)]      (4.6)

REMARK 4.2: extends by induction to ANY-order linear PDE operator and
multiple hidden layers ⇒ PINNs ↔ joint GP for linear PDEs.

THEOREM 4.3 (NTK convergence at init):
For 1-hidden-layer PINN, as N → ∞:
    K(0) →_P  K* := [Θ_uu^(1)  Θ_ur^(1); Θ_ru^(1)  Θ_rr^(1)]      (4.7)
deterministic limit (explicit form in Appendix C).

THEOREM 4.4 (NTK STAYS CONSTANT during training):
Under assumptions:
  (i)   parameters uniformly bounded ‖θ(t)‖_∞ ≤ C for t ∈ [0,T];
  (ii)  loss-residue integrals uniformly bounded;
  (iii) σ smooth with |σ^(k)| ≤ C for k = 0,1,2,3,4 (sigmoid, tanh, sin OK).
Then:
    lim_{N→∞} sup_{t∈[0,T]} ‖K(t) - K(0)‖_2 = 0                    (4.8)

INTUITION: parameters move o(1/sqrt(N)) ⇒ ∇u, ∇u_xx barely change ⇒ kernel
stays static. Combined with Theorem 4.3:
    K(t) ≈ K(0) ≈ K*  ∀t                                            (4.9)
which gives the closed-form prediction:
    [u(x_test,θ(t)); u_xx(x_test,θ(t))]
       ≈ K*_test (K*)^{-1} (I - exp(-K* t)) [g(x_b); f(x_r)]       (4.10)

⇒ infinite-width PINN equivalent to kernel regression IF K* invertible
(NOTE: NTK of PINNs is OFTEN DEGENERATE in practice).


5  SPECTRAL BIAS IN PINNs
==============================================================================
From eq 5.2:
    [du/dt; du_xx/dt] ≈ (I - exp(-K(0) t)) · [g; f]                 (5.2)
With spectral decomposition K(0) = Q^T Λ Q:
    Q · ([du/dt; du_xx/dt] - [g; f]) ≈ -exp(-Λ t) Q · [g; f]        (5.3)
⇒ training error in i-th eigen-direction decays at rate exp(-λ_i t).
Eigenvectors of HIGHER eigenvalues correspond to LOWER frequencies
[29,42,32]. Hence PINNs inherit spectral bias.

KEY RESULT (decomposition of Tr K):
    Σ_i λ_i(t) = Tr(K(t)) = Tr(K_uu(t)) + Tr(K_rr(t))
               = Σ_i λ_i^uu(t) + Σ_i λ_i^rr(t)
DEFINITION 5.1: Average convergence rate c = Tr(K)/n (mean eigenvalue).
K_1 DOMINATES K_2 if c_1 ≫ c_2.

EMPIRICAL FINDING (Figure 1, fabricated solution u = sin(aπx), a=1,2,4):
- Eigenvalues of NTK decay rapidly (most ≈ 0).
- Eigenvalue distribution similar across frequencies a (heuristically
  explaining why PINNs learn all frequencies almost simultaneously
  [Lu et al. 43]).
- K_rr DOMINATES K_uu (typical case for high-frequency f).
- ⇒ residual loss converges much faster than BC fitting ⇒ PINN may FAIL
  to enforce BCs.
This is identified as A KEY ROOT CAUSE OF PINN TRAINING FAILURE.


6  PRACTICAL INSIGHTS — Algorithm 1 (NTK-trace adaptive weighting)
==============================================================================
General weighted loss:
    L(θ) = (λ_b/(2N_b)) Σ_i |u(x_b_i,θ) - g(x_b_i)|^2
         + (λ_r/(2N_r)) Σ_i |r(x_r_i,θ)|^2                          (6.2)
Gives modified NTK ODE (eq 6.3):
    [du/dt; dL_u/dt] = -K̃(t) [u-g; L_u-f]
    K̃(t) = [(λ_b/N_b) K_uu  (λ_r/N_r) K_ur;
             (λ_b/N_b) K_ru  (λ_r/N_r) K_rr]
Maximum stable learning rate: η ≤ 2/λ_max(K̃(t)).
Increasing batch size N_b, N_r is equivalent to increasing λ_b, λ_r.

ALGORITHM 1 (NTK ADAPTIVE TRAINING):
  for n = 1,…,S do
    (a) Compute λ_b, λ_r:
          λ_b = ( Σ_i λ_i(t) ) / ( Σ_i λ_i^uu(t) )
              ≈ Tr(K_rr(0)) / Tr(K_uu(0))
          λ_r = ( Σ_i λ_i(t) ) / ( Σ_i λ_i^rr(t) )  ≈ 1
    (b) Update θ via gradient descent on weighted loss (6.2).
  end for
Trace via Tr(K) — no full eigendecomposition needed. Update every K
iterations (e.g. K = 10 or 1000) — extra cost negligible.

KEY DISTINCTION FROM Wang-Teng-Perdikaris [28]: their gradient-statistics
balancer was empirical & lacked theory. Algorithm 1 follows directly from
NTK theory and tackles the convergence-rate discrepancy at its root.


7  NUMERICAL EXPERIMENTS
==============================================================================

7.1 NTK convergence (model problem 1D Poisson, u = sin(πx))
- N_b = N_r = 100 uniformly spaced; full-batch GD; LR = 1e-5; 10k iters.
- Widths 10, 100, 500. Single hidden layer.
- Figure 2 (a),(b): relative ‖θ‖, ‖K‖ change → 0 as width → ∞ (consistent
  with Lemma D.2 and Theorem 4.4).
- Figure 2 (c): eigenvalues of K(0) and K(10000) for width 500 nearly
  identical.
- Figure 3: same conclusions for 3-layer FCN — conjecture extends to deep
  PINNs and nonlinear PDEs.

7.2 Adaptive training (1D Poisson, u = sin(4πx) — high-freq)
- 1 hidden layer, width 100; full-batch GD, LR = 1e-5, 40k iters; tanh
  activation; NTK-parameterized.
- Computed weights: λ_r = 1, λ_b ≈ 100 (from Algorithm 1).
- Figure 4: BASELINE PINN L2 error = 2.40e-1 ; NTK-WEIGHTED L2 = 1.63e-3
  (≈ 2 orders of magnitude improvement).
- Figure 5 sweeps λ_b ∈ [1,500]: minimum near λ_b ≈ 100, matching theory.
- Large λ_b ⇒ larger std-dev (due to indefinite kernel K̃ with imaginary
  eigenvalues ⇒ instability).

7.3 1D WAVE EQUATION (KEY BENCHMARK; same problem reused by McClenny SA-PINN)
    u_tt - 4 u_xx = 0,  x,t ∈ [0,1]                                (7.5)
    u(0,t) = u(1,t) = 0                                             (7.6)
    u(x,0) = sin(πx) + (1/2) sin(4πx)                              (7.7)
    u_t(x,0) = 0                                                    (7.8)
Analytical (d'Alembert):
    u(x,t) = sin(πx)cos(2πt) + (1/2)sin(4πx)cos(8πt)               (7.9)

Loss has THREE components: BC (u), IC on u_t, residual (eq 7.10):
    L = (λ_u/(2N_u)) Σ |u-g|^2
      + (λ_ut/(2N_ut)) Σ |u_t|^2
      + (λ_r/(2N_r)) Σ |L u|^2

Network: 5 hidden layers × 500 neurons; tanh; Glorot init.
Batch sizes N_u = N_ut = N_r = 300; Adam optimizer; 80k iterations.
Initial λ_u = λ_ut = λ_r = 1.

GENERALISED Algorithm 1 update (every 1000 iters):
    λ_u  = [Tr(K_u) + Tr(K_ut) + Tr(K_r)] / Tr(K_u)                  (7.13)
    λ_ut = [Tr(K_u) + Tr(K_ut) + Tr(K_r)] / Tr(K_ut)                 (7.14)
    λ_r  = [Tr(K_u) + Tr(K_ut) + Tr(K_r)] / Tr(K_r)

RESULTS (Figure 6):
    Baseline PINN:   L2 = 4.518e-01  (FAILS — does not capture solution)
    NTK Algorithm 1: L2 = 1.728e-03  (excellent agreement)

DIAGNOSTIC (Figure 7-8):
- Eigenvalues of K_u, K_ut, K_r at multiple training snapshots.
- K_r and K_ut DOMINATE K_u in baseline ⇒ BC component is starved of
  convergence ⇒ baseline collapses.
- After applying Algorithm 1: λ_u, λ_ut grow rapidly then stabilise; λ_r
  stays near 1. The eigenvalue mismatch between λ_p · K_p components is
  resolved.


8  DISCUSSION
==============================================================================
- First rigorous NTK theory for PINNs. Proves NTK convergence for
  1-hidden-layer + linear PDE; conjectured to extend to deep + nonlinear.
- PINN failure root cause: convergence-rate discrepancy among loss
  components AND spectral bias.
- Algorithm 1 dynamically updates loss weights based on NTK trace ratios.
- Open questions: extension to multi-layer/nonlinear; CNNs/ResNets;
  inverse problems (PINNs are empirically better at inverse — can NTK
  theory explain why?); momentum optimizers (Adam); architectures that
  natively avoid spectral bias.
- IMPORTANT CAVEAT: weighting alone CANNOT change the eigenvalue
  distribution shape of NTK ⇒ cannot fully resolve spectral bias. Weighting
  may also produce indefinite K̃ with imaginary eigenvalues ⇒ training
  instability. Better architectures/loss formulations needed.
