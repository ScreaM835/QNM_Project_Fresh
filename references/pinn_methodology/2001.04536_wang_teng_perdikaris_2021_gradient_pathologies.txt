# Understanding and mitigating gradient pathologies in physics-informed neural networks

Sifan Wang (Graduate Group in Applied Mathematics and Computational Science,
UPenn), Yujun Teng (Mechanical Eng & Applied Mechanics, UPenn), Paris
Perdikaris (Mechanical Eng & Applied Mechanics, UPenn).

###### Abstract

The widespread use of neural networks across different scientific domains
often involves constraining them to satisfy certain symmetries, conservation
laws, or other domain knowledge. Such constraints are often imposed as soft
penalties during model training and effectively act as domain-specific
regularizers of the empirical risk loss. Physics-informed neural networks is
an example of this philosophy in which the outputs of deep neural networks
are constrained to approximately satisfy a given set of partial differential
equations. In this work we review recent advances in scientific machine
learning with a specific focus on the effectiveness of physics-informed
neural networks in predicting outcomes of physical systems and discovering
hidden physics from noisy data. We will also identify and analyze a
fundamental mode of failure of such approaches that is related to numerical
stiffness leading to unbalanced back-propagated gradients during model
training. To address this limitation we present a learning rate annealing
algorithm that utilizes gradient statistics during model training to balance
the interplay between different terms in composite loss functions. We also
propose a novel neural network architecture that is more resilient to such
gradient pathologies. Taken together, our developments provide new insights
into the training of constrained neural networks and consistently improve
the predictive accuracy of physics-informed neural networks by a factor of
50-100× across a range of problems in computational physics. All code and
data accompanying this manuscript are publicly available at
https://github.com/PredictiveIntelligenceLab/GradientPathologiesPINNs

###### Keywords

Deep learning · Differential equations · Optimization · Stiff dynamics ·
Computational physics

## 1. Introduction

Thanks to breakthrough results across a diverse range of scientific
disciplines, deep learning is currently influencing the way we process data,
recognize patterns, and build predictive models of complex systems. Many of
these predictive tasks are currently being tackled using over-parameterized,
black-box discriminative models such as deep neural networks, in which
interpretability and robustness is often sacrificed in favor of flexibility
in representation and scalability in computation. Such models have yielded
remarkable results in data-rich domains, yet their effectiveness in the
small data regime still remains questionable, motivating the question of
how can one endow these powerful black-box function approximators with prior
knowledge and appropriate inductive biases.

Attempts to address this question are currently defining two distinct
schools of thought. The first pertains to efforts focused on designing
specialized neural network architectures that implicitly embed any prior
knowledge and inductive biases associated with a given predictive task.
Without a doubt, the most celebrated example in this category is
convolutional neural networks which have revolutionized the field of
computer vision by craftily respecting invariance along the groups of
symmetries and distributed pattern representations found in natural images.
Another example includes covariant neural networks, that are tailored to
conform with the rotation and translation invariance present in many-body
molecular systems. Despite their remarkable effectiveness, such approaches
are currently limited to tasks that are characterized by relatively simple
and well-defined symmetry groups, and often require craftsmanship and
elaborate implementations.

The second school of thought approaches the problem of endowing a neural
net with prior knowledge from a different angle. Instead of designing
specialized architectures that implicitly bake in this knowledge, current
efforts aim to impose such constraints in a soft manner by appropriately
penalizing the loss function of conventional neural network approximations
denoted by f_θ(·) parametrized by a set of weights and biases θ. These
penalty constraints lead to loss functions taking the general form

    L(θ) := (1/N_u) Σ_{i=1}^{N_u} [u_i − f_θ(x_i)]²
            + (1/λ) R[f_θ(x)],                                          (1)

where the empirical risk loss over a given set of input-output pairs
{x_i, u_i} is penalized by some appropriate functional R[f_θ(x)] designed
to constrain the outputs of the neural network to satisfy a set of
specified conditions, as controlled by the regularization parameter λ. A
representative example of this approach is physics-informed neural networks
(PINNs), in which the outputs of a neural network are constrained to
approximately satisfy a system of partial differential equations (PDE) by
using a regularization functional R[f_θ(x)] that typically corresponds to
the residual or the variational energy of the PDE system under the neural
network representation. This framework has enabled the solution of forward
and inverse problems in computational physics by reviving the original
ideas of Psichogios-Ungar (1992) and Lagaris et al. (1998) using modern
software developments in reverse-mode differentiation in order to
automatically compute any derivatives present in the PDE operator. Although
such approaches appear seemingly straightforward and have yielded
remarkable results across a range of problems, the effects of the
regularization mechanism in equation (1) remain poorly understood, and in
several cases can even lead to unstable and erroneous predictions.

In this work, we use physics-informed neural networks as a test-bed for
analyzing the performance of constrained neural networks trained using
regularized loss functions in the form of equation (1). Our specific
contributions can be summarized in the following points:

  • Our analysis reveals a fundamental mode of failure in physics-informed
    neural networks related to stiffness in the gradient flow dynamics.
  • This leads to an unstable imbalance in the magnitude of the back-
    propagated gradients during model training using gradient descent.
  • We propose a simple solution based on an adaptive learning rate
    annealing algorithm that aims to balance the interplay between data-fit
    and regularization.
  • We also propose a novel neural network architecture that has less
    stiffness than the conventional fully-connected neural network.
  • We systematically test the proposed ideas and demonstrate consistent
    improvements in the predictive accuracy of physics-informed neural
    networks by a factor of 50-100× across a range of problems in
    computational physics.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 A primer in physics-informed neural networks

PINNs aim at inferring a continuous latent function u(x,t) that arises as
the solution to a system of nonlinear PDEs of the general form

    u_t + N_x[u] = 0,    x ∈ Ω,  t ∈ [0,T],
    u(x, 0)     = h(x),    x ∈ Ω,                                       (2)
    u(x, t)     = g(x,t),  t ∈ [0,T], x ∈ ∂Ω,

where x and t denote space and time coordinates, N_x[·] is a nonlinear
differential operator, Ω ⊂ ℝ^D, and ∂Ω is its boundary. We approximate
u(x,t) by a deep neural network f_θ(x,t), and define the residual

    r_θ(x,t) := ∂_t f_θ(x,t) + N_x[f_θ(x,t)],                           (3)

where partial derivatives can be computed to machine precision via reverse
mode differentiation. A good set of candidate parameters can be identified
via gradient descent using a composite loss function of the general form

    L(θ) := L_r(θ) + Σ_{i=1}^M λ_i L_i(θ),                              (4)

where L_r(θ) is a residual loss term, and L_i(θ), i = 1,…,M correspond to
data-fit terms (e.g., measurements, IC, BC). For a typical IBVP these
losses take the specific form

    L_r   = (1/N_r) Σ_{i=1}^{N_r} [r(x_r^i, t_r^i)]²,                   (5)
    L_ub  = (1/N_b) Σ_{i=1}^{N_b} [u(x_b^i, t_b^i) − g_b^i]²,           (6)
    L_u0  = (1/N_0) Σ_{i=1}^{N_0} [u(x_0^i, 0)   − h_0^i]²,             (7)

where {x_0^i, h_0^i} is initial data, {(x_b^i, t_b^i), g_b^i} is boundary
data, and {(x_r^i, t_r^i)} a set of collocation points randomly placed
inside Ω.

### 2.2 Gradient pathologies in physics-informed neural networks

Despite a series of promising results, the original PINN formulation often
has difficulties constructing an accurate approximation to u(x,t) for
reasons that yet remain poorly understood. These pathologies can arise even
in the simplest setting corresponding to solving classical linear elliptic
equations. Consider the Helmholtz equation in two space dimensions

    Δu(x,y) + k² u(x,y) = q(x,y),    (x,y) ∈ Ω := (−1,1)²,              (8)
    u(x,y) = h(x,y),                  (x,y) ∈ ∂Ω,                       (9)

with fabricated solution u(x,y) = sin(a₁ π x) sin(a₂ π y) and source

    q(x,y) = −(a₁π)² sin(…) − (a₂π)² sin(…) + k² sin(…),                (10)

with a₁ = 1, a₂ = 4. A PINN approximation is constructed by minimizing

    L(θ) = L_r(θ) + L_ub(θ).                                            (11)

Train a 4-layer dense, 50-neuron, tanh net for 40,000 Adam steps (lr=10⁻³,
exponential decay). Result: poor boundary fit, relative L₂ error 15.7 %
(15.7e-2 ≈ 1.81e-01 in Fig 1 caption).

Tracking gradients of each term separately reveals (Fig 2): the gradients
of L_ub are sharply concentrated near zero in every layer, whereas the
gradients of L_r are large. Without enforced BCs the PDE has infinitely
many solutions, so when ∇_θ L_ub vanishes the network drifts toward an
arbitrary solution that satisfies the equation but ignores the boundary.

### 2.3 Gradient analysis for physics-informed neural networks

To make the mechanism precise, consider the 1-D Poisson problem
Δu(x) = g(x) on [0,1] with Dirichlet BCs and exact solution u(x) = sin(Cx),
g(x) = −C² sin(Cx). Suppose the trained PINN can be written as

    f_θ(x) = u(x) ε_θ(x),    |ε_θ(x) − 1| ≤ ε,    ‖∂^k ε_θ/∂x^k‖_∞ < ε.

Then (proof in Appendix 5.1):

    ‖∇_θ L_ub(θ)‖_∞ ≤ 2 ε · ‖∇_θ ε_θ(x)‖_∞,                            (14)
    ‖∇_θ L_r (θ)‖_∞ ≤ O(C⁴) · ε · ‖∇_θ ε_θ(x)‖_∞.                      (15)

So if C is large, ∇_θ L_r dominates ∇_θ L_ub by a factor of order C⁴.
Numerical experiments for C ∈ {1, 2, 4} (Fig 3) confirm this scaling.

### 2.4 Stiffness in the gradient flow dynamics

Continuous-time training is governed by the gradient flow

    dθ/dt = −∇_θ L_r(θ) − Σ_{i=1}^M ∇_θ L_i(θ).                       (16)

Gradient descent is forward-Euler discretization of (16); stability requires
η < 2/σ_max(∇²_θ L(θ)). Monitoring σ_max(∇²_θ L) during training shows
that as solution complexity (a₂) grows, σ_max grows by orders of magnitude
(Fig 5), severely restricting the admissible learning rate.

A second-order Taylor expansion gives

    L(θ_{n+1}) − L(θ_n) = η ‖∇_θ L(θ_n)‖² · (−1 + ½ η Σ λ_i y_i²).      (30)

If many eigenvalues λ_i of ∇²L are large, the loss can increase even when
the gradient direction is correct. Empirically (Fig 4), eigenvalues of
∇²L_r reach 10⁵ while those of ∇²L_ub stay small; L_r oscillates wildly,
L_ub decreases monotonically. Conclusion: the de facto use of Adam may be a
poor — or even unstable — choice for training PINNs.

### 2.5 Learning-rate annealing for physics-informed neural networks

Re-examine

    L(θ) := L_r(θ) + Σ_{i=1}^M λ_i L_i(θ).                              (37)

The constants λ_i effectively re-scale the per-term learning rate. Setting
them by trial and error is impractical. Drawing motivation from Adam, the
proposed annealing scheme tracks gradient statistics online.

ALGORITHM 1 (Learning-rate annealing for PINNs)
  Initialize λ_i = 1, i = 1, …, M.
  For n = 1, …, S do
    (a) Compute   λ̂_i = max_θ |∇_θ L_r(θ_n)| / mean_θ |∇_θ L_i(θ_n)|.   (40)
    (b) Update    λ_i ← α λ_i + (1 − α) λ̂_i.                          (41)
    (c) Step      θ_{n+1} = θ_n − η ( ∇_θ L_r(θ_n)
                                       + Σ_i λ_i ∇_θ L_i(θ_n) ).       (42)
  end for
  α ∈ [0.5, 0.9] (paper uses α = 0.9). Updates may be performed every
  iteration or every K (e.g. K = 10). Sensitivity to α is low.

Applied to the Helmholtz problem with the same 4×50 dense net, the
annealing scheme reduces the relative L₂ error from 1.81e-01 down to
1.27e-02 (Fig 6) — more than an order of magnitude. The histograms (Fig 7)
of ∇L_r and ∇L_ub no longer peak near zero. Fig 8 shows λ_ub converging to
its steady-state value.

### 2.6 An improved fully-connected neural architecture

Inspired by attention mechanisms, the authors propose a modified
fully-connected architecture with two transformer projections U, V and
residual gating Z. Forward propagation:

    U   = φ(X W₁  + b₁),
    V   = φ(X W₂  + b₂),                                               (43)
    H^(1)   = φ(X W_{z,1} + b_{z,1}),                                  (44)
    Z^(k)   = φ(H^(k) W_{z,k} + b_{z,k}),    k = 1, …, L,              (45)
    H^(k+1) = (1 − Z^(k)) ⊙ U + Z^(k) ⊙ V,    k = 1, …, L,             (46)
    f_θ(X)  = H^(L+1) W + b.                                           (47)

θ = {W₁, b₁, W₂, b₂, (W_{z,l}, b_{z,l})_{l=1..L}, W, b}.               (48)

Each layer has M units; φ : ℝ^M → ℝ^M. The two transformer networks
introduce element-wise multiplicative interactions between input features
and residual connections through the gating tensors Z^(k).

For the Helmholtz benchmark with this architecture + annealing (model M4),
the relative L₂ error drops to 3.69e-03 — almost two orders of magnitude
better than baseline. The leading Hessian eigenvalue σ_max(∇²L) is roughly
3× smaller for the new architecture (Fig 11), suggesting the modified
forward pass alone reduces gradient-flow stiffness.

### 3.3 Lid-driven cavity (steady incompressible Navier–Stokes)

    u·∇u + ∇p − (1/Re) Δu = 0  in Ω,                                   (55)
    ∇·u = 0                    in Ω,                                   (56)
    u = (1, 0)  on Γ₁ (top lid),                                       (57)
    u = (0, 0)  on Γ₀ (other walls),                                   (58)

Ω = (0,1)², Re = 100. Reference solution: 2nd-order finite-difference
stream-function/vorticity solver on 128² grid (Appendix 5.2; ε = 1e-4
steady-state criterion, time step δt = 1e3).

#### 3.4 Velocity-pressure representation [x,y] ↦ [u, v, p]

Residuals (59)–(61):

    r_u = u u_x + v u_y + p_x − (1/Re)(u_xx + u_yy),
    r_v = u v_x + v v_y + p_y − (1/Re)(v_xx + v_yy),
    r_c = u_x + v_y.

Loss L(θ) = L_ru + L_rv + L_rc + L_ub + L_vb (62)–(67). 5-layer × 50-neuron
tanh, 40,000 Adam steps. ALL FOUR MODELS FAIL: relative L₂ errors 7.48–7.84e-01 (Fig 17). Diagnosis: the soft-enforced incompressibility constraint plus the imperfect BC fit lead to non-uniqueness of the latent
pressure.

#### 3.5 Stream-function-pressure representation [x,y] ↦ [ψ, p]

(u,v) = (∂ψ/∂y, −∂ψ/∂x) so ∇·u ≡ 0 by construction. Need only momentum
residuals (third derivatives of the network needed). Loss

    L = L_ru + L_rv + L_ub + L_vb.                                     (70)

Results (Fig 18):
    M1   2.71e-01     M2   2.49e-01     M3   1.21e-01     M4   3.42e-02

Take-away: choice of formulation can be at least as important as choice of
optimizer/architecture; an incompressibility-preserving variable change
(streamfunction) makes the problem solvable for PINNs at Re = 100.

## 4. Summary and Discussion

PINNs often struggle to accurately approximate the solution of PDEs. We
identify and analyze a fundamental mode of failure related to stiff
gradient flow dynamics that lead to unbalanced gradients during model
training via back-propagation. We mitigate this shortcoming by proposing a
learning-rate annealing algorithm that utilizes gradient statistics during
model training to adaptively assign appropriate weights to different terms
in a PINN loss function. The new algorithm aims to address the unbalanced-
gradients pathology and effectively leads to noticeable improvements in
predictive accuracy. This development is not limited to PINNs; it can be
generalized to other tasks involving the interplay of multiple objective
functions that may lead to unbalanced gradients (e.g., multi-task
learning). Finally, we propose a novel neural network architecture that
can enhance the accuracy and robustness of PINNs by decreasing the
stiffness of gradient flow, indicating that specialized architectures can
play a prominent role in the future success of PINN models. Taken together,
our developments consistently improve PINN predictive accuracy by a factor
of 50-100× across a range of problems in computational physics.

Open questions raised: relation between PDE stiffness and gradient-flow
stiffness; possibility of reducing stiffness via domain decomposition,
alternative loss functions, or other architectures; design of more stable
and effective optimizers (implicit, IMEX operator splitting, proximal); how
stiffness affects approximation and generalization error of PINNs.

## Appendix 5.1 (sketch) — Bound for ∇_θ L_r vs ∇_θ L_ub on 1-D Poisson

Writing f_θ(x) = u(x) ε_θ(x) with ‖ε_θ − 1‖_∞ ≤ ε and ‖∂^k ε_θ‖_∞ < ε,
boundary-term gradients are O(ε) while the residual term, after integration
by parts and substitution, picks up four powers of C from differentiating
sin(Cx) four times, giving |∂L_r/∂θ| ≤ O(C⁴) · ε · ‖∂ε_θ/∂θ‖_∞. ∎

## Appendix 5.2 — FD reference solver (lid-driven cavity)

Stream-function-vorticity formulation:

    ∂ω/∂t + (∂ψ/∂y) ω_x − (∂ψ/∂x) ω_y = ν Δω,
    Δψ = −ω,
    u = ∂ψ/∂y,  v = −∂ψ/∂x,  ω = v_x − u_y.

Uniform 128×128 grid, 2nd-order central differences (eq. 84), Wood's
boundary formula for vorticity (eq. 87), explicit Euler in time with δt =
1e-3, convergence criterion 1e-4 on per-step velocity max-difference.
