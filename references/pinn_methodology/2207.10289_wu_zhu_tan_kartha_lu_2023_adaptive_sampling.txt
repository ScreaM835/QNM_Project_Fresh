ABSTRACT
--------
PINNs embed the PDE into the loss using auto-diff and evaluate this loss at a
set of scattered residual points. The location and distribution of these
residual points are highly important to PINN performance, but only a few
simple sampling methods have been used. This paper systematically compares
TWO categories: non-adaptive uniform sampling and adaptive nonuniform
sampling.
- Six uniform methods: equispaced grid, uniformly random, Latin-hypercube
  (LHS), Halton, Hammersley, Sobol.
- Resampling strategy for uniform sampling.
- Two NEW residual-based adaptive methods proposed:
    RAD  (residual-based adaptive distribution)
    RAR-D (residual-based adaptive refinement with distribution)
TOTAL = 10 sampling methods compared on 4 forward + 2 inverse problems
(>6000 simulations). Findings:
- RAD and RAR-D significantly improve accuracy with FEWER residual points.
- Result usable as a practical guideline.

KEYWORDS: PDEs · PINNs · Residual point distribution · Non-adaptive uniform
sampling · Uniform sampling with resampling · Residual-based adaptive
sampling


1  INTRODUCTION
==============================================================================
PINNs (Raissi et al. [1]) embed PDEs in NN loss via auto-diff. Mesh-free,
flexible, supports forward + inverse + observed-data integration. Many
extensions: meta-loss [11], gPINNs [12], adaptive weights [13,14,15],
point-wise weights [16,17,8,18], domain decomposition [19,20,21], time
marching [22-26], hard BC enforcement [27,28,8].

ROOT IDEA OF THIS PAPER: PDE loss is evaluated at residual points T_f. Their
location/distribution matters as much as mesh points in FEM, but most prior
PINN work used only equispaced grids or uniformly random. This paper is a
systematic comparison.

1.1 Related work / contributions:
- Review uniform: Grid, Random, LHS [29,30], Sobol [31], Halton [32],
  Hammersley [33].
- First systematic test of resampling strategy (period N).
- Adaptive sampling history: Lu et al. [3] proposed RAR (here RAR-G, 2019);
  Nabian et al. [37] proposed PDF resampling p ∝ ε (April 2021).
- This paper proposes the unifying RAD and RAR-D with hyperparameters k, c.
- During preparation, [38-44] appeared with related ideas — all are special
  cases of RAD/RAR-D. Compared in Section 2.4.


2  METHODS
==============================================================================

2.1 PINNs in solving forward and inverse PDEs
PDE: f(x; ∂u, ∂²u, …; λ) = 0 on Ω, BCs B(u,x)=0 on ∂Ω; IC = special Dirichlet.
Network u_hat(x;θ). Loss:
    L(θ;T) = w_f L_f(θ;T_f) + w_b L_b(θ;T_b)              (1)
    L_f = (1/|T_f|) Σ_{x∈T_f} |f(x; ∂u_hat,…; λ)|²
    L_b = (1/|T_b|) Σ_{x∈T_b} |B(u_hat,x)|²
For inverse: add data-misfit term L_i = (1/|T_i|)Σ|u_hat - u|² with weight w_i,
train θ and λ jointly. In this paper BCs are imposed exactly via architecture,
so the only loss term is L_f for forward problems.
Inverse weights used here: w_f = w_i = 1 (diff-react), w_f = 1, w_i = 1000 (KdV).

2.2 Uniformly-distributed non-adaptive sampling
2.2.1 FIXED residual points (six methods, Fig. 1):
  1. Grid (equispaced)
  2. Random (PCG-64)
  3. LHS (stratified Monte Carlo)
  4. Halton (low-discrepancy, base-prime reversal)
  5. Hammersley (Halton with first dimension equispaced)
  6. Sobol (base-2 digital low-discrepancy)
2.2.2 Random-R: Random sampling with resampling every N iterations.
  N is a critical hyperparameter ("resampling period").

2.3 Nonuniform adaptive sampling

2.3.1 RAR-G (Residual-based Adaptive Refinement with Greed) — Lu et al. [3]
  Algorithm 1:
    1. Initial T sampled uniformly; train PINN.
    2. repeat:
         Sample dense pool S_0 uniformly.
         Compute residuals on S_0.
         S ← top-m residuals; T ← T ∪ S.
         Train PINN.
       until iter/point budget reached.
  GREEDY — only adds top-m residual points.

2.3.2 RAD (Residual-based Adaptive Distribution) — NEW
  PDF for resampling (eq 2):
       p(x) ∝ ε^k(x) / (E[ε^k(x)] + c)
  with k ≥ 0, c ≥ 0; ε(x) = |f(x; u_hat(x))|.
  Special cases:
    k = 0  ⇒  uniform distribution (Random-R recovered).
    c → ∞ ⇒  uniform distribution (Random-R recovered).
    c = 0, k = 1 ⇒ Nabian et al. [37] (April 2021).
  Algorithm 2:
    1. Initial T uniform; train PINN.
    2. repeat:
         T ← new sample drawn from p(x) (eq 2).
         Train PINN.
       until iter limit.
  Brute-force draw (low-D): dense pool S_0 → compute p(x) → normalise to
  PMF p_tilde(x) → sample subset.
  Higher-dim alternatives: inverse transform, MCMC, GAN.
  Default: k=1, c=1.

2.3.3 RAR-D (Residual-based Adaptive Refinement with Distribution) — NEW
  Hybrid of RAR-G and RAD: REFINEMENT (T grows over time) + sampling from
  PDF (eq 2). Recovers RAR-G as k → ∞.
  Algorithm 3:
    1. Initial T uniform; train PINN.
    2. repeat:
         S ← m points sampled from PDF (eq 2).
         T ← T ∪ S.
         Train PINN.
       until budget.
  Default: k=2, c=0.

2.4 Comparison with related work
Special cases of RAD / RAR-D found in literature:
  Lu et al. [3] (2019, RAR/RAR-G): RAR-D with k → ∞.
  Nabian et al. [37] (April 2021): RAD with k=1, c=0.
  Gao et al. [40] (Dec 2021): RAD with c=0.
  Tang et al. [41] (Dec 2021, DAS): RAD/RAR-D with k=2, c=0.
  Zeng et al. [43] (Apr 2022): subdomain RAR-G.
  Peng et al. [42] (May 2022, RANG): RAR-G with node-generation [47]; 2D only.
  Zapf et al. [38] (May 2022): RAR-G with point removal.
  Hanna et al. [44] (May 2022): RAR-D with p ∝ max{log(ε/ε_0),0}.
  Daw et al. [39] (Jul 2022): point removal + uniform refill.

POINT-WISE WEIGHTING ↔ ADAPTIVE SAMPLING (KEY EQUIVALENCE):
For uniformly-sampled residual points,
  L_f ≈ E_U[ε²(x)]
With point-wise weight w(x):
  L_f ≈ E_U[w(x) ε²(x)]
With RAD distribution p(x):
  L_f ≈ E_p[ε²(x)]
If we choose w(x) ∝ p(x), the two losses are equal.
⇒ SA-PINN [16,17,8,18] is mathematically equivalent to a particular RAD.


3  RESULTS — 4 forward + 2 inverse problems, >6000 simulations
==============================================================================

Common setup: tanh activations; multiple runs per case (≥10);
geometric mean ± std reported.

Table 1 (Hyperparameters; depth × width × optimizer):
  3.2 Diffusion          : 4 × 32  / Adam
  3.3 Burgers'           : 4 × 64  / Adam + L-BFGS
  3.4 Allen-Cahn         : 4 × 64  / Adam + L-BFGS
  3.5 Wave (multi-scale) : 6 × 100 / Adam + L-BFGS
  3.6 Diff-React (inv)   : 4 × 20  / Adam
  3.7 KdV (inverse)      : 4 × 100 / Adam

KEY FINDINGS (verbatim spirit):
- RAD has consistently performed BEST on all 6 problems.
- For complicated solutions (Burgers, multi-scale wave), RAD and RAR-D
  beat uniform sampling by 2-3 ORDERS OF MAGNITUDE.
- For smooth problems (diffusion, diff-react), uniform methods (Hammersley,
  Random-R) are already competitive.
- Random-R reliably beats Random (just resampling helps).
- Among fixed-uniform methods, low-discrepancy (Halton/Hammersley/Sobol)
  beat Random/LHS, which beat Grid.

3.2 Diffusion equation: u_t = u_xx + e^{-t}(-sin πx + π² sin πx),
analytic u = sin(πx) e^{-t}.
- Adam 15k iter; N_r in [10,80].
- Solution is smooth; all methods <1% with 30 points.
- Random-R optimal period ≈ 200; RAD/RAR-D ≈ Random-R here.

3.3 Burgers' equation: u_t + u u_x = ν u_xx, ν = 0.01/π. Sharp front near x=0
at t=1.
- 4×64 net; Adam 15k + L-BFGS 15k iter; N_r ∈ [1000,10000].
- Hammersley best uniform (4.51% with 2000 pts). All Random-class >13%.
- Random-R 1.69%; RAD k=1,c=1 0.02%; RAR-D k=2,c=0 0.03%.
- Adaptive sampling beats uniform by 2 orders of magnitude.

3.4 Allen-Cahn: u_t = D u_xx + 5(u - u³), D=0.001. Sharp interfaces.
- 4×64 net.
- Hammersley 0.14% (best uniform).
- RAD 0.08%; RAR-D 0.09%.
- Confirms: optimal (k,c) = (1,1) for RAD and (2,0) for RAR-D STABLE
  across number of residual points.

3.5 WAVE EQUATION (KEY BENCHMARK — same as Wang-NTK §7.3 and McClenny SA-PINN):
    u_tt - 4 u_xx = 0, x∈[0,1], t∈[0,1]
    BC: u(0,t)=u(1,t)=0
    IC: u(x,0)=sin(πx)+½ sin(4πx); u_t(x,0)=0
    Analytic: u = sin(πx)cos(2πt) + ½ sin(4πx)cos(8πt) (multi-scale)
- Net 6×100 (deeper than Burgers). Adam 15k + L-BFGS 1k iter.
- N_r ∈ [1000, 10000].
- ALL UNIFORM METHODS FAIL (>50%). Hammersley converges fastest.
- Random-R behaviour vs N_r: medium N_r (~4000) helps, but small (1000)
  or large (10000) N_r kills the advantage.
- N_r = 2000:
    Random-R    0.72%
    RAR-G       0.81%
    RAR-D k=2,c=0  0.29%
    RAD k=1,c=1   0.09%   ← BEST
- Recommendation: RAD when N_r small (it's more expensive per-step);
  RAR-D when N_r large (cheaper, comparable accuracy).
- Adding new points: best to add 10 per refinement step (across both
  RAR-D and RAR-G).

3.6 Diffusion-reaction (INVERSE):
    λ d²u/dx² - k(x) u = sin(2πx), λ = 0.01.
    True k(x) = 0.1 + exp(-(x-0.5)²/(2·0.15²))
- Infer k(x) from 8 uniform observations of u.
- 4×20 net; Adam 50k iter; w_f=w_i=1.
- N_r = 15 fixed:
    Hammersley/Halton (identical in 1D) best uniform.
    RAD k=1,c=1: 0.17% / 2.76% (u / k); BEST.
    Random-R competitive (≈ same).
    RAR-G/RAR-D actually worse than Random here (smooth solution; greedy
    refinement misled).

3.7 KdV (INVERSE):
    u_t + λ_1 u u_x + λ_2 u_xxx = 0, x∈[-1,1], t∈[0,1].
    True λ_1=1, λ_2=0.0025. IC u(x,0)=cos(πx); periodic BCs.
    Observe u(x,0.2) and u(x,0.8) at 64 uniform points each.
- 4×100 net; Adam 100k iter; w_f=1, w_i=1000.
- N_r = 600 fixed (Table 3):
    Hammersley ~5% (best uniform).
    Random-R 2nd-best (~1%).
    RAD <1% with smallest std → BEST in robustness AND accuracy.
    RAR-D ~3% (cheaper, viable alternative).


4  CONCLUSIONS / PRACTICAL GUIDELINE
==============================================================================
PRACTICAL GUIDELINE for choosing a sampling method:

• DEFAULT: RAD with k=1, c=1. Tune (k,c) to balance large-residual vs
  small-residual locations.

• When budget-limited: RAR-D with k=2, c=0. Cheaper than RAD because the
  number of residual points grows over training (rather than full
  resampling each time).

• When adaptive sampling is unavailable (e.g., hard-constrained PINN with
  Lagrangian, or hardware constraints): use Random-R. Period of resampling
  matters — neither too small nor too large; a few hundred iterations
  works well.

• When residual points must be FIXED: prefer a low-discrepancy sequence
  (Hammersley first choice) over Grid / Random / LHS.

OPEN DIRECTIONS:
- Brute-force PDF sampling unfeasible in high-D ⇒ use GAN or MCMC.
- The RAD PDF p ∝ ε^k/(E[ε^k]+c) may not be optimal — meta-learning a
  better PDF (à la Psaros et al. [11]) is a promising direction.
